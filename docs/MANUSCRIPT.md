# Toward Label-Free Archaeological Prospection: Training-Free Detection and Zero-Shot Interpretation of Pre-European Earthworks from Public LiDAR in the Yazoo Basin and Coastal South Carolina

**Target Journal**: *Archaeological Prospection*

**Status**: Working manuscript. The paper's argument is that a **label-free, zero-shot** approach is a workable and scalable direction for earthwork survey. Such an approach needs no labeled examples from the target landscape. It fits settings where the finite archaeological record cannot supply the training data that supervised detectors require. We evaluate it as a sensitivity (recall) and false-positive screening study, not as a discovery report. No candidates are field-verified here. The workflow is presented as a staged pipeline: exhaustive morphological detection of earthworks of any age, contextual screening that flags likely modern ones, and manual evaluation. Claims are scoped accordingly.

## Abstract

Automated detection of archaeological earthworks faces a structural problem: supervised detectors need labeled training examples, but the archaeological record is finite, and for most feature classes and regions the known examples are too few to train on. We propose a label-free alternative in which nothing learns from the target landscape, combining a deterministic geomorphon detector that requires no training, a rule-based *False Positive Shield*, and a vision-language model applied zero-shot as an interpretation layer. The approach inherits a challenge, as it finds every feature that matches earthwork morphology, including levee spoil, canal cleanout, and farm and industrial earth piles, because age cannot be inferred from the structure of the ground. Filtering is therefore a staged design involving the detection of earthworks of any age, screening them against land-use context, and leaving the formation-hypothesis judgment to the analyst. We test the workflow in two regions, the Yazoo Basin of the Mississippi River Valley and coastal South Carolina.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The problem of the Delta

The Yazoo Basin of northwestern Mississippi, the Delta, holds one of the densest concentrations of pre-European earthen architecture in North America (Grooms et al. 2023; Lipo and Dunnell 2007). The largest centers, such as Winterville and Lake George, remain prominent monuments, though canopy can hide even a 17 m mound from airborne bare-earth survey (Section 3.4). Most mounds are less conspicuous and can consist of conical features under a meter tall that are eroded and plowed, hidden in secondary forest or alone in cropland.

Since the early twentieth century, the Delta has been transformed. Mechanized agriculture has leveled low relief across most of the basin, and flood-control works have reshaped the rest. Dredging produced thousands of kilometers of canals flanked by spoil banks that, once eroded and vegetated, resemble ancient embankments, while levees, roadbeds, and industrial pads add to a signature of modern activity that any high-resolution terrain method must confront (Tarolli and Sofia 2016).

The combination makes the Delta a hard setting for automated detection. A human analyst scanning hillshade tiles faces fatigue and a pull toward large, obvious structures, while an automated approach sensitive enough to catch a 0.3 m rise will flag every spoil bank in its path. That sensitivity is costly, paid in wasted ground-truthing. The Delta is our primary test of this problem, and the shell rings of coastal South Carolina provide a second, contrasting one (Section 1.3), asking whether a solution built without local labels carries to a different feature class and region.

### 1.2 The label-free approach and what this paper adds

Automated detection of archaeological features from airborne LiDAR is an established field (Chase et al. 2010; Vinci et al. 2024). Object-based image analysis has located mounds and shell rings in the Southeastern United States (Davis et al. 2018, 2019) and large earthworks elsewhere (Freeland et al. 2016), and convolutional and region-based neural networks are able to detect barrows, burial mounds, qanats, and similar structures in LiDAR-derived terrain (Verschoof-van der Vaart and Lambers 2019; Caspari and Crespo 2019; Guyot et al. 2021; Soroush et al. 2020; Kadhim et al. 2023). These supervised methods work, yet they need labeled examples from the landscape being surveyed, from 18 shell rings to more than a thousand barrows per feature class in the studies closest to ours (Appendix D), and accuracy drops when a detector moves between regions or sensors (Guyot et al. 2021; Lambers et al. 2019; Trier et al. 2021). A recent review of 45 such studies names that labeling requirement as the field's central obstacle (Zeynali et al. 2025). The archaeological record is finite and unevenly documented. For many feature types and regions the labeled examples do not exist, and producing them is costly, sometimes destructive, and sometimes impossible (Bickler 2021). Methods that require an increase in labels with every new landscape do not scale. In the Yazoo Basin, the known-mound sample is small, of uneven positional quality, and cannot be enlarged at will.

Machine learning offers a response in **foundation models**, trained on large, general datasets and applied to new tasks with little or no task-specific labeling. Applying a model to a task or place it never saw in training is called *zero-shot* (Figure 2; Radford et al. 2021; Bommasani et al. 2021). The approach now reaches remote sensing (Kuckreja et al. 2024; Mai et al. 2024) and the detection of buried archaeological structures (Sech et al. 2023). For archaeology, its value is that the training signal comes from terrain the discipline need not have excavated, leaving the finite record for evaluation and interpretation.

The detection side of this idea predates the machine-learning vocabulary for it. Geomorphons are a fixed geometric classification (Jasiewicz and Stepinski 2013), and the object-based image analysis and template matching that we applied to these feature classes (Davis et al. 2018, 2019) are label-free in the same sense. This paper adds four things to that lineage: an automated, auditable context shield that turns the screening Davis et al. (2018) did by hand into a reproducible KEEP, FLAG, or REJECT verdict, a tolerance-recall characterization against a regional catalog in true meters, a controlled test of what a fine-tuned vision-language model adds (Section 3.6), and a transfer test of whether one representation crosses region and feature type (Section 3.7). We use *training-free* for the deterministic detection and *zero-shot* for the model's deployment.

**This paper shows that the label-free approach is workable for earthwork survey.** The workflow uses no labeled examples from the target landscape. Its geomorphon detector is deterministic, and its vision-language layer is fine-tuned on New York State terrain and applied to the Delta without local labels. A pipeline built this way recovers most of the known Delta mounds in the catalog (Section 3.1), and the deterministic detector performs the recovery. The same untouched workflow is then tested against the South Carolina shell rings (Section 3.7). We do not claim Delta survey is solved, only that a zero-shot approach is worth developing and among the few that scale to a finite record. The workflow reasons about shape rather than height, which suits a near-level plain, and because shape alone cannot separate mounds from modern earthworks (Davis et al. 2018, 2019), it automates the context screening Davis et al. (2018) recommended and adds the model as an interpretation layer for shield survivors.

We are careful about how far the demonstration reaches. Detector sensitivity, shield behavior, and the interpretation layer's contribution are each measured, whereas end-to-end precision on new candidates is not, as it requires field verification. Wherever a claim rests on a single illustrative run rather than a controlled experiment, we say so. We claim a demonstrated approach with stated limits, not a finished instrument.

### 1.3 Study areas

The Yazoo Basin is an alluvial plain of about 11,000 km² between the Mississippi River and the eastern bluffs (Figure 1). Slopes run under 1% (about 0.6 degrees), and the surface records the migration of the Mississippi and Yazoo rivers as abandoned channels, relict natural levees, and backswamp depressions. The archaeological record ranges from multi-mound platform centers such as Winterville and Lake George (Holly Bluff) to small, isolated conical mounds. The survey problem here is not the height of features. It is separating deliberate human construction from modern disturbance on a near-level plain.

Our second region is the tidal coast of Beaufort County, South Carolina, where Late Archaic shell rings, raised rims of shell and midden that enclose lower, flat plazas, offer a feature class and setting unlike the Delta's mounds (Russo 2006; Davis et al. 2018, 2019). Ten cataloged rings there let us ask whether a workflow built with no labels from either landscape carries across regions and feature types (Section 3.7).

## 2. Methodology

Our pipeline has four stages of data ingestion, geomorphon classification, the False Positive Shield, and AI reasoning (Figure 3). Each stage narrows the candidate set, and the most expensive operation, a call to the vision-language model, runs only on candidates that have survived the cheaper shape and context filters.

![**Figure 3. The screening workflow as a three-stage pipeline.** Stage 1 is morphological and exhaustive: the public 3DEP bare earth is reduced to geomorphon landform types, and structural combinations of those types extract compact earthwork candidates of any age, because age is not present in shape. Stage 2 is contextual and conservative: the False Positive Shield reads land cover, footprint linearity, and mapped modern features and returns KEEP, FLAG, or REJECT, flagging rather than silently deleting. The fine-tuned Qwen3-VL-30B-A3B-Thinking model adds an optional reading of earthwork form (Section 3.6). Stage 3 is manual evaluation of the concentrated, ranked candidate list. The detector evaluated in Section 3 uses a single 5 m scale with a 0.3 m elevation-difference tolerance (Section 2.2).](figures/fig4_workflow.png)

### 2.1 Data ingestion

Bare-earth DEMs are fetched from the USGS 3D Elevation Program (3DEP) ImageServer at 1 m resolution. The regional-scan pipeline processes them in Web Mercator (EPSG:3857), which, at this latitude, inflates ground distances by about 19% and areas by about 42%, so the recall evaluation in Section 3.1 is computed in UTM Zone 15N, and case-study distances are converted to true ground meters. Areas of interest are tiled into 500 m squares with 50 m of overlap. Delta coverage derives largely from the 2009 to 2010 USACE Delta Phase 1 acquisition, and 3DEP delivers a consistent bare-earth product without per-tile point-cloud handling.

### 2.2 Geomorphon classification

Archaeologists have developed many visualizations to extract subtle features from LiDAR bare-earth surfaces (Devereux et al. 2008; Hesse 2010; Doneus 2013; Kokalj and Somrak 2019), yet a single hillshade shows only what its sun angle reveals. We instead reduce the DEM to **geomorphons** (Jasiewicz and Stepinski 2013), a landform classification already applied to burial-mound detection (Sărășan et al. 2020).

For each cell, the method casts lines of sight in eight directions and asks whether the terrain along each rises, falls, or stays within a flatness tolerance, and the resulting code maps the cell to one of ten landform classes: Flat, Peak, Ridge, Shoulder, Spur, Slope, Hollow, Footslope, Valley, and Pit (Figure 4). A cell that looks down in every direction is a Peak, a linear high a Ridge, and a linear low a Valley. In the original formulation, the flatness tolerance is an *angle* (Jasiewicz and Stepinski use 1.0°), so classification depends on local form rather than absolute height.

Our implementation differs from that canonical form in two respects. Every detection in Section 3 comes from a **single-scale** classifier with a five-cell search radius (5 m on the 1 m DEM) whose flatness tolerance is an **elevation difference of 0.3 m** rather than an angle. A multi-scale, persistence-scored form exists in the same engine but generates only training data for the vision-language model (Appendix B.5). The 0.3 m tolerance sets a floor on the relief the detector registers, a limitation for sub-0.5 m mounds that we return to in Section 5.

![**Figure 4. Geomorphons.** (A) For each cell, eight lines of sight are classified as rising (+), falling (−), or level (0) using a flatness threshold (1.0 degree in the canonical angular method illustrated here, while the evaluated detector substitutes a 0.3 m elevation-difference tolerance, Section 2.2). (B) The eight ternary values form a code, and a cell that looks down in all directions resolves to PEAK. (C) Six diagnostic landforms and their archaeological analogs. Classification depends on local morphology, not absolute elevation, which is why the method finds subtle mounds on a flat plain.](figures/fig_geomorphon_concepts.png)

In this framing a conical burial mound resolves as a compact Peak, a platform mound as a flat-topped Peak, a levee as a linear Ridge, and a ditch or canal as a linear Valley. The classifier is generic, and the archaeological interpretation is imposed afterward.

### 2.3 Candidate extraction

The deterministic detector converts a geomorphon raster into a short list of candidate footprints. It selects the landform classes appropriate to the query (for a mound query, Peak, Ridge, Shoulder, and Spur), labels connected components of the resulting mask, and keeps components whose area falls in a class-specific window (for example, 20 to 5,000 m² for a generic mound, 100 to 10,000 m² for a platform mound). For each surviving component it records the centroid, the footprint area, the local height relative to surrounding terrain, and the bounding-box aspect ratio, which is carried forward as the linearity input to the shield. The detector returns the ten strongest candidates per tile.

### 2.4 The False Positive Shield

The shield turns sensitivity into usable specificity. Its premise is not new, since Davis et al. (2018) identified land-use context and historical map comparison as ways to suppress the construction-derived false positives that defeat morphology-only detection. Our contribution is an *automated* form of that guidance, which queries the context sources by program, combines them into an auditable KEEP, FLAG, or REJECT verdict that surfaces borderline and untestable cases, and protects compact "agricultural island" mounds in cultivated fields. Three layers combine into one verdict per candidate (Figure 5), a single decisive layer is enough to reject, and rejected candidates are dropped before any model call.

1. **Morphological linearity.** Pre-European mounds are compact, while levees, road beds, and spoil banks are long and thin. Footprints above an aspect-ratio threshold are dropped as linear, and moderate elongation is flagged. The test is disabled for enclosure queries, which target elongated features.

2. **NLCD land cover.** The National Land Cover Database (NLCD), a 30 m raster whose classes code development intensity, water, agriculture, and vegetation, is queried at the candidate point from a public service. Candidates on developed land or open water are rejected as modern. Compact features in Cultivated Crops or Pasture/Hay are kept as possible **agricultural islands**, because mounds often survive within mechanized fields, and moderate elongation there is flagged as a probable plow line or ditch.

3. **Modern feature proximity.** Canals, ditches, levees, and roads are read from USGS topographic quadrangles, including the Historical Topographic Map Collection, whose early-twentieth-century features (such as logging roads now under canopy) are absent from modern maps. A candidate within the rejection radius is dropped as probable spoil or road shoulder, and one within the flag radius is marked.

The thresholds, class codes, and radii for all three layers are stated in Appendix B.5.

![**Figure 5. The False Positive Shield.** Three context layers screen every candidate and combine into a single verdict. A linear footprint, developed land or open water, or a mapped modern feature within the rejection radius is each sufficient to reject. Borderline cases are flagged and retained with a reduced score. When a layer cannot be evaluated, the candidate is recorded as having incomplete context rather than being passed without record.](figures/fig_fp_shield.png)

One implementation rule matters for honest reporting. A layer that cannot be evaluated (a missing noise map, an unreachable service) does not pass the candidate as clean. The shield records the layer as unavailable and the context as incomplete, distinguishing a candidate that passed a test from one never tested (Appendix B.5).

### 2.5 AI reasoning

The final stage passes shield survivors to a fine-tuned vision-language model for a natural-language archaeological reading. We state at the outset what Section 3 bears out. **No reported detection number depends on the model.** The regional scans stop at the shield, and the model's direct test is Section 3.6.

#### 2.5.1 The multi-view ensemble

Each surviving candidate is rendered as a six-panel composite image (Appendix A, Figure A1), the evidence a human analyst would assemble by hand. Four panels are hillshades lit from azimuths of 45, 135, 225, and 315 degrees so that no feature is lost in the shadow of a single sun angle (Bennett et al. 2012), the fifth is the geomorphon raster, and the sixth overlays contours on a hillshade. The composite goes to the model with the detector's candidate list.

The prompt also carries a text summary of the shield's nearby-noise output for each candidate (for example, `Nearby Noise: irrigation ditch (15 m away)`), a step we call **Context-Aware Visual Correlation**, and the model is asked to judge from the image whether the feature matches that modern context or shows the erosion and symmetry of an ancient earthwork.

#### 2.5.2 Model and serving

The reasoning model we employ in this study is **Qwen3-VL-30B-A3B-Thinking** (Yang et al. 2025), a mixture-of-experts vision-language model with about 30.5 billion total parameters but only about 3.3 billion active per token, fine-tuned with QLoRA. This design gives the reasoning capacity of a 30-billion-parameter model at the inference cost of a 3-billion-parameter one, so the model runs on a single workstation. Our deployed checkpoint is the best-balanced point of the fine-tuning history (Appendix A, released weight tag `terrallm-v91`) and scores 85.3% on an internal benchmark of thirteen terrain-reasoning tasks. Served behind an OpenAI-compatible API, an interpretation takes tens of seconds per candidate, compared with about two seconds for the deterministic detector, so the model is reserved for shield survivors (Appendix B.5).

For a subset of candidates, we also ran a manual cross-check with a second model (Gemini 3 Pro), an analyst quality-control step outside the pipeline.

#### 2.5.3 How the model was built: training data, fine-tuning, and zero-shot transfer

The interpretation layer adapts a general vision-language model to terrain reasoning rather than training a detector from scratch. Our model training used New York State LiDAR to produce 40,000 geomorphon-and-hillshade images paired with questions, generated by a program released with the code and adapted with QLoRA (Dettmers et al. 2023; Hu et al. 2021) on a single workstation-class accelerator (hardware in Appendix B.5). No Yazoo Basin or other Delta data enters training, so the application is **zero-shot**. The model learns the shape vocabulary of earthworks rather than region-specific templates and can be pointed at a new river valley without local labeling. Appendix A provides the build details.


## 3. Results and validation

### 3.1 Recall against the reference mound set

We assembled a reference set ("gold list") of 35 mounds in the Yazoo Basin, identified by their **Lower Mississippi Survey grid-quadrat designations** (the survey-grid system of Phillips, Ford, and Griffin 1951 and Phillips 1970). The coordinates are legacy positions from prior surveys with unevenly documented positional accuracy. For each mound, the procedure downloads a DEM, computes geomorphons, runs the single-scale detector (Section 2.2), and records whether any candidate falls within 30 m of the reference coordinate.

Agreement with legacy coordinates is not recall, so we hand-corrected the reference set before scoring. One of us (CPL) marked each feature's center on registered hillshade and relief layers, flagged features not visible in the bare-earth surface, and recorded the shift from the catalog point (Appendix B.1). The median catalog error is 2.9 m, though the tail reaches 199 m. One mound (22-N-11_m3) has no visible expression and is excluded, leaving 34.

Against the hand-corrected centers, with every distance computed in UTM Zone 15N as a true ground measure, the detector recovers **28 of 34 mounds (82%) at a 30 m tolerance**, at a median offset of 6.1 m (Table 1). Recall falls to 44% at 10 m, partly because the detector reports the centroid of its geomorphon peak mask, which on a large mound need not coincide with the geometric center. The correction also exposed two apparent hits that matched coordinates 199 m and 107 m from the actual mounds. Agreement with a bad coordinate can look like recall. Because a centered-window metric can also be inflated by chance, we ran the identical frozen protocol at 135 decoy points 500 m from the corrected centers, and the decoys score 20% at 30 m and 2% at 10 m, so recall exceeds its chance floor at every tolerance (Table 1). Recall stratifies by relief (19 of 22 mounds above 1.5 m recovered, 8 of 11 between 0.5 and 1.5 m, 1 of 1 below), and since the set holds a single sub-half-meter mound and likely over-represents taller survivors, recall on the faintest class is untested. Appendix B.1 provides the correction protocol, the decoy and relief details, the tolerance curve relative to the original catalog, the six misses, and the matching protocol.

One point bounds the interpretation. The recall measures the **geomorphon detector**, the detection stage of the workflow, against a catalog of known mounds. It measures sensitivity, not end-to-end precision. The fraction of *reported* candidates that are real is a separate and harder question, taken up in Section 3.2.

### 3.2 The role of the shield, and what is not yet measured

The reference-set evaluation stresses **recall**. Every entry is a real mound, so it cannot measure how many false positives the shield removes or provide a **precision** number, the fraction of reported candidates that are real across a regional scan. Precision requires a labeled sample with both mounds and confirmed modern features, and that sample is in production. What we can report now is the shield's **rejection rate** on a controlled scan (Section 3.3), and low precision on a candidate stream does not undermine high recall on known mounds. They are different experiments.

As a first step, we built a review instrument that presents each surviving candidate as a multi-view relief panel with a per-candidate verdict, and we ran a desk review of the eighty survivors from the Jaketown scan (Section 3.3). This was a visual screen, not a field determination, and the tile is adversarial, spanning the modern town and its engineered floodplain. Few survivors read as convincing mounds. Most are compact but modern features of agriculture and flood control (field-corner berms, drainage furrows, spoil ridges, a borrow pit, relict natural levees) that clear the linearity test and sit in Cultivated Crops or Woody Wetlands, so neither active shield layer fires. The 71% rejection rate reflects removal of modern fabric, not archaeological purity. Applying a relief-and-compactness gate to the survivors (relief at least 0.5 m, a 100-5,000 m² footprint) leaves **four**. This is neither a precision measurement nor an independent validation (Appendix B.2), yet a two-parameter filter the shield does not contain performs most of the mound-versus-modern separation on this tile, and we recommend adding it as a standard layer tuned to the regime screened. A genuine precision figure still requires a ground-truthed, non-adversarial evaluation (Section 5).

### 3.3 Case study: a controlled Jaketown rescan

A regional scan of the Jaketown complex (Ford, Phillips, and Haag 1955) illustrates the pipeline at tile scale. The detector produced 277 raw candidates, and the shield **rejected 197 (71%)**, with 189 on developed land, 2 on open water, and 6 as linear footprints, leaving eighty KEEP or FLAG survivors (Figure 7). The result fits the geography, since the tile spans the modern town and most geomorphon "peaks" are structures and pads, exactly what the land-cover layer is meant to remove.

![**Figure 7. Two cautions apply. The 71% figure is a **rejection rate**, not precision, because the survivors are not ground-truthed, and the modern-feature layer was inactive because no noise map was supplied, so every candidate carries incomplete context.

### 3.4 Case study: Lake George / Holly Bluff

Lake George (the Holly Bluff site, 22-Yz-557; Williams and Brain 1983) is one of the largest Mississippian centers in the basin, anchored by a platform mound published at about 17 m. It is also where the limits of public bare-earth LiDAR are sharpest.

We scanned the site, a 1.9 × 1.8 km (true meters) area of interest centered on the published Mound A coordinate, with the same detector and shield. The result is a clean negative. **The mound is not present in the 3DEP bare-earth surface.** Across the area of interest, the relief is about 2 m, the tallest feature the detector found rises 0.42 m, and the candidates nearest the mapped Mound A location are sub-decimeter depressions in forest classed as Woody Wetlands. There is no 17 m platform, and nothing that resembles one (Figure 8).

The cause is canopy. Mound A stands under mature bottomland forest, where the seamless 3DEP product returns a near-flat surface. Whether too few pulses reached the ground or the ground classification removed the structure with the vegetation, the raster the pipeline consumes does not contain the mound. A geomorphon classifier cannot recover relief absent from the DEM it is given.

Two points bound the method. A purpose-built ground filter applied to the raw point cloud has recovered partial relief at this same mound, on the order of 6 m, so the failure is specific to the seamless 3DEP product, not to LiDAR in general. Winterville Mound A (22-Ws-500; Brain 1989), about 12 m high, is an open-terrain counterpart resolved in the same product (Figure 9). The system screens the open, agriculturally exposed majority of the basin and is blind to the forested remainder until a canopy-penetrating surface is supplied.

### 3.5 Case study: agricultural-island protection in cropland

Where Lake George shows the method's limit, a third scan shows the shield doing its designed work. We ran the pipeline over a tract of mechanized cropland 1.3 km on a side containing a reference mound (21-M-11), where a compact pre-European rise and the furrows, turn-rows, and spoil banks of modern farming occupy the *same* land-cover class (Cultivated Crops). A land-cover filter alone cannot separate them. Only shape can.

The scan produced 163 candidates. The shield kept 54, flagged 39, and rejected 70, and the split falls along shape. Fifty candidates in Cultivated Crops were kept as compact islands, one of them a 572 m² rise of 1.65 m relief and aspect 1.47, the low, round footprint of a small mound surviving in a plowed field. Candidates with aspect ratios from about 13 to 60 (plow lines, turn-rows, field roads, and the spoil bank flanking the bayou) were rejected as linear, and rises with aspect near 3 were flagged rather than kept or dropped without record (Figure 10). Within one land-cover class the shield returns KEEP for a mound-shape and REJECT for a levee-shape, the behavior Section 2.4 specifies.

One caution bounds the reading. The cataloged coordinate for 21-M-11 and the nearest candidate differ by about 168 m in true meters, consistent with legacy positional error rather than a clean detection, so this scan demonstrates the shield's population-level behavior in cropland, not a point recovery of that mound. The keep/flag/reject split measures how the context layers act on a candidate stream, not how many kept islands are pre-European, a distinction that awaits ground truth.

### 3.6 The interpretation layer as a discriminator

We tested the discrimination question five ways against the one ground-truthed set the region affords: Eskew's (2008) field-verified mounds and modern earthworks, 26 of her sites plus Winterville and Lake George, 28 in all (Appendix B.3). Shown the standard 160 m six-panel image alone, the model keeps most real mounds yet accepts nineteen of twenty-two modern earthworks. The shield does little better. Its land-cover and linearity layers reject two of the twenty-two, because mounds and levee-spoil piles alike sit as compact features in Cultivated Crops, and the OpenStreetMap proximity layer raises this to three, limited by how few Delta field drains that map records. A 600 m context view helped in some three-vote replicates and not others, and the strongest zero-shot configuration (context view, mapped-feature text, a structured rubric, nine votes per site) resolves the instability in one direction. The model keeps six of six mounds, rejects zero of twenty-two moderns, and its vote shares carry no age information (Figure 11a, b). Three of the six mounds are leveled or canopy-hidden and absent from the data, so mound-keeping on this set rewards over-acceptance. Prompting does not change this behavior. Because the model was built in part to help explain away false positives (Section 2.5.1), this is a designed capability failing a direct test. The staged division of labor of Section 4 is the design response rather than the design vindicated. A first, morphological stage is asked only for sensitivity, which this behavior supplies, because every feature in the negative set is a real earthwork and age is a dimension of evidence that morphology does not carry.

The fifth test asks whether the discriminator can be learned instead, without breaking the zero-shot design, from 1,400 drills built from New York terrain alone: 700 negatives harvested beside OpenStreetMap-mapped construction and 700 synthetic eroded mounds injected away from mapped features. Fine-tuning on these drills raises rejection of modern earthworks from 1 of 22 to 8 of 21 under an identical protocol, a contrast that reproduces across replicates and survives a paired site-level test (exact McNemar, p = 0.011, detailed in Appendix B.3). A factorial of control fine-tunes decomposes the gain (Table 2). Trained on the synthetic positives alone, the recipe nearly reproduces the full behavior, whereas on the negatives alone it rejects everything, including mounds. Most of the gain is threshold drift anchored by synthetic positives, and the negative class alone teaches refusal. The full recipe also rejects I.C. Village, one of only three mounds with measurable relief in the data, so any learned verdict enters the pipeline as a flag, never a deletion. Occlusion and context-transplant tests show what was trained (Table 2). Rejections collapse when the 600 m context view is masked or replaced with a quiet donor context, spread to real mounds under a construction-heavy context, and persist when the candidate's own detail view is masked. The filter reads modern construction in the wide image, not age from form. The drill corpus tied label to context by construction, so whether age-from-form is trainable at this resolution remains untested rather than refuted, and the learned rejection does not transfer (nine of ten South Carolina rings kept, zero of ten decoys rejected). Neither prompting nor this corpus extracts age from a candidate's form, and no reported detection depends on the model.

### 3.7 Generalization to a different feature and region: coastal shell rings

The universal-morphology claim makes a testable prediction. A workflow that recognizes earthworks by structural combinations of geomorphon primitives rather than by a memorized landscape should transfer to a different feature type and region with no retraining. We test that on the ten cataloged shell rings of coastal South Carolina (Section 1.3), a structural combination distinct from a compact mound, in a setting the model never saw.

We take Davis et al.'s (2018, 2019) catalog of ten shell-ring and mound sites in Beaufort County and run the same pipeline on the same public product (full 3DEP coverage, UTM Zone 17N). Rings vary in rim height and are often oval, so a rigid circular template fails, and a per-sector detector instead requires a raised rim spanning opposite sectors around a depressed center (Appendix B.4), a new structural combination rather than a retrained model.

The ten sites lie on the tidal coast of Beaufort County (Figure 1b), and the ring morphology is recoverable in the same public product and representation (Figure 12a), with clear rings appearing as raised annuli of Ridge/Shoulder/Spur geomorphons around lower centers. That transfer belongs to the representation.

An automated ring detector is a different matter, and we report it with its null. The ring coordinates were hand-corrected under the protocol of Section 3.1, and the analyst judged all ten rings visible in the public product. A first hand-tuned structural detector localizes a ring within the rim-crest radius of the corrected center at four of ten sites, and within the ring footprint at seven of ten (Table B3). Footprint-level identification is the outcome a surveyor needs, which is why the chance floor matters. The identical frozen procedure at forty decoy points 500 m off-site scores at the same rates, because the coastal plain is dense with ring-scoring relief, so four of ten within 20 m is what chance predicts (Appendix B.4). Containment metrics on centered windows must be decoy-checked before they are read as recall. What stands is the qualitative transfer (Figure 12a), the per-site accounting (Table B3), and the finding that a working ring *detector*, as opposed to a ring-revealing representation, does not yet exist. The correction pass shows the rings are there, so the failures are the detector's own.

The discrimination step does not transfer for free, which is itself informative. The shield's land-cover layer rejects none of the ring candidates, because real shell rings and the natural rises they must be told apart from alike sit on Wetlands and Forest. Detection generalizes across feature type and region, while discrimination needs a setting-specific signal, the same lesson as Section 3.6. Read against a six-panel image of each ring, the New-York-trained model calls all five clear rings earthworks despite never seeing a shell ring or coastal terrain in training, though at its base acceptance rate (23 of 28 sites of every kind in Section 3.6) five of five has a probability near 0.4 by chance, weak evidence that supports universality rather than discrimination.

## 4. Discussion

The result we most want to foreground is not the recall number itself but what it is achieved *without*. Every quantified detection here is produced without labeled training examples from the Delta. The geomorphon detector is deterministic, and the interpretation layer never saw a Yazoo mound. In a discipline whose training signal is finite, separating where a model learns from where it is applied may be the only approach that scales, since a supervised detector is bounded by the known examples in a region while a zero-shot approach is bounded by the supply of general terrain. The label-free architecture recovers real mounds it was never trained to see. For a field that has pursued automated detection through region-specific supervised training, that finding matters most.

The workflow also automates the first pass of large-scale survey. A regional scan that would take an analyst weeks of hillshade inspection runs in hours, and because the detector reasons about shape rather than a single illumination, it surfaces low-profile features that fixed-angle review misses. Its validated core is the detector's **sensitivity**, about four in five hand-verified mound centers at 30 m (Section 3.1, Figure 6), though sensitivity says nothing about precision (Section 3.2). The case studies bound the method from three sides. Jaketown shows the shield stripping the modern fabric of a town-spanning tile, the cropland scan shows it keeping compact mounds as islands while rejecting plow lines and dredge spoil, and Lake George marks the outer limit, where closed canopy removes even a 17 m mound from the public surface.

The workflow is not a replacement for the archaeologist. It is a staged pipeline whose inference is elimination among formation hypotheses, formation-process theory (Schiffer 1987) applied as multiple working hypotheses. A compact rise on the Delta surface has candidate origins: dredge or bayou-cleanout spoil, road or rail fill, a building pad, a product of farm operations, a natural fluvial remnant, or deliberate pre-European construction, and the set is open on the natural side, since the Gulf Coastal Plain holds mound-like features of debated natural origin (prairie or pimple mounds). Ground structure cannot choose among these origins because several produce the same form, which is why neither the model nor the shield's shape rules can supply age (Section 3.6). Choosing requires the land-use context: is this rise explained by the canal beside it, the road it flanks, the field system it sits in? Where a modern formation hypothesis fits, the feature is set aside as a true positive of a construction event outside the study's interest. Where every modern explanation fails, what remains is not a detection but a hypothesis, that the feature is cultural and not recent.

The stages implement that logic as a design goal, and the gap between goal and measured state matters. A first, morphological stage must be exhaustive, flagging every earthwork-form feature regardless of origin. A second stage tests modern formation hypotheses against context, where the error costs are asymmetric. Discarding a pre-European mound is an irreversible loss while passing a spoil pile costs review time, so the contextual layers flag rather than delete. As measured, this stage eliminates little. The shield rejects two to three of the twenty-two known moderns and the learned filter eight, so most moderns reach the third stage with their modern hypotheses undefeated, and "cultural and not recent" is a hypothesis the analyst must still establish. In the third stage the analyst works through the flagged queue with the evidence the pipeline assembles, plus sources it does not yet consult, such as historical aerials, land-leveling records, and, where warranted, a field visit. At current second-stage strength that queue is large (Appendix C), so second-stage filtering, not detection, is the binding constraint on basin-scale use. A model that reads the structure of the ground reads the wrong evidence dimension for age, and the work program is richer context data, not a better shape reader.

That work program rests on an asymmetry in what can be labeled. The positive class is the archaeological record, which is finite, incapable of enlargement, and with every example used for training lost to evaluation, which is why the workflow trains on none of it. The negative class is the opposite. Modern earthworks exist in unbounded supply and come with labels attached, since a compact rise beside a mapped canal is canal spoil on the testimony of the map, though the label is presumptive, with an audit estimating 4 to 12% noise (Appendix B.3). The fifth arm of Section 3.6 runs on this asymmetry, and the factorial bounds it. Trained alone, the negative class teaches refusal, while the synthetic positives anchor what must be kept. The asymmetry survives as data economics, yet two deployment tests measure what the supervised effort delivers. Ordering the Jaketown survivors by the fine-tune's vote share ranks the desk review's eight plausibles slightly below the seventy-two it rejected (AUC 0.43), and on the cropland tract the fine-tune rejects 26 of the 54 agricultural-island keeps, colliding with the shield rule the pipeline is built around. A filter that fires on visible construction fires on the busy context every Delta field supplies. The learned component's value here is therefore boundary-mapping, a measure of what fine-tuning adds and what it costs. The deployed second stage remains the auditable rule-based shield, and the formation-hypothesis judgment stays with the human.

The reporting discipline of Section 3 is itself a methodological point. An automated survey system accumulates components faster than controlled evaluations of them. A pipeline can report context it never checked, and a case study can be run over the wrong coordinates, which is why the shield surfaces incomplete context rather than passing it, and why every reported number maps to a released script and run record (Table 3). For a method whose output is a coordinate, getting the coordinate right is not a detail. It is the whole point.


## 5. Limitations and future work

Appendix C states each limitation in full. The boundaries that matter most are these.

**Age discrimination is measured and remains unsolved (Section 3.6).** Neither shape nor the shield's active rules separate pre-European from modern earthworks, and prompting does not change the model. The fine-tune raises rejection from 1 of 22 to 8 of 21 at the cost of one of the three present mounds, reads visible construction rather than age, reproduces most of its gain from synthetic positives alone, and does not transfer. Its verdicts have no demonstrated deployment role, since the ranking test fails and the island-collision test measures real cost (Appendix B.3). The path forward is richer context data, not a better shape reader.

**Precision is unmeasured.** We report recall and rejection rates, not field-verified precision. A desk screen suggests precision is low on modern-dominated tiles, the shield lacks the positive-relief gate that would carry much of the remaining separation (Section 3.2), and surviving-candidate densities (roughly 55 per km² in cropland) make second-stage filtering the binding constraint (Appendix C). A labeled, ground-truthed evaluation is in preparation.

**The evaluated detector is a single-scale approximation.** The multi-scale, persistence-scored form is not yet in the detection path, and the 0.3 m floor may suppress the sub-0.5 m mounds we emphasize, a class the reference set cannot test (Appendix B.1, B.5). A local-relief baseline recovers 22 of 34 mounds against the geomorphon detector's 28 of 34, so the representation carries the margin.

**The inputs bound the method.** Closed canopy removes even a 17 m mound from the public bare-earth product (Section 3.4). The reference coordinates carry legacy error exposed by hand-correction (median 2.9 m, maximum 199 m), and two apparent hits were artifacts of that error (Section 3.1). The land-cover and modern-feature layers depend on external services and map extraction whose completeness is not quantified.

**Zero-shot transfer is the design's central wager.** The model's recognition and discrimination behavior is measured on Delta and coastal ground truth (Sections 3.6, 3.7), while its terrain competence rests on a proxy benchmark and its descriptions are unscored. Ground-truthing the Jaketown and cropland survivors and a canopy-penetrating reacquisition at Lake George come next.

## 6. Conclusion

We described and evaluated a reproducible workflow for screening bare-earth LiDAR for earthen mounds in a heavily modified alluvial landscape, and we have tried to report it as we ran it. It has two measured strengths, a geomorphon detector that recovers about 4 in 5 hand-verified mound centers at 30 m, and an auditable context shield that separates compact candidates from the linear and developed fabric of the modern Delta. Age discrimination, tested five ways in Section 3.6, resists both. Neither shape nor the shield's rules separate pre-European from modern earthworks, and fine-tuning recovers only a partial rejection that reads context rather than form, at the cost of one of the three present mounds. Age is not in the structure of the ground. Separating pre-European from modern construction means testing formation hypotheses against land-use context, and the judgment belongs to manual evaluation, with the learned component's measured role confined to boundary-mapping. We present that as the designed division of labor rather than a defect. The morphological stage flags earthworks of any age at high sensitivity, contextual screening tests modern formation explanations where the context data exists, and manual evaluation of the concentrated candidate list decides, reaching "cultural and not recent" as the hypothesis that survives when dredging, road work, buildings, and farm activity fail to explain the feature. The boundaries are plain. Precision on new candidates is not measured, no candidate is field-verified, the evaluated detector is a single-scale variant of the multi-scale method, and closed canopy can remove even a large mound from the public product. Turning archaeological screening into a reproducible pipeline is feasible, and the Delta's pace of landscape change makes it more needed each year. The upcoming agenda includes precision evaluation on a labeled tile, a multi-scale detector, a broader negative corpus, and field verification.

## Data and Code Availability

The complete workflow is released as an open-source Python package, **EarthworkLLM**, at <https://github.com/clipo/earthwork-llm> (Apache 2.0). It contains the geomorphon detector, the False Positive Shield, the regional scanner, and the validation scripts. The detector, the shield, and the regional case-study scans (Sections 3.3–3.5) are retrieved from the public USGS 3DEP ImageServer without an API key and no local data store. The recall evaluation (Section 3.1) and the interpretation-layer ablation (Section 3.6) need restricted data described below. The repository's reproduction guide maps each result to the script that generates it and records the coordinate system used for each (Section 2.1).

In keeping with archaeological data-sensitivity norms, the repository contains coordinates only for already-published public monuments (Winterville, Lake George / Holly Bluff, and Jaketown, each with a citation). The 35-mound reference set used for the recall evaluation (Section 3.1) draws on restricted Lower Mississippi Survey grid-quadrat coordinates and is **not** distributed. Qualified researchers may obtain it from the authors and supply it to the validation scripts through a documented environment variable. We ask that users of the code honor the same policy and refrain from contributing unpublished site locations.

The fine-tuned vision-language interpretation layer is a QLoRA adapter over Qwen3-VL-30B-A3B-Thinking, trained zero-shot on New York State LiDAR. It is released separately as model weights via Hugging Face, linked from the repository. It is required only for the interpretation-layer ablation (Section 3.6; Appendix B, Table B1). ## Appendix A. Interpretation layer: training corpus, fine-tuning, and benchmark detail

This appendix gives the full build details summarized in Section 2.5.3. None of it measures the interpretation layer's contribution to mound detection (Section 3.6). It is provided for reproducibility.


![**Figure A1. Multi-view ensemble.** The six-panel composite supplied to the model for each candidate: four hillshades at different sun azimuths, the geomorphon classification, and a contour overlay. Multiple illumination angles reveal subtle relief and erosion texture that a single hillshade hides.](figures/fig2_multiview_ensemble.png)

**Training data.** The fine-tuning corpus is built from high-resolution New York State LiDAR (the public NYSGPO collections). New York offers varied, noisy terrain that forces the model to learn the geometric difference between natural and cultural relief rather than memorizing a single landscape. That terrain includes glacial drumlins and eskers, historic cellar holes, quarry pits, and military earthworks. No Yazoo Basin or other Delta data is used in training. The corpus is assembled by program. A DEM tile is reduced to geomorphons and hillshades, a deterministic routine reads off the ground-truth landform, and the pair is written as an image-plus-question training example. Three kinds of example dominate. *Geomorphon drills* (tens of thousands of items) present a hillshade-and-geomorphon composite and ask the model to name the dominant landform, balanced across all ten geomorphon classes. These were added to improve geomorphon classification accuracy, the competence on which everything downstream depends. *Multi-scale feature-identification* examples ask the model to map a morphological primitive at a given scale to a feature interpretation (for example, a linear Valley to a drainage channel, a flat-topped Peak to a platform). *Anti-forgetting replay* examples preserve the base model's general visual and reasoning ability so that domain adaptation does not erode it. The production checkpoint is fine-tuned on a balanced mixture of about 40,000 such examples; an earlier, dense model generation was trained on a far larger corpus (on the order of a million examples), and a central finding of our checkpoint history (below) is that the smaller balanced mixture, targeted at the weakest competence, was the more effective recipe.

**Fine-tuning method.** Adaptation uses QLoRA (Dettmers et al. 2023), which combines 4-bit quantization with low-rank adaptation (LoRA; Hu et al. 2021): the base model is loaded in 4-bit precision (NF4 with double quantization, bf16 compute) and frozen, and small low-rank adapter matrices are trained on top of it. We use a LoRA rank of 64 and an alpha of 128 with 0.05 dropout, and we freeze the vision tower so that only the language-and-reasoning pathway is adapted. Training runs for a single epoch at a learning rate of 1×10⁻⁵ (cosine schedule, brief warmup) with an effective batch size of 16 (a per-device batch of one with sixteen-step gradient accumulation) and a 1,024-token context. The entire run fits on a single workstation-class accelerator with unified memory, which is the point: the system is meant to be retrainable and deployable in the field, not only in a data center. QLoRA makes this feasible by training a few hundred million adapter parameters rather than all 30 billion of the base model.

**Checkpoint history and what it measures.** We track progress on an internal benchmark comprising 13 terrain-reasoning tasks grouped into 5 categories: geomorphon classification, tactical/landscape assessment, recovery-and-detection search, zero-shot transfer to unseen terrain, and domain terminology. The tasks are scored by expected-keyword recall and averaged by category. The trajectory shows that building this model was an exercise in trading capabilities against one another rather than climbing a single number. A first aligned checkpoint scored about 42% overall. A dense 32-billion-parameter "Thinking" variant, trained on the full ~1-million-example corpus, reached 84.1% overall, with geomorphon classification near 71%. Moving to the mixture-of-experts base and retraining on the 40,000-example balanced mixture produced an intermediate checkpoint. It pushed geomorphon classification up to about 85% but cut zero-shot transfer to about 74%. That is the wrong trade for a system whose premise is transfer to an unseen basin. We therefore rebalanced the mixture by adding zero-shot and recovery examples and trained a final run. From its checkpoints, we selected the deployed model because it is the best-balanced checkpoint in the run. It scores **85.3%** overall and passes every category threshold at once. The per-category scores are geomorphon classification **72.7%** (the only checkpoint in the run above the 70% bar that also keeps transfer high), zero-shot transfer **76.2%**, recovery search **96.4%**, tactical assessment **94.4%**, and domain terminology **90%**. On a mission-weighted metric that prioritizes the capabilities this application depends on (zero-shot transfer: 0.30, recovery: 0.25, tactical: 0.20, geomorphon: 0.15, terminology: 0.10), it scores **85.8%**. We chose it over later checkpoints that edged higher on the transfer-weighted metric but let geomorphon classification fall below the 70% threshold. For a pipeline whose first stage is a deterministic geomorphon detector, a model that has lost its grip on geomorphon vocabulary is the wrong trade.

Three cautions about this benchmark are owed to the reader. It originates in a broader **military terrain-analysis** project, and its five categories reflect that origin. Several test general or tactical terrain competence rather than archaeological mound detection, and the terminology category is a single task quoted at 90%. The thirteen tasks are scored based on expected keyword recall. This is a coarse proxy. It credits the presence of the right vocabulary without checking the reasoning behind it, and it can penalize a correct synonym. With only thirteen tasks, it cannot support fine distinctions between checkpoints, and small differences should not be over-read. The scores measure the model against this internal rubric, not against Delta earthworks. They indicate general terrain-reasoning competence, not performance in archaeological detection. The benchmark does not test detection. The ablation in Section 3.6 provides that test. The per-checkpoint scoring artifacts are released with the project so the numbers can be audited against the code.

## Appendix B. Supplemental methods, statistics, and the interpretation-layer ablation

This appendix holds the detailed statistics summarized in Sections 3.1, 3.2, and 3.6. None of it is required to follow the main argument; it is provided for completeness and reproducibility.

### B.1 Recall: tolerance curve, matching protocol, and missed mounds (Section 3.1)

**Coordinate correction protocol.** Every reference coordinate (the 35 Yazoo mounds and the 10 South Carolina shell rings of Section 3.7) was hand-verified before scoring. A web interface presents each site over a 500 m window in three registered layers (multidirectional hillshade, wide local relief, fine local relief); the analyst (CPL) clicks the center of the overall feature, flags sites with no visible expression, and can attach notes. Clicks are converted to UTM and WGS84 and saved with the shift from the catalog point; originals are never modified. Catalog error across the 44 visible sites: median 2.9 m, mean 16.0 m, maximum 198.6 m (19-M-2), with 21-M-1 at 107.3 m and 22-M-1 at 38.0 m. One mound, 22-N-11_m3, has no visible expression and is excluded from scoring.

**Recall against corrected centers** (Table 1): 44% at 10 m (15/34), 56% at 15 m (19/34), 68% at 20 m (23/34), 76% at 25 m (26/34), and 82% at 30 m (28/34); the 28 recovered offsets have median 6.1 m, mean 10.3 m, maximum 29.3 m. The detector is deterministic, so a repeat run on re-fetched DEMs returns the same result; this establishes pipeline determinism, not independent robustness. For comparison, agreement with the uncorrected catalog was 89% at 30 m (31/35, median 5.1 m); the difference is instructive. Two apparent catalog hits were spurious: at 19-M-2 and 21-M-1 the detector matched features 5 and 18 m from catalog points that lie 199 and 107 m from the actual mounds, and those matches sit 48 and 35 m from the true centers. One catalog miss (22-N-11_m3) was a data absence, not a detector failure. The six mounds missed against corrected centers are 22-M-1 (54 m), 22-N-13_m5 (51 m), 19-M-2 (48 m), 22-N-11_m2 (41 m), 22-N-21_m2 (41 m), and 21-M-1 (35 m); the first two are low-profile, 22-N-11_m2 sits in a tight cluster where a neighbor dominates the ranking, and 22-N-21_m2 is a borderline candidate that crossed the tolerance when the window recentered. The matching protocol is lenient: for each mound a 300 m tile is centered on the (corrected) point, the detector returns up to ten candidates, and a hit is scored if any of them falls within the tolerance. This is a best-case, centered-window sensitivity test rather than detection within an uncurated regional scan; a stricter single-best-candidate rule would make it harder. A residual convention difference also remains: the detector reports the centroid of its geomorphon peak mask, not the geometric center the analyst marks, which contributes several meters of offset on larger mounds.

**Decoy control.** Applying the Section 3.7 standard to the flagship recall: the frozen matching protocol (300 m centered window, up to ten candidates, nearest-candidate distance to window center) was run at four 500 m cardinal offsets from every corrected center, 135 viable decoy windows after skipping water and no-data (`scripts/yazoo_decoy_control.py`). Decoy hit rates: 2% at 10 m, 7% at 15 m, 10% at 20 m, 17% at 25 m, 20% at 30 m. Mound recall (Table 1) exceeds the decoy floor by 22 to 4 times across the tolerance range, so the containment counts carry detection signal rather than the density of mound-like relief in the landscape. The floor is conservative in three ways: windows that could never score (water, no-data, featureless fill) are skipped, which raises it, while windows where the detector returns no candidate are scored as misses; cardinal offsets from clustered mounds could land decoys near real archaeology, but a spatial join shows no decoy window holds another reference mound within 180 m of its center; and the decoys sample the landscape average rather than the levee-crest settings mounds prefer, so a terrain-matched decoy stratum would give a sterner floor at the wider tolerances.

**Recall by relief class.** Local relief at each corrected center (99th-percentile relief within 40 m against a 45 m Gaussian surface; `scripts/mound_relief_strata.py`) joined to the recall run gives: above 1.5 m, 19 of 22 recovered; 0.5 to 1.5 m, 8 of 11; below 0.5 m, 1 of 1. The metric is kernel-dependent (the 45 m Gaussian attenuates relief for footprints at or beyond the kernel scale), so the class boundaries approximate rather than equal mound height, and the single sub-0.5 m mound sits at 0.48 m, on the boundary. Recall declines with relief class (86% to 73%), the reference set likely over-represents taller surviving mounds, since leveling is exactly what removed Glover and Lula from the surface, and the set does not test the faintest class; extrapolation there is unpromising given the detector's 0.3 m floor. Per-mound values are released (`mound_relief_strata.csv`).

**Baseline comparison.** To test whether the geomorphon representation carries the result, we ran a simple local-relief baseline under the identical protocol against the corrected centers: the DEM minus a 40 m Gaussian surface, with the ten strongest local maxima as candidates. The baseline recovers 22 of 34 mounds at the 30 m tolerance (18 at 10 m), against the geomorphon detector's 28 of 34 (15 at 10 m). The geomorphon representation contributes six additional recoveries at 30 m over a plain relief-peak detector on the same windows, while the baseline is slightly better at the tightest tolerance, consistent with relief peaks centering on summits while geomorphon masks center on whole landforms (`scripts/lrm_baseline.py`).

### B.2 The positive-relief gate on Jaketown survivors (Section 3.2)

Applying the relief-and-compactness threshold (local relief at least 0.5 m and a 100–5,000 m² footprint) to the eighty Jaketown survivors leaves four and removes 76. Two readings of that number must both be resisted. It is not a precision measurement: none of the four is ground-truthed, and the 95% reduction reflects how flat and modern the survivor pool on this adversarial tile already was, which the desk screen had shown. It is also not an independent validation when checked against that same desk screen, because the threshold and the human review are two forms of one construct, positive relief plus compactness, so their agreement is close to circular; and the agreement is in any case only partial, since of the eight survivors the review rated plausible the threshold keeps three and discards five. The gate should be tuned as an operating point rather than a fixed cutoff: a 0.5 m relief floor suits open cropland but would discard the sub-0.5 m mounds the introduction emphasizes, so the threshold must be set to the regime being screened and its interaction with the detector's own 0.3 m floor characterized. A single adversarial tile cannot settle precision either way; disentangling the explanations requires precision measured on a randomly sited, non-adversarial tile with the complete noise map and the compactness gate enabled (Section 5), anchored by field verification.

### B.3 Interpretation-layer ablation (Section 3.6)

Eskew's (2008) contour-circularity algorithm flagged a set of candidate earthworks in the northern basin; she, Lipo, and Hunt then visited them on the ground in June 2008 and sorted them into genuine pre-European mounds and modern earthworks that resemble them (levee spoil, channel clearance, field berms). That field-verified split is the discrimination a downstream interpreter must make, and its vintage aligns with the imagery: the June 2008 ground verification and the 2009-2010 Delta Phase 1 LiDAR acquisition are nearly contemporaneous, so the field labels describe the surface the DEM records. We assemble it as a labeled set of six field-confirmed mounds (Glover Place, Gates, I.C. Village, Lula, and the open-terrain monuments Winterville and Lake George) and twenty-two field-confirmed modern earthworks. For each site we fetch the same bare-earth DEM, build the six-panel image the pipeline serves, and ask the deployed model for a MOUND / NOT-MOUND verdict, taking a majority vote over three runs because generation at temperature zero is not fully deterministic under vLLM's batching.

The model labels four of the six confirmed mounds as mounds (Gates, I.C. Village, Lula, Winterville); it returns NOT-MOUND for Glover and Lake George. An audit of the 3DEP surface at all six positives bounds this correctly: the maximum local relief within 80 m of the coordinate is 13.2 m at Winterville, 2.7 m at I.C. Village, and 2.6 m at Gates, but only 0.06 m at Glover, 0.06 m at Lula, and 0.45 m at canopy-covered Lake George. Glover and Lula have been leveled and Lake George is canopy-hidden, so only three positives are present in the data at all. On those three the model is three for three; its NOT-MOUND verdicts at Glover and Lake George are correct readings of surfaces that contain no mound; and its MOUND verdict at leveled Lula is a further over-acceptance. Of the twenty-two modern earthworks it accepts nineteen as mounds and rejects three (Table B1). As a filter for pre-European features it is ineffective. This clarifies the architecture rather than undermining it: every feature in the negative set is a compact, mound-like earthwork, so shape cannot separate a Mississippian mound from a 1970s canal-spoil pile. The separation is contextual, not morphological, and it is the shield's task. The route to improving the model is not region-specific data, because a mound's shape is the same in any valley, but stricter use of the structural combinations and a wider range of universal non-mound shapes in training.

The result (Table B2) is symmetric with the model's: the shield keeps all six mounds, but the land-cover and linearity layers reject only two of the twenty-two modern earthworks. Both mounds and levee-spoil piles sit as compact features in Cultivated Crops, so neither land cover nor linearity separates them. **The context arm.** The deployed pipeline supplies the model with context, and a human separates levee spoil from a mound by seeing the adjacent canal, which is invisible in a 160 m crop. A third arm therefore repeats the ablation with one change: alongside the standard six-panel detail, the model receives a 600 m context view of the same location (hillshade and local relief, candidate at center; `scripts/vlm_ablation_context.py`). No labels leak; the added input is more of the same public DEM.

Because generation is not fully deterministic under vLLM batching even at temperature zero, we ran the context arm three times. Modern-earthwork rejection was 9 of 21, 3 of 20, and 7 of 22 across the replicates (unscored sites are tied votes), pooled 19 of 63 (30%) against 3 of 22 (14%) in the image-only arm; mound recall ranged from three to five of six. The per-site record is more informative than the totals. Two to three modern earthworks are rejected in every replicate, one of them unanimously (zero MOUND votes in all nine queries): these are the sites where the wide view shows the compact pile sitting against the linear canal that produced it. About eight moderns are accepted in every replicate. The remainder, roughly half the set, flips between runs, almost always on two-to-one vote splits, and even the present-in-data mounds flip in one run. The model is at its decision boundary on these sites, and a three-vote majority is too small a sample of its output distribution to resolve them. A fourth arm was therefore designed to resolve them. Per-run results are released alongside the summary (`run1/2/3_results.csv`).

**The strongest zero-shot arm.** This arm combines every no-training improvement available to the architecture (`scripts/vlm_ablation_armc.py`): the six-panel detail and the 600 m context view, the nearest mapped modern feature from the OpenStreetMap proximity run supplied as text (the deployed pipeline's context-correlation input, exercised in the ablation for the first time), a structured rubric that requires one-line shape, context, and age checks before the verdict, and nine votes per site at an explicit sampling temperature of 0.6 in place of temperature-zero batching noise. The result is unambiguous. The model keeps six of six mounds and rejects zero of twenty-two modern earthworks, and the vote shares carry no age information: ranking sites by MOUND-vote fraction gives an area under the ROC curve of 0.33, at or below chance, with MOUND-vote shares on the moderns at least as high as on the mounds. The context-arm fluctuation was vote-sampling noise on split votes, and the suggestive context effect does not survive the stronger protocol. The model's settled zero-shot behavior on this set is that of a high-sensitivity recognizer of earthwork form: it accepts every field-confirmed earthwork, of either age, and added context views, added context text, a rubric, and more votes do not change it. Per-site results are released alongside (`armc_results.csv`).

**The learnability arm: the discrimination fine-tune.** The fifth arm tests whether the discriminator the prompting arms could not elicit can be trained, without breaking the zero-shot design. (The released weights carry the tags `terrallm-v91` for the deployed model and `terrallm-v10` for the discrimination fine-tune, and the released file names use the same tags.) The training set (`scripts/generate_discrimination_v10.py`) contains no Delta or coastal data. Negatives are 700 compact rises harvested from public 3DEP terrain beside OpenStreetMap-mapped canals, roads, railways, and drains in four boxes along New York's Erie Canal corridor (UTM 18N). Their NOT-MOUND labels are presumed from map proximity rather than assigned by an annotator, so they carry label noise: a visual audit of 25 randomly sampled negatives found 22 sitting beside visible construction with bank- or fill-like relief, 2 whose association with the named feature is unclear, and 1 on natural meander-scroll terrain where the stated rationale is unsupported (1 of 25 strict; the exact binomial 95% interval on that rate reaches about 20%, so a larger audit would tighten it). The audit tests whether the modern rationale is visible, not the converse risk that a harvested negative is itself a prehistoric feature; western New York holds documented mounds and earthworks near waterways, no check against a site inventory was made, and the risk is stated rather than bounded. Two of Eskew's numbered ground-survey points are excluded from the evaluation set: ID 14, whose coordinate Eskew flagged as likely incorrect, and ID 17, outside the study area; ID 24 is Glover Place, counted among the confirmed mounds. Positives are 700 synthetic eroded mounds (conical and platform, 15 to 60 m across, 0.5 to 5 m high, smoothed flanks) injected into the same terrain away from mapped features, so their labels are exact by construction. Each sample is one composite image (the 160 m six-panel detail above a 600 m context view) with the rubric prompt and a rubric-formatted response. Training used LoRA (rank 64, alpha 128) on the bf16 base model for two epochs at a learning rate of 1e-5, with a 1,000-sample replay of the original fine-tuning corpus to protect the existing recognition behavior, on the same single workstation as the original fine-tune (this adapter targets all linear layers, unlike the deployed adapter's attention-and-router targeting described in B.5).

Both models were then evaluated under the identical composite protocol (`scripts/vlm_eval_v10.py`; one composite image, the trained prompt, five votes per site, temperature 0.6). This arm uses five votes rather than the strongest zero-shot arm's nine because the composite image roughly doubles per-query cost; replicate runs at the same vote count are released alongside the primary run. Five votes were requested per site, but unparseable responses left two fine-tune sites with four parsed votes (including the one site scored as a tie, a 2-2 split) and eight deployed-model sites with three or four; the same parse shortfalls recur in the replicate and occlusion runs at similar rates, per the released CSVs. On the Eskew set the deployed model keeps six of six mounds and rejects one of twenty-two moderns, with a vote-share AUC of 0.48, which replicates the strongest-prompt conclusion under the new protocol. The discrimination fine-tune keeps five of six mounds and rejects eight of twenty-one (the tied site is excluded from the rejection denominator; the AUC includes it at share 0.5, and excluding it gives 0.61), three of them with zero MOUND votes, and its vote-share AUC is 0.62 (bootstrap 95% CI 0.38 to 0.83; Figure 11c). Because the context arm showed that few-vote majorities fluctuate, both evaluations were replicated: two further five-vote fine-tune runs return rejections of 10 of 21 and 9 of 22 (AUCs 0.71 and 0.64), a second base-model run returns 1 of 22 again (AUC 0.49), I.C. Village is the mound lost in every fine-tune replicate, and five moderns are rejected in all three runs with thirteen in at least one. Replicates reuse the same sites and both models judge the same sites, so pooled site-run counts would overstate independence; the inference comes from paired site-level tests over the 22 modern-earthwork sites with rejection as the outcome: on the primary runs the discordant sites split eight to one in the fine-tune's favor (exact McNemar, one-sided p = 0.020, two-sided 0.039), and on per-site majority verdicts across replicates nine to one (one-sided p = 0.011, two-sided 0.021). Descriptively, the runs total 27 of 64 rejections against 2 of 44. The pooled per-site mean-share AUC is 0.66 (bootstrap 95% CI 0.39 to 0.89). The between-model contrast attributes the behavior change to the drills; the within-model class contrast is weaker: the fine-tune rejects 8 of 21 moderns against 1 of 6 mounds, and 1 of 3 present-in-data mounds, rates these sample sizes cannot distinguish, so what is demonstrated is a context-typed modern-construction filter rather than an age discriminator as such. All replicate outputs are released (`data/v10_eval/`).

A replay-only control isolates the drills' contribution from fine-tuning as such (`config/training/v10_control_replay.yaml`): the same LoRA recipe trained for the same two epochs on the 1,000-sample replay corpus alone, with no discrimination drills. The control collapses toward rejection: under the identical protocol it rejects 19 of 22 moderns but keeps only one of six mounds, and the one it keeps is canopy-hidden Lake George, which contains no mound in the bare-earth data, while all three present-in-data mounds are rejected. Its vote shares still rank the classes (AUC 0.77), and that number carries a lesson: a control with no discrimination training outscores the drill model's 0.62 on this metric, propped up by a share of 1.0 at canopy-hidden Lake George, a positive that contains no mound, so vote-share AUC at six positives is not reliable evidence of learned discrimination, which is why the paired rejection tests carry the inference throughout. No verdict behavior this control produces is usable, because it spends five of six mounds to reject moderns, the maximal violation of the Section 4 cost asymmetry. Completing the factorial with the two single-class cells (`config/training/v10_factorial_neg.yaml`, `v10_factorial_pos.yaml`; each 700 drills of one class plus the same replay) decomposes the recipe. The negatives-only cell collapses entirely: it rejects all 22 moderns and all 6 mounds, learned refusal rather than discrimination. The positives-only cell nearly reproduces the full-drill operating point: 6 of 21 moderns rejected at five of six mounds kept, and it keeps I.C. Village, losing Gates instead. The five cells together read: base, 1/22 rejected at 6/6 kept; replay-only, 19/22 at 1/6; negatives-only, 22/22 at 0/6; positives-only, 6/21 at 5/6; full drills, 8/21 at 5/6. The attribution is therefore the reverse of the design intuition, and the decisive contrast is the one comparison that is step-matched: the negatives-only and positives-only cells saw identical training (3,400 sample-passes each, the same replay, only the drill class differing) and land at 22/22 rejected with no mounds kept against 6/21 with five of six kept. Negatives alone teach refusal; the synthetic positives anchor mound-form retention, and the retention cannot be drift because the positives-only cell trained longer than the replay-only cell (2,000 passes) that lost five mounds. The subsidiary comparison is not step-matched: the full-drill cell saw about 40% more optimization than the positives-only cell (4,800 passes), drift alone pushes rejection up, and the full cell's added rejections are not a superset of the positives-only cell's, so the two-rejection difference reads as threshold churn at least as much as negative-drill sharpening and is not attributed. Both drill cells spend exactly one of the three present-in-data mounds, the positives-only cell losing Gates (2.6 m relief) where the full cell loses I.C. Village, so neither recipe preserves the archaeological positives at no cost (`eskew_ctrl.csv`, `eskew_factorial_neg.csv`, `eskew_factorial_pos.csv`). The lost mound is I.C. Village, one of the three positives with measurable relief in the public product, so the cost is real and not an artifact of leveled sites, and recall on present-in-data mounds is two of three. The leveled Glover and Lula are still accepted, so the learned filter is not simple relief thresholding, though the same acceptances admit a less favorable reading: a MOUND class only weakly coupled to mound morphology, with the rejection behavior keying on visible modern construction in the 600 m context view rather than on age as such. An occlusion test supports the context reading (`scripts/vlm_eval_v10_occlusion.py`): replacing the context view with neutral gray collapses the fine-tune's rejections to 1 of 22, none of the primary run's eight rejections survives, and the mound lost shifts from I.C. Village to Lula, a coherent fallback to reading the detail view rather than random disruption. A gray block is out of distribution for a model trained on real-context composites, so the masked result alone would be consistent with input disruption; context transplants close that objection with in-distribution inputs (`scripts/vlm_eval_v10_variants.py`). Given the same quiet real context at every site (Glover Place's), rejections collapse to 1 of 22, matching the gray mask. Given the same construction-heavy context at every site (Ground-Survey 01's), rejections rise to 9 of 22 while mound retention falls to three of six, so the filter rejects real mounds when handed busy context. And with the detail view masked and the true context intact, rejection persists at 11 of 22, so context is sufficient and the candidate's own form is not required. Verdicts follow the context view in both directions on the modern-earthwork side: the context-reading mechanism is demonstrated there. Two design limits are owed the reader. Each condition uses a single donor context, both donors belong to the evaluation set (for the donor's own site the transplant is an identity condition), so each effect estimate carries donor-level idiosyncrasy; the modern-side effects (a 21-site collapse, an 8-site rise) are large enough to survive that. The mound side is less clean: I.C. Village is still rejected under the quiet donor context but kept under the busy one, and the detail mask loses Gates as well (mound retention 5/6, 5/6, 3/6, 4/6 across the four variants), so mound verdicts do not simply follow the context view, and part of the I.C. Village rejection travels with the detail view or a view interaction. Two structural cautions bound the inference. The drill corpus tied label to context by construction, so context was a perfectly predictive training cue and a shortcut-taking learner would not consult form; and the positives are centered synthetic injections, so form cues were never de-confounded from position or texture. The filter as trained is a visual context reader on the evidence dimension the shield consults by map lookup, and whether age-from-form is trainable at this resolution is untested rather than refuted. An evaluation on real, non-synthetic positives remains the outstanding check. Cross-tabulating the fine-tune's eight rejections against the query-time proximity run shows the learned filter working where the map is silent: one rejected site (Ground-Survey 07) has no OpenStreetMap feature within 300 m, three others were KEEP verdicts for the full shield, three were FLAGs, and only one is a site the shield itself rejects (`shield_eskew_proximity.csv` against `eskew_v10.csv`). The filter thus escapes the map-completeness ceiling that caps the rule-based layer, though it is capped instead by what the 600 m window shows, and its whole-window aperture, against the proximity layer's 25 m rejection radius, is consistent with the loss of I.C. Village. On the South Carolina set under the same protocol the fine-tune keeps nine of ten corrected ring centers but rejects zero of ten land decoys. The base model under the identical protocol keeps ten of ten rings and also rejects zero of ten decoys, so the fine-tune's ring recognition is inherited from the base (the one ring it loses, Davis_32, is its own regression) and neither model rejects coastal decoys. Each decoy is the first viable 500 m cardinal offset from a ring with at least 0.5 m of window relief; decoys are not verified to contain a compact rise, so decoy acceptance mixes generic over-acceptance with the specific failure that the drills teach one negative context, mapped modern construction, and the coastal decoys hold natural terrain outside that class. Two deployment tests measure what the learned verdicts are worth on real candidate streams (`scripts/vlm_eval_v10_stream.py`). On the eighty Jaketown survivors, ranked by MOUND-vote share, the desk review's eight plausibles land slightly below the seventy-two desk-rejected candidates (AUC 0.43, bootstrap 95% CI 0.26 to 0.61; mean share 0.45 against 0.52; five of the eight plausibles are voted NOT-MOUND), so the ordering does not enrich the review queue and would bury the plausibles. On the cropland tract's 54 agricultural-island keeps, the fine-tune rejects 26 (48%), which is the measured form of the collision its mechanism predicts: a visible-construction reader fires on the busy context of active fields, the setting the island rule protects (`jaketown_survivors_v10.csv`, `agisland_keeps_v10.csv`). Three conclusions follow. A context-typed rejection of modern earthworks is trainable from universal, proximity-labeled data, so the prompting null is not a capability ceiling, though what was learned is context reading, not age from form. The learned verdicts have no demonstrated deployment role: as a filter they spend real mounds, as a ranking they do not enrich, and on agricultural islands they invert the shield's protection, so the arm's contribution is boundary-mapping. And the sensitivity-first architecture is unchanged: no reported detection uses the discrimination fine-tune, whose per-site results are released alongside (`data/v10_eval/`).

### B.5 Detector, shield, and serving configuration (Sections 2.2, 2.4, 2.5)

**Multi-scale persistence (implemented, not yet in the detection path).** Mounds exist at different sizes. The geomorphon engine therefore computes classifications at four search radii, 2, 5, 10, and 25 m, with a per-cell persistence score. The score measures how well a cell keeps the same landform class across scales: a feature that is a Peak at 2 m and remains a Peak at 5 m and 10 m is more credible than one that appears at a single scale and vanishes at the next. In the present study this multi-scale representation is used to generate training data for the vision-language model. It is **not** wired into the detection-and-validation path, which uses the single-scale classifier of Section 2.2. Integrating multi-scale persistence into detection and re-running the Section 3 validation against it are tasks for future work; we expect it to improve recovery of the smallest and largest mounds that a single 5 m scale serves least well.

**Shield layer thresholds (Section 2.4).** Linearity: a bounding-box aspect ratio of at least 4.5 rejects the candidate; 3.0 to 4.5 flags it with a reduced score; the test is disabled for enclosure queries. Land cover: NLCD Developed classes (21 to 24) and Open Water (11) reject; compact candidates (aspect < 2.5) in Cultivated Crops (82) or Pasture/Hay (81) are kept as agricultural islands; aspect 2.5 to 4.5 in those classes flags as a probable plow line or ditch. Proximity: modern linear features are extracted from US Topo and Historical Topographic Map Collection quadrangles by optical character recognition and color-based line extraction, extended to irrigation features, borrow pits, and abandoned railroad grades; a candidate within 25 m of a mapped feature is rejected, within 50 m flagged. A distinct internal detector parameter, an aspect-ratio down-weighting at 4.0, exists inside the candidate scorer and is separate from the shield's 4.5/3.0 thresholds.

**Noise-map and service-failure handling.** The modern-feature layer depends on a noise map that must be generated per area of interest by downloading the relevant quadrangles and running optical character recognition, which has a per-image cost; the regional scanner logs a warning when it runs without one. When the land-cover service is unreachable, which happened during the Jaketown run of Section 3.3, the shield does not treat the candidate as clean: it records the layer as unavailable and marks the candidate's context as incomplete, so downstream reporting can distinguish a candidate that passed the test from one that was never tested. The land-cover layer was repointed from an Esri service that began requiring an authentication token to the token-free MRLC public WMS.

**Serving and hardware (Section 2.5.2).** The model is served with vLLM behind an OpenAI-compatible API. The adapter targets the attention projections and the mixture-of-experts router, so it is served as a LoRA module without merging. The runs reported here used a single ARM workstation with 121 GB of unified CPU-GPU memory; serving the bf16 base with adapters occupies about 65 GB, a 4-bit-quantized serving of the same model fits in roughly 20 GB of accelerator memory, and fine-tuning the bf16 base with LoRA fits within the same 121 GB. On this hardware a full interpretation takes on the order of twenty to thirty seconds per candidate, against about two seconds for the deterministic detector.

## Appendix C. Extended limitations and work program

The full statement of each limitation summarized in Section 5.

**Age discrimination is measured and remains unsolved (Section 3.6, Appendix B.3).** No reported detection depends on the fine-tuned model. On Eskew's field-verified set, the image-only model accepts 19 of 22 modern earthworks and the shield's active layers reject only 2 of 22, so neither shape nor land-cover rules discriminate age. A 600 m context view appeared to help in three-vote replicates (rejection 9/21, 3/20, 7/22), but the strongest zero-shot protocol, adding mapped-feature text, a structured rubric, and nine votes per site, keeps 6 of 6 mounds, rejects 0 of 22 moderns, and shows no signal in the vote shares (AUC 0.33), so the apparent gain was vote-sampling noise. The model reads earthwork form, not age, and prompting does not change that. The proximity layer, activated from open OpenStreetMap data, rejects 3 of 22 and flags 6 more, bounded by how few of the rural Delta's field drains that map records; a quad-derived noise map with historical features may raise that ceiling. Fine-tuning against universal modern-earthwork negatives, tested as the fifth arm, raises rejection from 1 of 22 to 8 of 21 (paired site-level McNemar p = 0.011) at the cost of one of the three present mounds; the occlusion test shows the filter reads visible construction, not age from form, and its learned rejection does not transfer to the coastal decoys, so the filter is partial and typed to the contexts it was taught. The remaining paths for the second stage are richer context data and a second-generation drill corpus (jittered injection positions, natural-rise negatives, real public-monument positives outside the study regions, and context decorrelated from label), rather than region-specific data.

**The detector evaluated is a single-scale approximation.** Section 3's results use a single-scale, elevation-referenced geomorphon classifier (Section 2.2), not the multi-scale, angle-thresholded, persistence-scored method that the engine also implements and that the introduction motivates (Appendix B.5). Integrating multi-scale persistence into the detection path and rerunning the Section 3 evaluation with it are priorities. The single-scale 0.3 m flatness floor may suppress the sub-0.5 m mounds we emphasize. We expect the multi-scale form to change the recovery of the faintest features and most likely improve it.

**Baseline comparison (measured).** A local-relief blob detector run under the identical protocol against the corrected centers recovers 22 of 34 mounds at 30 m against the geomorphon detector's 28 of 34 (Appendix B.1), so the geomorphon representation, not the protocol, carries the margin. A comparison against an object-based rule set remains worthwhile.

**Reproducibility bookkeeping.** Different reported numbers were produced by different scripts and, at present, two coordinate systems (Section 2.1). Table 3 maps each number to its script, coordinate system, and required data, and Appendix B.5 documents the internal aspect-ratio down-weighting (4.0) that is distinct from the shield's 4.5/3.0 linearity thresholds, so the two linearity tests are not conflated.

**Shield precision is unmeasured by field truth, and a desk screen suggests it is low on modern-dominated tiles.** We report detector recall and the shield's rejection rate (71% at Jaketown), but not field-verified precision. A preliminary visual review of the Jaketown survivors (Section 3.2) found none that present as convincing mounds. The survivors are compact modern and natural features (field-edge berms, drainage and dredge-spoil ridges, borrow pits, natural levees) that clear the linearity and land-cover layers. This is a desk screen, not ground truth. It exposes a concrete gap: the shield lacks a simple positive-relief-and-compactness filter, which on the Jaketown tile does most of the mound-versus-modern separation the other layers do not, cutting the 80 survivors to 4 (Appendix B.2). We recommend adding such a layer as standard, tuned as an operating point rather than a fixed cutoff: a 0.5 m floor suits open cropland but would discard the sub-0.5 m mounds we target. Converting any of this into a precision figure requires the labeled, non-adversarial, ground-truthed evaluation that remains required and is in preparation. The review burden the shield leaves is quantifiable from the scans: the cropland tract retains 93 kept-or-flagged candidates over about 1.7 km² (roughly 55 per km²), and the adversarial Jaketown tile retains 80 over about 4.3 km². At basin scale those densities imply a manual queue in the hundreds of thousands, which is the arithmetic that makes second-stage filtering, the positive-relief gate and the discrimination fine-tune, the binding constraint on practical use.

**Land-cover dependence on an external service.** The land-cover layer depends on a remote public service whose access policy can change without notice (one endpoint began requiring an authentication token during this work; the pipeline now uses the token-free MRLC public WMS, Appendix B.5). A cached or local NLCD raster would make the layer dependable offline.

**Noise map coverage.** The modern-feature layer is only as good as the quadrangle coverage and the optical character recognition behind it, and it must be generated per area of interest. Its recall against actual canals and levees has not been quantified.

**Catalog error is measured, and it matters.** Hand-correcting every reference coordinate against the relief (Section 3.1) put a number on the legacy catalog's error: median 2.9 m, maximum 199 m. Two apparent detector hits were artifacts of the largest errors, and one cataloged mound has no visible expression at all. Recall is therefore reported against corrected feature centers rather than catalog agreement. The residual centroid-to-center offsets (median 6.1 m; Table 1) mix detector centering with the difference between a peak-mask centroid and a geometric center, which a centroid-to-outline comparison could separate.

**Zero-shot transfer.** Transfer from New York terrain to the Delta is the design's central bet. Sections 3.6 and 3.7 now measure the model on Delta and coastal ground truth for the recognition and discrimination tasks; what remains unmeasured is detection precision and any field verification. The internal benchmark's 76.2% zero-shot category score is a proxy for general terrain competence only. Independent ground truth beyond the reference set is needed to corroborate the recall estimate.

**Canopy is a hard limit of the public bare-earth product.** The corrected Lake George rescan (Section 3.4) shows that the seamless 3DEP DTM does not contain the 17 m Mound A under closed bottomland-hardwood canopy. The method is blind to forested mounds until a leaf-off or custom canopy-penetrating surface is supplied. Recovery is product-dependent, and quantifying it across the basin's forested fraction is future work.

**Case studies remain partly preliminary.** The Jaketown rejection rate, the corrected Lake George negative, and the cropland agricultural-island scan are reported as demonstrations of shield *behavior*. None is yet a precision measurement. Ground-truthing of the Jaketown and cropland survivors, and a canopy-penetrating reacquisition at Lake George, are the next steps.
