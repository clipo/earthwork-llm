# Toward Label-Free Archaeological Prospection: Training-Free Detection and Zero-Shot Interpretation of Pre-European Earthworks from Public LiDAR in the Yazoo Basin and Coastal South Carolina

**Authors**: C. P. Lipo, D. S. Davis, and R. J. DiNapoli

**Target Journal**: *Archaeological Prospection*

**Status**: Working manuscript. The paper's argument is that a **label-free, zero-shot** approach is a workable and scalable direction for earthwork survey. Such an approach needs no labeled examples from the target landscape. It fits settings where the finite archaeological record cannot supply the training data that supervised detectors require. We evaluate it as a sensitivity (recall) and false-positive screening study, not as a discovery report. No candidates are field-verified here. Age discrimination is tested five ways against a field-verified set (Section 3.6): neither shape nor the shield's active rules separate pre-European from modern earthworks, and under the strongest zero-shot protocol the model accepts every earthwork of either age; fine-tuning on universal discrimination drills recovers a partial age signal (8/21 rejection, vote-share AUC 0.62) that does not yet transfer between regions. The workflow is presented as a staged pipeline: exhaustive morphological detection of earthworks of any age, contextual screening that flags likely modern ones, and manual evaluation. A generalization test on coastal South Carolina shell rings (Section 3.7) supports the representation-transfer claim; a decoy-point control shows a first ring detector performs at chance. Claims are scoped accordingly.

## Abstract

Archaeological survey in the Yazoo Basin is hard for four reasons: mound relief is often under half a meter; modern earthworks mimic it; the area spans thousands of square kilometers; and supervised detectors need labels the finite archaeological record cannot supply. We describe a **label-free** workflow. A geomorphon classifier reduces the bare-earth DEM to landform types, a deterministic detector extracts compact candidates, and a *False Positive Shield* screens them against land cover, linearity, and mapped modern features. A vision-language model fine-tuned on New York State LiDAR serves as an interpretation layer. The untrained detector recovers 28 of 34 hand-verified mound centers (82%) within a 30 m tolerance. On a field-verified set neither shape nor land-cover rules separate pre-European from modern earthworks, and the strongest zero-shot protocol accepts every earthwork of either age. Fine-tuning on drills built from New York terrain alone raises rejection from one to eight of twenty-one, at the cost of one present mound, so the rejection behavior is trainable from universal data but does not yet transfer. That null motivates a staged design: the workflow detects earthworks of any age, screens them against modern context, and leaves the decision to manual evaluation. The representation transfers with no retraining to coastal South Carolina shell rings, though a decoy-point control shows a first ring detector performs at chance. Precision on new candidates is not measured, and canopy can hide even a 17 m mound. We present the workflow as a sensitivity and screening study showing that label-free survey scales.


## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The problem of the Delta

The Yazoo Basin of northwestern Mississippi, the Delta, holds one of the densest concentrations of pre-European earthen architecture in North America: a center of Woodland and Mississippian settlement within a Lower Mississippi Valley mound-building tradition whose roots reach back to the Late Archaic (Grooms et al. 2023; Lipo and Dunnell 2007). The largest centers, such as Winterville and Lake George, remain prominent monuments, though as Section 3.4 shows, canopy can hide even a 17 m mound from an airborne bare-earth survey. Most mounds are less conspicuous: conical features under a meter in height, eroded and plowed, hidden in secondary forest or standing alone in cropland.

Since the early twentieth century, the Delta has been transformed. Mechanized agriculture has leveled low relief across most of the basin. Large flood-control works by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers have reshaped the rest. Dredging for drainage and navigation produced thousands of kilometers of canals. Each canal is flanked by linear spoil banks. Once eroded and vegetated, these banks take on a shape close to that of an ancient embankment. Levees, raised roadbeds, and industrial pads contribute to the noise. This is the topographic signature of modern human activity, and any high-resolution terrain method must contend with it (Tarolli and Sofia 2016).

This combination is what makes the Delta hard. A human analyst scanning hillshade tiles for mounds faces survey fatigue and a tendency to fixate on large, obvious structures while missing low-profile features. The landscape also yields many false positives. An automated approach sensitive enough to catch a 0.3 m rise will also flag every spoil bank in its path. The cost of that sensitivity is paid later, in wasted ground-truthing.

### 1.2 Contribution

Automated detection of archaeological features from airborne LiDAR is an established and active field (Chase et al. 2010; Vinci et al. 2024). Object-based image analysis has located earthen mounds and shell rings in the Southeastern United States (Davis et al. 2018, 2019) and large earthworks elsewhere (Freeland et al. 2016), and convolutional and region-based neural networks now detect barrows, burial mounds, qanats, and similar structures in LiDAR-derived terrain (Verschoof-van der Vaart and Lambers 2019; Caspari and Crespo 2019; Guyot et al. 2021; Soroush et al. 2020; Kadhim et al. 2023). These supervised methods work, but they share a dependence that is a structural obstacle for archaeology: they need tens to hundreds of labeled examples of a feature class, and their accuracy drops when a detector trained in one region or on one sensor is moved to another (Guyot et al. 2021; Lambers et al. 2019). The archaeological record is finite and unevenly documented. For many feature types and regions, the labeled examples a supervised detector needs do not exist, and producing them is costly, sometimes destructive, and in some cases not possible (Bickler 2021). A method whose need for labeled data grows with every new landscape does not scale to a discipline in which the training signal is fixed and small. This is the situation in the Yazoo Basin, where the known-mound sample is small, of uneven positional quality, and cannot be enlarged at will.

Machine learning offers a response. **Foundation models** are trained on large, general data sets and applied to new tasks and domains with little or no task-specific labeling; applying a model to a task or place it never saw in training is called *zero-shot* (Figure 2; Radford et al. 2021; Bommasani et al. 2021). The approach now reaches remote sensing (Kuckreja et al. 2024; Mai et al. 2024) and the detection of buried archaeological structures (Sech et al. 2023). Its value for archaeology is that it separates where a model learns from where it is applied: the training signal comes from terrain the discipline need not have excavated, and the finite archaeological record is devoted to evaluation and interpretation rather than to training.

The detection side of this idea is older than the machine-learning vocabulary for it. Rule-based morphometric detection needs no training labels either: geomorphons are a fixed geometric classification (Jasiewicz and Stepinski 2013), and the object-based image analysis and template matching that two of us applied to these same feature classes (Davis et al. 2018, 2019) are label-free in the same sense. What this paper adds to that lineage is four things: an automated, auditable context shield that turns the ancillary screening Davis et al. (2018) did by hand into a reproducible KEEP, FLAG, or REJECT verdict; a measured tolerance-recall characterization against a regional catalog in true meters; a measured test of what a fine-tuned vision-language model adds, which Section 3.6 answers; and a test of whether one representation transfers across region and feature type (Section 3.7). We use *training-free* for the deterministic detection and reserve *zero-shot* for the model's deployment, where the term applies in its proper sense.

**This paper shows that the label-free approach is workable for earthwork survey, and it reports the result in plain terms.** The workflow uses no labeled examples from the target landscape: the geomorphon detector is deterministic and trained on nothing, and the vision-language interpretation layer is fine-tuned on New York State terrain and applied to the Delta without local labels. A pipeline built this way recovers most of a catalog of known Delta mounds (Section 3.1), having seen no Delta mound in training. This is the paper's central claim. We do not claim that Delta survey is solved; we claim that a zero-shot, foundation-model approach works well enough to be worth developing, and that it is one of the few approaches that scales to a finite record. Within that approach, the workflow makes three design choices grounded in the literature. It reasons about shape rather than height, working on geomorphons rather than raw elevation, which suits a near-level plain. It automates the context screening recommended by Davis et al. (2018), combining land-cover class, mapped modern features, and footprint linearity into a KEEP, FLAG, or REJECT shield, because shape alone cannot separate mounds from modern earthworks (Davis et al. 2018, 2019). And it adds a fine-tuned vision-language model as an interpretation layer for the candidates that survive screening.

We are careful about how far the demonstration reaches. We measure the label-free detector's sensitivity, the shield's screening behavior, and the contribution of the interpretation layer (Section 3.6). We do not measure end-to-end precision on new candidates; that requires field verification, which we identify as the next step. We say so wherever a claim rests on a single illustrative run rather than a controlled experiment, and we claim the value of a demonstrated approach with stated limits, not a finished instrument.

### 1.3 Study area

The Yazoo Basin is an elliptical alluvial plain of about 11,000 km² between the Mississippi River on the west and the bluffs on the east (Figure 1). It is flat. Slopes are under one percent in most places. Its surface records the migration of the Mississippi and Yazoo rivers as a set of abandoned channels, relict natural levees, and backswamp depressions. The archaeological record ranges from multi-mound platform centers such as Winterville and Lake George (Holly Bluff) to small, isolated conical mounds. The survey problem here is not finding height. It is separating deliberate human construction from modern disturbance on a near-level plain.

## 2. Methodology

The pipeline has four stages: data ingestion, geomorphon classification, the False Positive Shield, and AI reasoning (Figure 3). Each stage narrows the candidate set. The most expensive operation is a call to the vision-language model. It runs only on candidates that have survived the cheaper shape and context filters.

![**Figure 3. The screening workflow as a three-stage pipeline.** Stage 1 is morphological and exhaustive: the public 3DEP bare earth is reduced to geomorphon landform types, and structural combinations of those types extract compact earthwork candidates of any age, because age is not present in shape. Stage 2 is contextual and conservative: the False Positive Shield reads land cover, footprint linearity, and mapped modern features and returns KEEP, FLAG, or REJECT, flagging rather than silently deleting; the fine-tuned Qwen3-VL-30B-A3B-Thinking model adds an optional reading of earthwork form (Section 3.6). Stage 3 is manual evaluation of the concentrated, ranked candidate list. The detector evaluated in Section 3 uses a single 5 m scale with a 0.3 m elevation-difference tolerance (Section 2.2).](figures/fig4_workflow.png)

### 2.1 Data ingestion

Bare-earth DEMs are fetched from the USGS 3D Elevation Program (3DEP) ImageServer at 1 m resolution. The regional-scan pipeline processes them in Web Mercator (EPSG:3857), which at the basin's latitude inflates ground distances by about 19% and areas by about 42%; the recall evaluation of Section 3.1 is computed in the analytical CRS, UTM Zone 15N, and the distances and areas quoted for the case studies have been converted to true ground meters by the local scale factor. Regional areas of interest are tiled into 500 m squares with 50 m of overlap. LiDAR coverage of the Delta derives largely from the 2009 to 2010 USACE Delta Phase 1 acquisition, and working from 3DEP delivers a consistent, gridded, bare-earth product without per-tile point-cloud handling.

### 2.2 Geomorphon classification

LiDAR transformed archaeology by giving bare-earth surfaces under vegetation. A single hillshade is a subjective instrument. What it shows depends on the sun angle the analyst chooses. Instead, we reduce the DEM to **geomorphons** (Jasiewicz and Stepinski 2013), a pattern-recognition classification of local landforms.

For each cell, the method casts lines of sight in eight directions and asks whether the terrain along each line rises, falls, or stays within a flatness tolerance, yielding a ternary code whose counts of ups and downs map the cell to one of ten landform classes: Flat, Peak, Ridge, Shoulder, Spur, Slope, Hollow, Footslope, Valley, and Pit (Figure 4). A cell that looks down in every direction is a Peak; a linear high is a Ridge; a linear low is a Valley. In the original formulation the flatness tolerance is an *angle* (Jasiewicz and Stepinski use 1.0°), so classification depends on local form rather than absolute height.

We report our implementation in full, because it differs from that canonical form in two respects that a reader must know to interpret the results. The classifier that produced every detection in Section 3 is a **single-scale** line-of-sight classifier with a search radius of five cells (5 m on the 1 m DEM), and its flatness tolerance is an **elevation-difference threshold of 0.3 m** across that window rather than an angle. The angular, multi-scale form with per-cell persistence scoring is implemented in the same geomorphon engine but is not the classifier that generated the reported detections; it is used to generate training data for the vision-language model, and its configuration and status are given in Appendix B.5. The 0.3 m tolerance sets a practical floor for the relief the detector registers; its interaction with the sub-0.5 m mounds, as emphasized in the introduction, is a limitation we return to in Section 5.

![**Figure 4. Geomorphons.** (A) For each cell, eight lines of sight are classified as rising (+), falling (−), or level (0) using a flatness threshold (1.0 degree in the canonical angular method illustrated here; the evaluated detector substitutes a 0.3 m elevation-difference tolerance, Section 2.2). (B) The eight ternary values form a code; a cell that looks down in all directions resolves to PEAK. (C) Six diagnostic landforms and their archaeological analogs. Classification depends on local morphology, not absolute elevation, which is why the method finds subtle mounds on a flat plain.](figures/fig_geomorphon_concepts.png)

This morphological framing is what gives the archaeological mapping its power. A conical burial mound resolves as a compact Peak, a platform mound as a flat-topped Peak or a Flat bounded by Shoulders, a levee or embankment as a linear Ridge, and a drainage ditch or canal as a linear Valley. The classifier is generic; the archaeological interpretation is imposed afterward.

### 2.3 Candidate extraction

The deterministic detector converts a geomorphon raster into a short list of candidate footprints. It selects the landform classes appropriate to the query (for a mound query, Peak, Ridge, Shoulder, and Spur), labels connected components of the resulting mask, and keeps components whose area falls in a class-specific window (for example, 20 to 5,000 m² for a generic mound, 100 to 10,000 m² for a platform mound). For each surviving component, it records the centroid, the footprint area, the local height relative to surrounding terrain, and the bounding-box aspect ratio. The aspect ratio is carried forward as the linearity input to the shield. The detector returns the ten strongest candidates per tile.

### 2.4 The False Positive Shield

The shield is the part of the system that turns sensitivity into usable specificity. Its premise is not new: Davis et al. (2018) identified land-use context and historical map comparison as means to suppress the levee- and construction-derived false positives that defeat morphology-only detection in modified landscapes. Our contribution is an *automated* form of that guidance, which queries the context sources by program and combines them into an auditable KEEP, FLAG, or REJECT verdict that surfaces borderline and untestable cases rather than passing them without record, and which protects compact "agricultural island" mounds within cultivated fields. Three independent layers combine into one verdict per candidate (Figure 5); a single decisive layer is enough to reject, and rejected candidates are dropped before any model call.

1. **Morphological linearity.** Prehistoric mounds are compact; levees, road beds, and canal spoil banks are long and thin. Footprints above an aspect-ratio rejection threshold are dropped as linear features, and moderate elongation is flagged and retained for review. The test is disabled for enclosure queries, which target elongated features.

2. **NLCD land cover.** The National Land Cover Database class at the candidate point is queried from a public service. Developed land and open water are modern, not prehistoric, and candidates on them are rejected. Compact features in Cultivated Crops or Pasture/Hay are kept as possible **agricultural islands**, because mounds often survive within mechanized fields; moderate elongation there is flagged as a probable plow line or ditch.

3. **Modern feature proximity.** Canals, ditches, levees, roads, and similar features are read from USGS topographic quadrangles, including the Historical Topographic Map Collection, whose early-twentieth-century features (such as logging roads now under canopy) are absent from modern maps. A candidate within the rejection radius of such a feature is dropped as probable spoil or road shoulder; within the flag radius it is marked for review.

The thresholds, class codes, and radii for all three layers are stated in Appendix B.5.

![**Figure 5. The False Positive Shield.** Three context layers screen every candidate and combine into a single verdict. A linear footprint, developed land or open water, or a mapped modern feature within the rejection radius is each sufficient to reject. Borderline cases are flagged and retained with a reduced score. When a layer cannot be evaluated, the candidate is recorded as having incomplete context rather than being passed without record.](figures/fig_fp_shield.png)

One implementation rule matters for honest reporting: a layer that cannot be evaluated (a missing noise map, an unreachable land-cover service) does not pass the candidate as clean. The shield records the layer as unavailable and marks the candidate's context as incomplete, so downstream reporting can distinguish a candidate that passed a test from one that was never tested (Appendix B.5).

### 2.5 AI reasoning

The final stage passes shield survivors to a fine-tuned vision-language model for a natural-language archaeological reading. We state at the outset what Section 3 bears out: **no reported detection number depends on the model**. The regional scans stop at the shield, and the model's direct test is Section 3.6. What follows describes the designed and benchmarked capability whose measured contribution is reported there.

#### 2.5.1 The multi-view ensemble

Each surviving candidate is rendered as a six-panel composite image (Appendix A, Figure A1) that gives the model the evidence a human analyst would assemble by hand. Four of the panels are hillshades lit from azimuths of 45, 135, 225, and 315 degrees at a 45-degree altitude, so that no feature is lost in the shadow of a single sun angle. This use of several sun angles offsets the strong directionality of any single shaded-relief image (Bennett et al. 2012); related relief visualizations (topographic openness, Doneus 2013; local relief models, Hesse 2010; blended visualizations, Kokalj and Somrak 2019) address the same problem and could be added to the ensemble. The fifth panel is the color-coded geomorphon raster. The sixth overlays topographic contours on a hillshade. The composite and the detector's candidate list are sent to the model.


Alongside the image, the prompt carries textual context about nearby modern noise. We call this **Context-Aware Visual Correlation**. For every candidate, the detector's shield output is summarized into the prompt (for example, `Nearby Noise: irrigation ditch (15 m away)`). The model is then asked to judge, from the image, whether the feature matches the modern context or instead shows the erosion and symmetry of an ancient earthwork. This directs the model to explain away false positives rather than flag high-scoring shapes.

#### 2.5.2 Model and serving

The reasoning model is **Qwen3-VL-30B-A3B-Thinking** (Yang et al. 2025), a mixture-of-experts vision-language model with about 30.5 billion total parameters but only about 3.3 billion active per token, fine-tuned with QLoRA. The design is deliberate: it gives the reasoning capacity of a 30-billion-parameter model at the inference cost of a 3-billion-parameter one, which is what allows the model to run on a single workstation in the field. The deployed checkpoint is the best-balanced point of the fine-tuning history (Appendix A; the released weights carry the tag `terrallm-v91`). On our internal benchmark of thirteen terrain-reasoning tasks, it scores 85.3% overall and passes every category threshold. The model is served behind an OpenAI-compatible API; an interpretation takes tens of seconds per candidate against about two seconds for the deterministic detector, which is why the model is reserved for shield survivors (serving configuration in Appendix B.5).

For a subset of candidates, we also performed a **manual cross-check with Gemini 3 Pro**, supplying the same composite image and prompt through that model's interface and comparing its reading to the fine-tuned model's. This cross-check is a manual quality control step performed by the analyst. It is not part of the automated pipeline.

#### 2.5.3 How the model was built: training data, fine-tuning, and zero-shot transfer

The interpretation layer adapts a general vision-language model to terrain reasoning rather than training a detector from scratch. Fine-tuning supplies the vocabulary and priors of a terrain analyst, not new perception. The model is trained only on high-resolution New York State LiDAR: a program-generated corpus of about 40,000 geomorphon-and-hillshade images paired with questions, adapted with QLoRA (Dettmers et al. 2023; Hu et al. 2021) on a single workstation-class accelerator, so the model is retrainable and deployable in the field. It uses no Yazoo Basin or other Delta data. Its application here is therefore **zero-shot**: the model learns the shape vocabulary of earthworks rather than region-specific templates, and it can be pointed at a new river valley without a local labeling effort, which is the property that scales when labeled examples cannot be produced. Appendix A gives the full build detail (corpus, hyperparameters, checkpoint history, per-category benchmark scores) and two cautions that bear repeating: the internal benchmark originates in a broader military terrain-analysis project and does not test archaeological detection, and the recall and shield results of Sections 3.1–3.5 do not pass through the model. The model's direct test is Section 3.6.


## 3. Results and validation

### 3.1 Recall against the reference mound set

We assembled a reference set ("gold list") of 35 mounds in the Yazoo Basin, identified by their **Lower Mississippi Survey grid-quadrat designations** (for example, 18-L-7 and 21-M-11; the survey-grid system of Phillips, Ford, and Griffin 1951 and Phillips 1970). The coordinates are legacy positions compiled from prior surveys, with positional accuracy that is not documented uniformly. For each mound, the search procedure downloads a DEM, computes geomorphons, runs the single-scale detector of Section 2.2, and records whether any candidate falls within 30 m of the reference coordinate.

Agreement with legacy coordinates is not the same thing as recall, so we hand-corrected the reference set before scoring: one of us (CPL) marked the center of the overall feature at every site on registered hillshade and local-relief layers, flagged features not visible in the bare-earth surface, and recorded the shift from the catalog point (protocol in Appendix B.1). The median catalog error is 2.9 m, but the tail is long, reaching 199 m at one site. One mound (22-N-11_m3) has no visible expression and is excluded, leaving 34.

We report the evaluation in the project's analytical CRS, UTM Zone 15N, so that all distances are in true ground meters. Against the hand-corrected centers, the detector recovers **28 of 34 mounds (82%) at a 30 m tolerance**, at a median centroid-to-center offset of 6.1 m (Table 1). Recall falls at tighter tolerances, to 44% at 10 m; part of that spread is a convention difference, because the detector reports the centroid of its geomorphon peak mask, which on a large mound need not coincide with the geometric center. The correction also exposed something a tolerance analysis cannot: two of the original catalog's apparent hits were matches to erroneous coordinates that lie 199 and 107 m from the actual mounds, so agreement with a bad coordinate can look like recall (per-site detail in Appendix B.1). The corrected centers are analyst judgments on the relief imagery, not ground truth, so the offset statistics confirm that detections land on the marked feature rather than establishing accuracy against survey-grade positions. Because a centered-window containment metric can also be inflated by chance (the caution Section 3.7 makes explicit), we ran the identical frozen protocol at 135 decoy points 500 m from the corrected centers: the decoys score 20% at the 30 m tolerance and 2% at 10 m, so recall exceeds its chance floor at every tolerance, by a factor of four at 30 m and twenty at 10 m. Recall also stratifies by relief: 19 of 22 mounds above 1.5 m of local relief are recovered, 8 of 11 between 0.5 and 1.5 m, and 1 of 1 below 0.5 m; the reference set holds a single sub-half-meter mound, so recall on the faintest class the introduction emphasizes is essentially untested by this set. Appendix B.1 gives the decoy and relief detail, the tolerance curve against the original catalog, the six mounds missed at 30 m, and the matching protocol.

One point bounds the interpretation of these numbers. The recall measures the **geomorphon detector**, the detection stage of the workflow, against a catalog of known mounds. It is a measure of sensitivity, not of end-to-end precision. The fraction of *reported* candidates that are real is a separate and harder question. We take it up in Section 3.2.

### 3.2 The role of the shield, and what is not yet measured

The reference-set evaluation stresses **recall**. Every entry is a real mound, so it cannot measure how many false positives the shield removes, and it cannot provide a **precision** number, the fraction of reported candidates that are real across a full regional scan. A precision evaluation requires a labeled regional sample with both mounds and confirmed modern features, and the production of that sample is in progress. During validation we also run each candidate through the shield and record whether screening would have dropped a true mound, to watch for the failure mode in which the shield discards real features. What we can report now is the shield's **rejection rate** on a controlled regional scan (Section 3.3), and low precision on a candidate stream does not undermine high recall on known mounds; they are different experiments.

As a first step toward precision, we built a review instrument that presents each surviving candidate as a multi-view relief panel and records a per-candidate verdict, and we ran a desk review of the eighty survivors from the Jaketown regional scan (Section 3.3). This was a visual screen, not a field determination, and the tile is adversarial: it spans the modern town and its engineered floodplain. The survivors are dominated by the compact but modern features of mechanized agriculture and flood control (field-corner berms, drainage furrows, paired spoil ridges, a borrow pit, relict natural levees), and few read as convincing mounds. They survive because they are compact enough to clear the linearity test and sit in Cultivated Crops or Woody Wetlands rather than on Developed land, so neither active shield layer fires. The lesson is that the shield's 71% rejection rate reflects the removal of modern fabric, not archaeological purity. Applying a simple relief-and-compactness gate to the eighty survivors (local relief at least 0.5 m, a compact 100–5,000 m² footprint) leaves **four**; this is neither a precision measurement nor an independent validation (Appendix B.2), but it establishes that a two-parameter filter the shield does not yet contain performs most of the mound-versus-modern separation on this tile. We recommend adding it as a standard layer, tuned to the regime being screened. A genuine precision figure still requires a ground-truthed, non-adversarial evaluation (Section 5).

### 3.3 Case study: a controlled Jaketown rescan

A regional scan of the Jaketown complex (Ford, Phillips, and Haag 1955) illustrates both the pipeline and the reporting discipline the shield is meant to support. An earlier draft described a curated result of four confirmed candidates filtered from 42 peaks; that result was not reproducible from the run records, and we correct it here.

The original scan produced 200 raw candidates, and inspection of its per-candidate records exposed the core problem: the land-cover layer had contributed nothing, because the external service it queried had begun refusing anonymous requests and every lookup had been failing without notice. We repointed the layer at a token-free public service and hardened the shield so that an unavailable layer is recorded as incomplete context rather than treated as a pass (Appendix B.5).

We then reran the scan over the same area with the functional land-cover layer (Figure 7). The detector produced 277 raw candidates and the shield **rejected 197 of them (71%)**: 189 on developed land, 2 on open water, and 6 as linear footprints. Eighty candidates survived as KEEP or FLAG. The result is consistent with the geography: the tile spans the modern town, so the majority of geomorphon "peaks" are structures and pads, which are exactly what the land-cover layer is meant to remove.

The modern-feature (canal and levee) layer was inactive for this run because no noise map was supplied, so all candidates are recorded as having incomplete context.](figures/fig_jaketown_shield_funnel.png)

Two cautions apply. The 71% figure is a **rejection rate**, not a precision figure: the 80 survivors are not ground-truthed. And the modern-feature layer was inactive because no noise map was supplied, which is why every candidate is marked as having incomplete context. What the rescan establishes is that the shield now acts on its context rather than only recording it.

### 3.4 Case study: Lake George / Holly Bluff

Lake George (the Holly Bluff site, 22-Yz-557; Williams and Brain 1983) is one of the largest Mississippian centers in the basin, anchored by a platform mound published at about 17 m in height. It is also where the limits of public bare-earth LiDAR are sharpest, and reporting it required correcting our own earlier run.

An earlier draft described a scan that "resolved the principal Lake George monument" as a 3,519 m² feature with 2.94 m of relief. On audit, that scan was centered on the wrong place: its area of interest covers the modern town of Holly Bluff, about 8 km northeast of the Lake George mound group. The feature reported as "the principal monument" was an unrelated rise near the town, and we correct this here.

We re-ran the scan over the actual mound site, a 1.9 × 1.8 km (true meters) area of interest centered on the published Mound A coordinate, with the same geomorphon detector and shield. The result is a clean negative. **The mound is not present in the 3DEP bare-earth surface.** Across the whole area of interest the surface relief is about 2 m, the tallest feature the detector found rises 0.42 m, and the candidates nearest the mapped Mound A location are sub-decimeter depressions in forest classed as Woody Wetlands. There is no 17 m platform, and nothing that resembles one (Figure 8).

The cause is canopy. Mound A stands under mature bottomland forest, and the seamless 3DEP product over this stand returns a near-flat bare-earth surface: either too few pulses reach the ground to reconstruct the mound, or the ground classification removed the structure along with the vegetation. Either way, the bare-earth raster the pipeline consumes does not contain the mound. This is a property of the input, not a failure of the detector: a geomorphon classifier cannot recover relief that is absent from the DEM it is given.

Two points bound the method. Recovery of canopy-covered mounds depends on the product and its processing: a purpose-built ground filter applied to the raw point cloud has recovered partial relief at this same mound, on the order of 6 m, so the failure is specific to the seamless 3DEP product, not to LiDAR in general. And Lake George has an open-terrain counterpart: Winterville Mound A (22-Ws-500; Brain 1989), about 12 m high, is resolved in the same 3DEP product (Figure 9). The system screens the open, agriculturally exposed majority of the basin well and is blind to the forested remainder until a canopy-penetrating surface is supplied.

### 3.5 Case study: agricultural-island protection in cropland

Where Lake George shows the method's limit, a third scan shows the shield doing the work it was designed for. We ran the pipeline over a tract of mechanized cropland about 1.3 km on a side (true meters) in the central basin that contains a reference mound (21-M-11). This is the setting in which the agricultural-island rule matters most. Here, a compact prehistoric rise and the linear furrows, turn-rows, raised-field roads, and canal spoil banks of modern farming occupy the *same* National Land Cover Database class (Cultivated Crops). A land-cover filter alone cannot separate them. Only shape can.

The scan produced 163 candidates. The shield kept 54, flagged 39, and rejected 70, and the split falls along shape. Fifty candidates in Cultivated Crops were kept as compact islands; one, a 572 m² rise of 1.65 m relief and aspect 1.47, has the low, round footprint of a small mound surviving in a plowed field. Candidates with aspect ratios from about 13 to 60 (plow lines, turn-rows, raised field roads, and the spoil bank flanking the bayou that crosses the tract) were rejected as linear features, and rises with aspect near 3 were flagged as probable plowing artifacts rather than kept or dropped without record (Figure 10). This is the behavior Section 2.4 specifies: within one Cultivated Crops class the shield returns KEEP for a compact mound-shape and REJECT for a levee-shape.

One caution bounds the reading. The cataloged coordinate for 21-M-11 and the nearest geomorphon candidate differ by about 168 m in true meters. This gap is consistent with positional uncertainty in legacy site coordinates rather than with a clean detection at the mapped point. We therefore read this scan as a demonstration of the shield's population-level behavior in cropland, not as a point recovery of that mound. As at Jaketown, the keep/flag/reject split measures how the context layers act on a candidate stream, not how many of the kept islands are prehistoric. That distinction also awaits ground truth.

### 3.6 The interpretation layer as a discriminator (summary; full ablation in Appendix B)

We tested the discrimination question five ways against the one ground-truthed set the region affords: Eskew's (2008) field-verified mounds and modern earthworks, 26 of her sites plus Winterville and Lake George as published monuments, 28 in all (Appendix B, Tables B1 and B3; per-arm detail in B.3). Shown the standard 160 m six-panel image alone, the model keeps most real mounds but accepts nineteen of twenty-two modern earthworks. The shield does little better on the same set: its land-cover and linearity layers reject only two of the twenty-two, because both mounds and levee-spoil piles sit as compact features in Cultivated Crops, and the OpenStreetMap proximity layer raises this only to three rejected and six flagged, held back by how few of the Delta's field drains that map records. Shape and land-cover class cannot separate a Mississippian mound from a 1970s canal-spoil pile. Adding a 600 m context view, with no retraining, produced rejection that fluctuated across three replicates (9 of 21, 3 of 20, 7 of 22), and the fourth test resolves that instability with the strongest zero-shot configuration the architecture supports: context view, mapped-feature text, a structured rubric, and nine votes per site at an explicit sampling temperature (Figure 11a). It resolves in one direction. The model keeps six of six mounds (three of the six are leveled or canopy-hidden and absent from the bare-earth data, so mound-keeping on this set rewards over-acceptance; Appendix B.3), rejects zero of twenty-two modern earthworks, and its vote shares carry no age information (Figure 11b; area under the ROC curve 0.33). The apparent context gain was vote-sampling noise around a stable modal behavior: the model is a high-sensitivity recognizer of earthwork form that accepts every field-confirmed earthwork regardless of age, and prompting does not change that. The model was built in part to help explain away false positives (Section 2.5.1), so this is a designed capability failing a direct test, and read as an age discriminator it is a null result. The staged division of labor of Section 4 is the design response rather than the design vindicated: a first, morphological stage is asked only for sensitivity, which this behavior supplies, because every feature in the negative set is a real earthwork and age is a dimension of evidence that morphology does not carry.

The fourth test bounds what prompting can do. The fifth asks whether the discriminator can be learned instead. We generated 1,400 discrimination drills from New York terrain alone: 700 negatives harvested beside OpenStreetMap-mapped canals, roads, railways, and drains, labeled modern by map proximity rather than by an annotator, and 700 synthetic eroded mounds injected into the same terrain away from mapped features (Appendix B.3). No Delta or coastal data entered training, so the zero-shot claim is preserved. Fine-tuning the same base model on these drills changes the behavior that no prompt could change. Under an identical protocol the deployed model rejects one of twenty-two modern earthworks; the discrimination fine-tune rejects eight of twenty-one, three of them with no dissenting vote (one-sided Fisher exact p = 0.009). Replicate runs reproduce the contrast (10 of 21 and 9 of 22, the same mound lost each time; pooled 27 of 64 against 2 of 44, p = 5 x 10^-6; Appendix B.3). The vote-share AUC is 0.62 against the deployed model's 0.48, but with six positives the interval is wide (bootstrap 95% CI 0.38 to 0.83; Figure 11c), so the rejection contrast, not the AUC, carries the evidence. The gain has a cost. The fine-tune keeps five of six mounds, but the one it drops, I.C. Village, is one of only three positives with measurable relief in the bare-earth data, so its recall on present-in-data mounds is two of three, and by the cost asymmetry of Section 4 its verdict must enter the pipeline as a flag for review, never a deletion. On the South Carolina set the recognition transfers (nine of ten rings) but the learned rejection does not (zero of ten decoy windows 500 m from the rings), because the drills teach one negative context, mapped modern construction, and the coastal decoys hold natural terrain outside that class. The rejection behavior is therefore trainable from universal data where it is not promptable; whether the model learned age or learned to see construction in the context view is not yet separable (Appendix B.3), and the filter is partial and typed to the contexts it was taught. No reported detection depends on the model.

### 3.7 Generalization to a different feature and region: coastal shell rings

The universal-morphology claim makes a testable prediction. If the workflow recognizes earthworks by structural combinations of geomorphon primitives rather than by a memorized landscape, it should transfer to a different feature type and a different region with no retraining. We test that on the shell rings of coastal South Carolina, a feature and setting the model never saw. A shell ring is a raised annular rim of shell and midden enclosing a lower, flat plaza (Russo 2006), which is a distinct structural combination from a compact mound.

We take Davis's (2018, 2019) catalog of ten shell-ring and mound sites in Beaufort County, South Carolina, and run the same pipeline on the same public product (full 3DEP coverage, UTM Zone 17N, no restricted or third-party data). Because rings vary in rim height and are often oval, a rigid circular template fails; a per-angular-sector detector instead requires a raised rim spanning opposite sectors around a depressed center (Appendix B.4). This is a new structural combination, not a retrained model.

The ten sites lie on the tidal coast of Beaufort County, South Carolina (Figure 12a), and the ring morphology is recoverable there in the same public product and representation (Figure 12b): the clear rings are visible as raised annuli of Ridge/Shoulder/Spur geomorphons around lower centers, in a region and feature class the pipeline never saw. That is the transfer result, and it belongs to the representation.

An automated ring detector is a different matter, and we report it with its null. The ring coordinates were hand-corrected under the protocol of Section 3.1, and the analyst judged all ten rings visible in the public product. A first hand-tuned structural detector localizes a ring within the rim-crest radius of the corrected center at four of ten sites, and within the ring footprint at seven of ten (Table B2). Those counts sound respectable until they are measured against chance: the identical frozen procedure at forty decoy points 500 m from the catalog sites scores at the same rates, because the coastal plain is dense with ring-scoring relief and the detector fires nearly everywhere, so four of ten within 20 m is what chance predicts (Appendix B.4). We report the counts, with the null, as a caution: containment-style metrics on centered windows must be checked against decoys before they are read as recall. What stands after the control is the qualitative transfer (Figure 12b), the per-site accounting (Table B2), and the finding that a working ring *detector*, as opposed to a ring-revealing representation, does not yet exist. The detector's failures are its own: the correction pass shows the rings are there.

The discrimination step does not transfer for free, which is itself informative. The shield's land-cover layer rejects none of the ring candidates, because both real shell rings and the natural coastal rises they must be told apart from sit on Wetlands and Forest, not on Water or Developed land. Detection, the universal-morphology part, generalizes across feature type and region; discrimination, the contextual part, needs a signal specific to the setting, the same lesson as Section 3.6. Read against a six-panel image of each ring, the New-York-trained model calls all five clear rings earthworks despite never seeing a shell ring or coastal terrain in training, but this must be weighted by its base acceptance rate (23 of 28 sites of every kind in Section 3.6, at which rate five of five has a probability near 0.4 by chance), so it is weak evidence, and it supports universality rather than discrimination.

## 4. Discussion

The result we most want to foreground is not the recall number itself but what it is achieved *without*. Every quantified detection in this study is produced without any labeled training examples from the Delta: the geomorphon detector is deterministic, and the interpretation layer never saw a Yazoo mound. In a discipline whose training signal is finite and cannot be manufactured, separating where a model learns from where it is applied may be the only approach that scales. A supervised detector is bounded by the number of known examples available for a region; a zero-shot, foundation-model approach is bounded instead by the supply of general terrain the world affords, which is very large. The evidence here does not prove that the fine-tuned model transfers well. Section 3.6 measures it: the model reads shape but does not separate ancient from modern earthworks until it is trained for that separation, and then only in part. What the evidence establishes is that the label-free architecture recovers real mounds it was never trained to see. For a field that has pursued automated detection through region-specific supervised training, we think that finding matters most.

The workflow also automates the first pass of large-scale survey. A regional scan that would take an analyst weeks of hillshade inspection runs in hours, and because the detector reasons about shape rather than a single illumination, it surfaces low-profile features that fixed-angle review tends to miss. Its validated core is the **sensitivity of the detector**: about four in five hand-verified mound centers at a 30 m tolerance, with the tight 22-N-13 and 22-N-9 clusters resolved into individual mounds (Section 3.1, Figure 6). Sensitivity of this kind is the property a survey tool must earn before its candidates are worth an analyst's time, and it is necessary but not sufficient, because it says nothing about precision (Section 3.2). The case studies bound the method from three sides: Jaketown shows the shield stripping the modern fabric of a town-spanning tile, the cropland scan shows it keeping compact mounds as islands while rejecting the plow lines and dredge spoil that share their land cover, and Lake George marks the outer limit, where closed canopy removes even a 17 m mound from the public bare-earth surface.

The workflow is not a replacement for the archaeologist. It is a staged pipeline, and Section 3.6 fixes what each stage can be asked to do. The first stage is morphological and must be exhaustive: it flags every earthwork-form feature, and in a landscape like the Delta many of those features are real earthworks of some age, alongside natural fluvial relief (levee remnants, point-bar ridges) that a broadened negative corpus must also learn. Calling the modern ones false positives misstates the situation. A levee-spoil pile is a true positive of a construction event outside the study's interest, and age is a dimension of evidence that shape does not carry, which is why neither the model nor the shield's shape and land-cover rules can supply it. The second stage is contextual, and its error costs are asymmetric: discarding a pre-European mound is an irreversible loss while passing a spoil pile costs a minute of later review, so the contextual layers flag rather than delete. Their measured weakness on the Eskew set is bounded by the completeness of the context data, not by the concept: where OpenStreetMap maps the canal, the shield rejects the spoil pile beside it. The third stage is manual evaluation, tractable because the first two stages concentrate the work in a domain where ground-truthing is the scarce resource. The work program for the second stage is richer context data and broader universal negative classes for the fine-tuned discriminator, whose first version raises rejection from one to eight of twenty-one without any Delta training data (Section 3.6), rather than region-specific data.

That work program rests on an asymmetry in what can be labeled, and the asymmetry deserves to be stated as a design principle. The positive class is the archaeological record: finite, incapable of enlargement, and with every example spent on training lost to evaluation, which is why the workflow trains on none of it. The negative class has the opposite character. Modern earthworks exist in unbounded supply, more are built each year, and they come with labels attached: a compact rise beside a mapped canal is canal spoil on the testimony of the map, with no annotator required, though the label is presumptive rather than guaranteed (a natural knoll beside a road passes the same proximity test), so the negative class carries map-derived label noise, estimated at roughly 4 to 12% by a 25-sample visual audit (Appendix B.3). The fifth arm of Section 3.6 runs on this asymmetry: its negatives were harvested from mapped modern construction in New York, its positives were synthesized from the universal mound form, and no archaeological site entered training. The result, a partial age filter learned at no cost to the record, shows where the supervised effort in this problem belongs. Exploiting the asymmetry by learning, rather than by rules, also moves the map to where it does the most good. The shield can consult a mapped canal only at query time, so its ceiling is the map's completeness at the site under test. The fine-tuned model consumes the map at training time instead, so at deployment it needs no map at all: its eight rejections come from the image alone, one of them at a site with no mapped feature within 300 m, and only one of the eight is a site the query-time proximity layer rejects (Appendix B.3). Whether it internalized the appearance of construction or is reading unmapped construction visible in the wide view cannot be separated at eight sites, but either way the learned filter is not capped by the map's completeness at the site under test in the way the rule-based layer is. The coastal decoys bound the claim: a filter trained on one family of negatives is typed to that family, so the negative corpus must be broadened across settings. But broadening a corpus of mapped modern construction is cheap in a way that enlarging the archaeological record is not, and a system built on this asymmetry can keep improving its rejection of what is not of interest while the record it is built to find stays untouched.

The honest accounting in Section 3 is itself a methodological point. An automated survey system accumulates components faster than it accumulates controlled evaluations of them: a pipeline can report context it never checked, a case study can be run over the wrong coordinates. We redesigned the shield to surface incomplete context, corrected the Jaketown candidate counts, and corrected a Lake George case study that had been scanned about 8 km off the actual mound site. For a method whose output is a coordinate, getting the coordinate right is not a detail. It is the whole point.


## 5. Limitations and future work

Appendix C states each limitation in full, with the numbers behind it. The boundaries that matter most are these.

**Age discrimination is measured and remains unsolved (Section 3.6).** Neither shape nor the shield's active rules separate pre-European from modern earthworks, and prompting does not change the model. The discrimination fine-tune raises rejection to 8 of 21 at a vote-share AUC of 0.62, at the cost of one present mound, and its learned rejection does not transfer to the coastal decoys. The paths forward are richer context data and a broader universal negative class, not region-specific data.

**Precision is unmeasured.** We report recall and rejection rates, not field-verified precision, and no candidate reported here is field-verified. A desk screen suggests precision is low on modern-dominated tiles, the shield lacks the positive-relief-and-compactness gate that would carry much of the remaining separation (Section 3.2), and the surviving-candidate densities (roughly 55 per km² in cropland) imply a basin-scale review queue that makes second-stage filtering the binding constraint (Appendix C). A labeled, non-adversarial, ground-truthed evaluation is in preparation.

**The evaluated detector is a single-scale approximation.** The multi-scale, persistence-scored form the engine implements is not yet in the detection path, and the single-scale 0.3 m flatness floor may suppress the sub-0.5 m mounds we emphasize; the reference set contains only one mound in that class (recovered), so recall on the faintest features is untested (Appendix B.1, B.5). A local-relief baseline recovers 22 of 34 mounds against the geomorphon detector's 28 of 34, so the representation, not the protocol, carries the margin, and the decoy floor for the whole protocol is 20% at 30 m against the detector's 82% (Appendix B.1).

**The inputs bound the method.** Closed canopy removes even a 17 m mound from the public bare-earth product (Section 3.4). The reference coordinates carry legacy error that hand-correction exposed (median 2.9 m, maximum 199 m), and two apparent hits were artifacts of that error (Section 3.1). The land-cover and modern-feature layers depend on external services and per-area map extraction whose completeness is not quantified.

**Zero-shot transfer is the design's central bet and its least-validated capability.** It is measured on a proxy benchmark (76.2% on that category), not on Delta ground truth, and the case studies are demonstrations of shield behavior rather than precision measurements. Ground-truthing the Jaketown and cropland survivors and a canopy-penetrating reacquisition at Lake George are the next steps.

## 6. Conclusion

We have described and evaluated a reproducible workflow for screening bare-earth LiDAR for earthen mounds in a heavily modified alluvial landscape, and we have tried to report it as we ran it. It has two measured strengths: a geomorphon detector that recovers about 4 in 5 hand-verified mound centers within a 30 m tolerance in true meters, and an auditable context shield that separates compact prehistoric shapes from the linear and developed fabric of the modern Delta. We tested age discrimination five ways in Section 3.6. Neither shape, read by the model, nor the shield's active rules separate pre-European from modern earthworks, and under the strongest zero-shot protocol the model accepts every earthwork on the set regardless of age. Fine-tuning on discrimination drills built from New York terrain alone recovers a partial rejection signal that no prompt could elicit, at the cost of one of the three mounds with measurable relief in the data and without transfer to a second region, so the discriminator is trainable and not yet general. We present that as the designed division of labor rather than a defect: the morphological stage flags earthworks of any age at high sensitivity, contextual screening marks likely modern ones for review where the context data exists, and manual evaluation of the concentrated candidate list decides. The boundaries are stated in plain terms. Precision on new candidates is not measured here, no candidate is field-verified, the evaluated detector is a single-scale variant of the multi-scale method, and closed canopy can remove even a large mound from the public bare-earth product. Turning archaeological screening into a reproducible pipeline is feasible, and the pace of landscape change in the Delta makes it more needed each year. The remaining agenda includes a precision evaluation on a representative labeled tile, integration of the multi-scale detector, a broader universal negative corpus for the discriminator, and field verification.

The repository's reproduction guide maps each result to the script that generates it and records the coordinate system used for each (Section 2.1).

In keeping with archaeological data-sensitivity norms, the repository contains coordinates only for already-published public monuments (Winterville, Lake George / Holly Bluff, and Jaketown, each with a citation). The 35-mound reference set used for the recall evaluation (Section 3.1) draws on restricted Lower Mississippi Survey grid-quadrat coordinates and is **not** distributed. Qualified researchers may obtain it from the authors and supply it to the validation scripts through a documented environment variable. We ask that users of the code honor the same policy and refrain from contributing unpublished site locations.

The fine-tuned vision-language interpretation layer is a QLoRA adapter over Qwen3-VL-30B-A3B-Thinking, trained zero-shot on New York State LiDAR. It is released separately as model weights via Hugging Face, linked from the repository. It is required only for the interpretation-layer ablation (Section 3.6; Appendix B, Table B1). ## Appendix A. Interpretation layer: training corpus, fine-tuning, and benchmark detail

This appendix gives the full build details summarized in Section 2.5.3. None of it measures the interpretation layer's contribution to mound detection (Section 3.6). It is provided for reproducibility.


![**Figure A1. Multi-view ensemble.** The six-panel composite supplied to the model for each candidate: four hillshades at different sun azimuths, the geomorphon classification, and a contour overlay. Multiple illumination angles reveal subtle relief and erosion texture that a single hillshade hides.](figures/fig2_multiview_ensemble.png)

**Training data.** The fine-tuning corpus is built from high-resolution New York State LiDAR (the public NYSGPO collections). New York offers varied, noisy terrain that forces the model to learn the geometric difference between natural and cultural relief rather than memorizing a single landscape. That terrain includes glacial drumlins and eskers, historic cellar holes, quarry pits, and military earthworks. No Yazoo Basin or other Delta data is used in training. The corpus is assembled by program. A DEM tile is reduced to geomorphons and hillshades, a deterministic routine reads off the ground-truth landform, and the pair is written as an image-plus-question training example. Three kinds of example dominate. *Geomorphon drills* (tens of thousands of items) present a hillshade-and-geomorphon composite and ask the model to name the dominant landform, balanced across all ten geomorphon classes. These were added to improve geomorphon classification accuracy, the competence on which everything downstream depends. *Multi-scale feature-identification* examples ask the model to map a morphological primitive at a given scale to a feature interpretation (for example, a linear Valley to a drainage channel, a flat-topped Peak to a platform). *Anti-forgetting replay* examples preserve the base model's general visual and reasoning ability so that domain adaptation does not erode it. The production checkpoint is fine-tuned on a balanced mixture of about 40,000 such examples; an earlier, dense model generation was trained on a far larger corpus (on the order of a million examples), and a central finding of our checkpoint history (below) is that the smaller balanced mixture, targeted at the weakest competence, was the more effective recipe.

**Fine-tuning method.** Adaptation uses QLoRA (Dettmers et al. 2023), which combines 4-bit quantization with low-rank adaptation (LoRA; Hu et al. 2021): the base model is loaded in 4-bit precision (NF4 with double quantization, bf16 compute) and frozen, and small low-rank adapter matrices are trained on top of it. We use a LoRA rank of 64 and an alpha of 128 with 0.05 dropout, and we freeze the vision tower so that only the language-and-reasoning pathway is adapted. Training runs for a single epoch at a learning rate of 1×10⁻⁵ (cosine schedule, brief warmup) with an effective batch size of 16 (a per-device batch of one with sixteen-step gradient accumulation) and a 1,024-token context. The entire run fits on a single workstation-class accelerator with unified memory, which is the point: the system is meant to be retrainable and deployable in the field, not only in a data center. QLoRA makes this feasible by training a few hundred million adapter parameters rather than all 30 billion of the base model.

**Checkpoint history and what it measures.** We track progress on an internal benchmark comprising 13 terrain-reasoning tasks grouped into 5 categories: geomorphon classification, tactical/landscape assessment, recovery-and-detection search, zero-shot transfer to unseen terrain, and domain terminology. The tasks are scored by expected-keyword recall and averaged by category. The trajectory shows that building this model was an exercise in trading capabilities against one another rather than climbing a single number. A first aligned checkpoint scored about 42% overall. A dense 32-billion-parameter "Thinking" variant, trained on the full ~1-million-example corpus, reached 84.1% overall, with geomorphon classification near 71%. Moving to the mixture-of-experts base and retraining on the 40,000-example balanced mixture produced an intermediate checkpoint. It pushed geomorphon classification up to about 85% but cut zero-shot transfer to about 74%. That is the wrong trade for a system whose premise is transfer to an unseen basin. We therefore rebalanced the mixture by adding zero-shot and recovery examples and trained a final run. From its checkpoints, we selected the deployed model because it is the best-balanced checkpoint in the run. It scores **85.3%** overall and passes every category threshold at once. The per-category scores are geomorphon classification **72.7%** (the only checkpoint in the run above the 70% bar that also keeps transfer high), zero-shot transfer **76.2%**, recovery search **96.4%**, tactical assessment **94.4%**, and domain terminology **90%**. On a mission-weighted metric that prioritizes the capabilities this application depends on (zero-shot transfer: 0.30, recovery: 0.25, tactical: 0.20, geomorphon: 0.15, terminology: 0.10), it scores **85.8%**. We chose it over later checkpoints that edged higher on the transfer-weighted metric but let geomorphon classification fall below the 70% threshold. For a pipeline whose first stage is a deterministic geomorphon detector, a model that has lost its grip on geomorphon vocabulary is the wrong trade.

Three cautions about this benchmark are owed to the reader. It originates in a broader **military terrain-analysis** project, and its five categories reflect that origin. Several test general or tactical terrain competence rather than archaeological mound detection, and the terminology category is a single task quoted at 90%. The thirteen tasks are scored based on expected keyword recall. This is a coarse proxy. It credits the presence of the right vocabulary without checking the reasoning behind it, and it can penalize a correct synonym. With only thirteen tasks, it cannot support fine distinctions between checkpoints, and small differences should not be over-read. The scores measure the model against this internal rubric, not against Delta earthworks. They indicate general terrain-reasoning competence, not performance in archaeological detection. The benchmark does not test detection. The ablation in Section 3.6 provides that test. The per-checkpoint scoring artifacts are released with the project so the numbers can be audited against the code.

## Appendix B. Supplemental methods, statistics, and the interpretation-layer ablation

This appendix holds the detailed statistics summarized in Sections 3.1, 3.2, and 3.6. None of it is required to follow the main argument; it is provided for completeness and reproducibility.

### B.1 Recall: tolerance curve, matching protocol, and missed mounds (Section 3.1)

**Coordinate correction protocol.** Every reference coordinate (the 35 Yazoo mounds and the 10 South Carolina shell rings of Section 3.7) was hand-verified before scoring. A web interface presents each site over a 500 m window in three registered layers (multidirectional hillshade, wide local relief, fine local relief); the analyst (CPL) clicks the center of the overall feature, flags sites with no visible expression, and can attach notes. Clicks are converted to UTM and WGS84 and saved with the shift from the catalog point; originals are never modified. Catalog error across the 44 visible sites: median 2.9 m, mean 16.0 m, maximum 198.6 m (19-M-2), with 21-M-1 at 107.3 m and 22-M-1 at 38.0 m. One mound, 22-N-11_m3, has no visible expression and is excluded from scoring.

**Recall against corrected centers** (Table 1): 44% at 10 m (15/34), 56% at 15 m (19/34), 68% at 20 m (23/34), 76% at 25 m (26/34), and 82% at 30 m (28/34); the 28 recovered offsets have median 6.1 m, mean 10.3 m, maximum 29.3 m. The detector is deterministic, so a repeat run on re-fetched DEMs returns the same result; this establishes pipeline determinism, not independent robustness. For comparison, agreement with the uncorrected catalog was 89% at 30 m (31/35, median 5.1 m); the difference is instructive. Two apparent catalog hits were spurious: at 19-M-2 and 21-M-1 the detector matched features 5 and 18 m from catalog points that lie 199 and 107 m from the actual mounds, and those matches sit 48 and 35 m from the true centers. One catalog miss (22-N-11_m3) was a data absence, not a detector failure. The six mounds missed against corrected centers are 22-M-1 (54 m), 22-N-13_m5 (51 m), 19-M-2 (48 m), 22-N-11_m2 (41 m), 22-N-21_m2 (41 m), and 21-M-1 (35 m); the first two are low-profile, 22-N-11_m2 sits in a tight cluster where a neighbor dominates the ranking, and 22-N-21_m2 is a borderline candidate that crossed the tolerance when the window recentered. The matching protocol is lenient: for each mound a 300 m tile is centered on the (corrected) point, the detector returns up to ten candidates, and a hit is scored if any of them falls within the tolerance. This is a best-case, centered-window sensitivity test rather than detection within an uncurated regional scan; a stricter single-best-candidate rule would make it harder. A residual convention difference also remains: the detector reports the centroid of its geomorphon peak mask, not the geometric center the analyst marks, which contributes several meters of offset on larger mounds.

**Decoy control.** Applying the Section 3.7 standard to the flagship recall: the frozen matching protocol (300 m centered window, up to ten candidates, nearest-candidate distance to window center) was run at four 500 m cardinal offsets from every corrected center, 135 viable decoy windows after skipping water and no-data (`scripts/yazoo_decoy_control.py`). Decoy hit rates: 2% at 10 m, 7% at 15 m, 10% at 20 m, 17% at 25 m, 20% at 30 m. Mound recall (Table 1) exceeds the decoy floor by 22 to 4 times across the tolerance range, so the containment counts carry detection signal rather than the density of mound-like relief in the landscape.

**Recall by relief class.** Local relief at each corrected center (99th-percentile relief within 40 m against a 45 m Gaussian surface; `scripts/mound_relief_strata.py`) joined to the recall run gives: above 1.5 m, 19 of 22 recovered; 0.5 to 1.5 m, 8 of 11; below 0.5 m, 1 of 1. The reference set contains a single mound below 0.5 m, so the set does not test the faintest class; the per-mound values are released (`mound_relief_strata.csv`).

**Baseline comparison.** To test whether the geomorphon representation carries the result, we ran a simple local-relief baseline under the identical protocol against the corrected centers: the DEM minus a 40 m Gaussian surface, with the ten strongest local maxima as candidates. The baseline recovers 22 of 34 mounds at the 30 m tolerance (18 at 10 m), against the geomorphon detector's 28 of 34 (15 at 10 m). The geomorphon representation contributes six additional recoveries at 30 m over a plain relief-peak detector on the same windows, while the baseline is slightly better at the tightest tolerance, consistent with relief peaks centering on summits while geomorphon masks center on whole landforms (`scripts/lrm_baseline.py`).

### B.2 The positive-relief gate on Jaketown survivors (Section 3.2)

Applying the relief-and-compactness threshold (local relief at least 0.5 m and a 100–5,000 m² footprint) to the eighty Jaketown survivors leaves four and removes 76. Two readings of that number must both be resisted. It is not a precision measurement: none of the four is ground-truthed, and the 95% reduction reflects how flat and modern the survivor pool on this adversarial tile already was, which the desk screen had shown. It is also not an independent validation when checked against that same desk screen, because the threshold and the human review are two forms of one construct, positive relief plus compactness, so their agreement is close to circular; and the agreement is in any case only partial, since of the eight survivors the review rated plausible the threshold keeps three and discards five. The gate should be tuned as an operating point rather than a fixed cutoff: a 0.5 m relief floor suits open cropland but would discard the sub-0.5 m mounds the introduction emphasizes, so the threshold must be set to the regime being screened and its interaction with the detector's own 0.3 m floor characterized. A single adversarial tile cannot settle precision either way; disentangling the explanations requires precision measured on a randomly sited, non-adversarial tile with the complete noise map and the compactness gate enabled (Section 5), anchored by field verification.

### B.3 Interpretation-layer ablation (Section 3.6)

Eskew's (2008) contour-circularity algorithm flagged a set of candidate earthworks in the northern basin; she, Lipo, and Hunt then visited them on the ground in June 2008 and sorted them into genuine pre-European mounds and modern earthworks that resemble them (levee spoil, channel clearance, field berms). That field-verified split is the discrimination a downstream interpreter must make. We assemble it as a labeled set of six field-confirmed mounds (Glover Place, Gates, I.C. Village, Lula, and the open-terrain monuments Winterville and Lake George) and twenty-two field-confirmed modern earthworks. For each site we fetch the same bare-earth DEM, build the six-panel image the pipeline serves, and ask the deployed model for a MOUND / NOT-MOUND verdict, taking a majority vote over three runs because generation at temperature zero is not fully deterministic under vLLM's batching.

The model labels four of the six confirmed mounds as mounds (Gates, I.C. Village, Lula, Winterville); it returns NOT-MOUND for Glover and Lake George. An audit of the 3DEP surface at all six positives bounds this correctly: the maximum local relief within 80 m of the coordinate is 13.2 m at Winterville, 2.7 m at I.C. Village, and 2.6 m at Gates, but only 0.06 m at Glover, 0.06 m at Lula, and 0.45 m at canopy-covered Lake George. Glover and Lula have been leveled and Lake George is canopy-hidden, so only three positives are present in the data at all. On those three the model is three for three; its NOT-MOUND verdicts at Glover and Lake George are correct readings of surfaces that contain no mound; and its MOUND verdict at leveled Lula is a further over-acceptance. Of the twenty-two modern earthworks it accepts nineteen as mounds and rejects three (Table B1). As a filter for pre-European features it is ineffective. This clarifies the architecture rather than undermining it: every feature in the negative set is a compact, mound-like earthwork, so shape cannot separate a Mississippian mound from a 1970s canal-spoil pile. The separation is contextual, not morphological, and it is the shield's task. The route to improving the model is not region-specific data, because a mound's shape is the same in any valley, but stricter use of the structural combinations and a wider range of universal non-mound shapes in training.

**The shield on the same set.** The architectural claim that age discrimination belongs to the context shield can be tested on the same 28 sites, and we test it. For each site we ran the detector, took the candidate nearest the coordinate (supplying the linearity layer's aspect ratio), queried NLCD at the point, and asked the shield for a verdict; the modern-feature proximity layer was inactive because no noise map exists for the northern basin (`scripts/shield_eskew_test.py`). The result (Table B3) is symmetric with the model's: the shield keeps all six mounds, but the land-cover and linearity layers reject only two of the twenty-two modern earthworks. Both mounds and levee-spoil piles sit as compact features in Cultivated Crops, so neither land cover nor linearity separates them. We then activated the proximity layer from open vector data, querying OpenStreetMap (canals, ditches, drains, roads, railways, dykes) within 300 m of each site through the Overpass API (`scripts/shield_eskew_proximity.py`), a fully open-source substitute for the quad-derived noise map. With all three layers active the shield still keeps all six mounds (one flagged), rejects three of the twenty-two moderns, and flags six more, so nine of twenty-two moderns are marked for review against one of six mounds. The per-site distances show the binding constraint: OpenStreetMap in the rural Delta does not map most of the agricultural drains these spoil piles sit beside (two sites have no mapped feature within 300 m), and the 25 m rejection radius is conservative. The proximity idea works where the data exists (sites 23-26 m from a mapped road or rail are rejected outright); its ceiling on this set is data completeness, which a quad-derived noise map including historical (HTMC) features may raise.

**The context arm.** The deployed pipeline supplies the model with context, and a human separates levee spoil from a mound by seeing the adjacent canal, which is invisible in a 160 m crop. A third arm therefore repeats the ablation with one change: alongside the standard six-panel detail, the model receives a 600 m context view of the same location (hillshade and local relief, candidate at center; `scripts/vlm_ablation_context.py`). No labels leak; the added input is more of the same public DEM.

Because generation is not fully deterministic under vLLM batching even at temperature zero, we ran the context arm three times. Modern-earthwork rejection was 9 of 21, 3 of 20, and 7 of 22 across the replicates (unscored sites are tied votes), pooled 19 of 63 (30%) against 3 of 22 (14%) in the image-only arm; mound recall ranged from three to five of six. The per-site record is more informative than the totals. Two to three modern earthworks are rejected in every replicate, one of them unanimously (zero MOUND votes in all nine queries): these are the sites where the wide view shows the compact pile sitting against the linear canal that produced it. About eight moderns are accepted in every replicate. The remainder, roughly half the set, flips between runs, almost always on two-to-one vote splits, and even the present-in-data mounds flip in one run. The model is at its decision boundary on these sites, and a three-vote majority is too small a sample of its output distribution to resolve them. A fourth arm was therefore designed to resolve them. Per-run results are released alongside the summary (`run1/2/3_results.csv`).

**The strongest zero-shot arm.** This arm combines every no-training improvement available to the architecture (`scripts/vlm_ablation_armc.py`): the six-panel detail and the 600 m context view, the nearest mapped modern feature from the OpenStreetMap proximity run supplied as text (the deployed pipeline's context-correlation input, exercised in the ablation for the first time), a structured rubric that requires one-line shape, context, and age checks before the verdict, and nine votes per site at an explicit sampling temperature of 0.6 in place of temperature-zero batching noise. The result is unambiguous. The model keeps six of six mounds and rejects zero of twenty-two modern earthworks, and the vote shares carry no age information: ranking sites by MOUND-vote fraction gives an area under the ROC curve of 0.33, at or below chance, with MOUND-vote shares on the moderns at least as high as on the mounds. The context-arm fluctuation was vote-sampling noise on split votes, and the suggestive context effect does not survive the stronger protocol. The model's settled zero-shot behavior on this set is that of a high-sensitivity recognizer of earthwork form: it accepts every field-confirmed earthwork, of either age, and added context views, added context text, a rubric, and more votes do not change it. Per-site results are released alongside (`armc_results.csv`).

**The learnability arm: the discrimination fine-tune.** The fifth arm tests whether the discriminator the prompting arms could not elicit can be trained, without breaking the zero-shot design. (The released weights carry the tags `terrallm-v91` for the deployed model and `terrallm-v10` for the discrimination fine-tune, and the released file names use the same tags.) The training set (`scripts/generate_discrimination_v10.py`) contains no Delta or coastal data. Negatives are 700 compact rises harvested from public 3DEP terrain beside OpenStreetMap-mapped canals, roads, railways, and drains in four boxes along New York's Erie Canal corridor (UTM 18N). Their NOT-MOUND labels are presumed from map proximity rather than assigned by an annotator, so they carry label noise: a visual audit of 25 randomly sampled negatives found 22 sitting beside clearly visible construction with bank- or fill-like relief, 2 whose association with the named feature is unclear, and 1 on natural meander-scroll terrain where the stated rationale is unsupported, an estimated contamination rate of roughly 4 to 12%. Positives are 700 synthetic eroded mounds (conical and platform, 15 to 60 m across, 0.5 to 5 m high, smoothed flanks) injected into the same terrain away from mapped features, so their labels are exact by construction. Each sample is one composite image (the 160 m six-panel detail above a 600 m context view) with the rubric prompt and a rubric-formatted response. Training used LoRA (rank 64, alpha 128) on the bf16 base model for two epochs at a learning rate of 1e-5, with a 1,000-sample replay of the original fine-tuning corpus to protect the existing recognition behavior, on the same single workstation as the original fine-tune.

Both models were then evaluated under the identical composite protocol (`scripts/vlm_eval_v10.py`; one composite image, the trained prompt, five votes per site, temperature 0.6). This arm uses five votes rather than the strongest zero-shot arm's nine because the composite image roughly doubles per-query cost; replicate runs at the same vote count are released alongside the primary run. Five votes were requested per site, but unparseable responses left two fine-tune sites with four parsed votes (including the one site scored as a tie, a 2-2 split) and eight deployed-model sites with three or four. On the Eskew set the deployed model keeps six of six mounds and rejects one of twenty-two moderns, with a vote-share AUC of 0.48, which replicates the strongest-prompt conclusion under the new protocol. The discrimination fine-tune keeps five of six mounds and rejects eight of twenty-one (the tied site is excluded from the rejection denominator; the AUC includes it at share 0.5, and excluding it gives 0.61), three of them with zero MOUND votes, and its vote-share AUC is 0.62 (bootstrap 95% CI 0.38 to 0.83; the rejection contrast is one-sided Fisher exact p = 0.009; Figure 11c). Because the context arm showed that few-vote majorities fluctuate, both evaluations were replicated: two further five-vote fine-tune runs return rejections of 10 of 21 and 9 of 22 (AUCs 0.71 and 0.64), a second base-model run returns 1 of 22 again (AUC 0.49), I.C. Village is the mound lost in every fine-tune replicate, five moderns are rejected in all three runs and thirteen in at least one, and the pooled contrast is 27 of 64 against 2 of 44 (one-sided Fisher exact p = 5 x 10^-6), with a pooled per-site mean-share AUC of 0.66 (bootstrap 95% CI 0.39 to 0.89). The contrast is stable; individual borderline sites still flip between runs. All replicate outputs are released (`data/v10_eval/`). The lost mound is I.C. Village, one of the three positives with measurable relief in the public product, so the cost is real and not an artifact of leveled sites, and recall on present-in-data mounds is two of three. The leveled Glover and Lula are still accepted, so the learned filter is not simple relief thresholding, though the same acceptances admit a less favorable reading: a MOUND class only weakly coupled to mound morphology, with the rejection behavior keying on visible modern construction in the 600 m context view rather than on age as such. An occlusion test (masking the context view) and an evaluation on real, non-synthetic positives would separate those readings. Cross-tabulating the fine-tune's eight rejections against the query-time proximity run shows the learned filter working where the map is silent: one rejected site has no OpenStreetMap feature within 300 m, four others were KEEP verdicts for the full shield, three were FLAGs, and only one is a site the shield itself rejects (`shield_eskew_proximity.csv` against `eskew_v10.csv`). On the South Carolina set under the same protocol the fine-tune keeps nine of ten corrected ring centers but rejects zero of ten land decoys. The base model under the identical protocol keeps ten of ten rings and also rejects zero of ten decoys, so the fine-tune's ring recognition is inherited from the base (the one ring it loses, Davis_32, is its own regression) and neither model rejects coastal decoys. Each decoy is the first viable 500 m cardinal offset from a ring with at least 0.5 m of window relief; decoys are not verified to contain a compact rise, so decoy acceptance mixes generic over-acceptance with the specific failure that the drills teach one negative context, mapped modern construction, and the coastal decoys hold natural terrain outside that class. Three conclusions follow. Age discrimination is learnable from universal, mechanically labeled data, so the prompting null is not a capability ceiling. The learned filter is partial and typed to the negative contexts in the drills. And the sensitivity-first architecture is unchanged: no reported detection uses the discrimination fine-tune, whose per-site results are released alongside (`data/v10_eval/`).

### B.4 Shell-ring detector and per-site results (Section 3.7)

### B.5 Detector, shield, and serving configuration (Sections 2.2, 2.4, 2.5)

**Multi-scale persistence (implemented, not yet in the detection path).** Mounds exist at different sizes. The geomorphon engine therefore computes classifications at four search radii, 2, 5, 10, and 25 m, with a per-cell persistence score. The score measures how well a cell keeps the same landform class across scales: a feature that is a Peak at 2 m and remains a Peak at 5 m and 10 m is more credible than one that appears at a single scale and vanishes at the next. In the present study this multi-scale representation is used to generate training data for the vision-language model. It is **not** wired into the detection-and-validation path, which uses the single-scale classifier of Section 2.2. Integrating multi-scale persistence into detection and re-running the Section 3 validation against it are tasks for future work; we expect it to improve recovery of the smallest and largest mounds that a single 5 m scale serves least well.

**Shield layer thresholds (Section 2.4).** Linearity: a bounding-box aspect ratio of at least 4.5 rejects the candidate; 3.0 to 4.5 flags it with a reduced score; the test is disabled for enclosure queries. Land cover: NLCD Developed classes (21 to 24) and Open Water (11) reject; compact candidates (aspect < 2.5) in Cultivated Crops (82) or Pasture/Hay (81) are kept as agricultural islands; aspect 2.5 to 4.5 in those classes flags as a probable plow line or ditch. Proximity: modern linear features are extracted from US Topo and Historical Topographic Map Collection quadrangles by optical character recognition and color-based line extraction, extended to irrigation features, borrow pits, and abandoned railroad grades; a candidate within 25 m of a mapped feature is rejected, within 50 m flagged. A distinct internal detector parameter, an aspect-ratio down-weighting at 4.0, exists inside the candidate scorer and is separate from the shield's 4.5/3.0 thresholds.

**Noise-map and service-failure handling.** The modern-feature layer depends on a noise map that must be generated per area of interest by downloading the relevant quadrangles and running optical character recognition, which has a per-image cost; the regional scanner logs a warning when it runs without one. When the land-cover service is unreachable, which happened during the Jaketown run of Section 3.3, the shield does not treat the candidate as clean: it records the layer as unavailable and marks the candidate's context as incomplete, so downstream reporting can distinguish a candidate that passed the test from one that was never tested. The land-cover layer was repointed from an Esri service that began requiring an authentication token to the token-free MRLC public WMS.

**Serving (Section 2.5.2).** The model is served with vLLM behind an OpenAI-compatible API. The adapter targets the attention projections and the mixture-of-experts router, so it is served as a LoRA module without merging. On the deployment hardware a full interpretation takes on the order of twenty to thirty seconds per candidate, against about two seconds for the deterministic detector.

## Appendix C. Extended limitations and work program

The full statement of each limitation summarized in Section 5.

**Age discrimination is measured and remains unsolved (Section 3.6, Appendix B.3).** No reported detection depends on the fine-tuned model. On Eskew's field-verified set, the image-only model accepts 19 of 22 modern earthworks and the shield's active layers reject only 2 of 22, so neither shape nor land-cover rules discriminate age. A 600 m context view appeared to help in three-vote replicates (rejection 9/21, 3/20, 7/22), but the strongest zero-shot protocol, adding mapped-feature text, a structured rubric, and nine votes per site, keeps 6 of 6 mounds, rejects 0 of 22 moderns, and shows no signal in the vote shares (AUC 0.33), so the apparent gain was vote-sampling noise. The model reads earthwork form, not age, and prompting does not change that. The proximity layer, activated from open OpenStreetMap data, rejects 3 of 22 and flags 6 more, bounded by how few of the rural Delta's field drains that map records; a quad-derived noise map with historical features may raise that ceiling. Fine-tuning against universal modern-earthwork negatives, tested as the fifth arm, raises rejection to 8 of 21 at a vote-share AUC of 0.62, at the cost of one present mound, and its learned rejection does not transfer to the coastal decoys, so the filter is partial and typed to the contexts it was taught. The remaining paths for the second stage are richer context data and a broader universal negative class, rather than region-specific data.

**The detector evaluated is a single-scale approximation.** Section 3's results use a single-scale, elevation-referenced geomorphon classifier (Section 2.2), not the multi-scale, angle-thresholded, persistence-scored method that the engine also implements and that the introduction motivates (Appendix B.5). Integrating multi-scale persistence into the detection path and rerunning the Section 3 evaluation with it are priorities. The single-scale 0.3 m flatness floor may suppress the sub-0.5 m mounds we emphasize. We expect the multi-scale form to change the recovery of the faintest features and most likely improve it.

**Baseline comparison (measured).** A local-relief blob detector run under the identical protocol against the corrected centers recovers 22 of 34 mounds at 30 m against the geomorphon detector's 28 of 34 (Appendix B.1), so the geomorphon representation, not the protocol, carries the margin. A comparison against an object-based rule set remains worthwhile.

**Reproducibility bookkeeping.** Different reported numbers were produced by different scripts and, at present, two coordinate systems (Section 2.1). Table 2 maps each number to its script, coordinate system, and required data, and Appendix B.5 documents the internal aspect-ratio down-weighting (4.0) that is distinct from the shield's 4.5/3.0 linearity thresholds, so the two linearity tests are not conflated.

**Shield precision is unmeasured by field truth, and a desk screen suggests it is low on modern-dominated tiles.** We report detector recall and the shield's rejection rate (71% at Jaketown), but not field-verified precision. A preliminary visual review of the Jaketown survivors (Section 3.2) found none that present as convincing mounds. The survivors are compact modern and natural features (field-edge berms, drainage and dredge-spoil ridges, borrow pits, natural levees) that clear the linearity and land-cover layers. This is a desk screen, not ground truth. It exposes a concrete gap: the shield lacks a simple positive-relief-and-compactness filter, which on the Jaketown tile does most of the mound-versus-modern separation the other layers do not, cutting the 80 survivors to 4 (Appendix B.2). We recommend adding such a layer as standard, tuned as an operating point rather than a fixed cutoff: a 0.5 m floor suits open cropland but would discard the sub-0.5 m mounds we target. Converting any of this into a precision figure requires the labeled, non-adversarial, ground-truthed evaluation that remains required and is in preparation. The review burden the shield leaves is quantifiable from the scans: the cropland tract retains 93 kept-or-flagged candidates over about 1.7 km² (roughly 55 per km²), and the adversarial Jaketown tile retains 80 over about 4.3 km². At basin scale those densities imply a manual queue in the hundreds of thousands, which is the arithmetic that makes second-stage filtering, the positive-relief gate and the discrimination fine-tune, the binding constraint on practical use.

**Land-cover dependence on an external service.** The service used by earlier runs began requiring an authentication token and was failing without notice; we repointed the layer at the token-free MRLC public WMS (Appendix B.5). The layer still depends on a remote service, and a cached or local NLCD raster would make it dependable offline.

**Noise map coverage.** The modern-feature layer is only as good as the quadrangle coverage and the optical character recognition behind it, and it must be generated per area of interest. Its recall against actual canals and levees has not been quantified.

**Catalog error is measured, and it matters.** Hand-correcting every reference coordinate against the relief (Section 3.1) put a number on the legacy catalog's error: median 2.9 m, maximum 199 m. Two apparent detector hits were artifacts of the largest errors, and one cataloged mound has no visible expression at all. Recall is therefore reported against corrected feature centers rather than catalog agreement. The residual centroid-to-center offsets (median 6.1 m; Table 1) mix detector centering with the difference between a peak-mask centroid and a geometric center, which a centroid-to-outline comparison could separate.

**Zero-shot transfer.** Transfer from New York terrain to the Delta is the design's central bet. Sections 3.6 and 3.7 now measure the model on Delta and coastal ground truth for the recognition and discrimination tasks; what remains unmeasured is detection precision and any field verification. The internal benchmark's 76.2% zero-shot category score is a proxy for general terrain competence only. Independent ground truth beyond the reference set is needed to corroborate the recall estimate.

**Canopy is a hard limit of the public bare-earth product.** The corrected Lake George rescan (Section 3.4) shows that the seamless 3DEP DTM does not contain the 17 m Mound A under closed bottomland-hardwood canopy. The method is blind to forested mounds until a leaf-off or custom canopy-penetrating surface is supplied. Recovery is product-dependent, and quantifying it across the basin's forested fraction is future work.

**Case studies remain partly preliminary.** The Jaketown rejection rate, the corrected Lake George negative, and the cropland agricultural-island scan are reported as demonstrations of shield *behavior*. None is yet a precision measurement. Ground-truthing of the Jaketown and cropland survivors, and a canopy-penetrating reacquisition at Lake George, are the next steps.
