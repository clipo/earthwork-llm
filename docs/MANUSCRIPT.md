# Toward Label-Free Archaeological Prospection: A Zero-Shot Workflow for Detecting Earthen Mounds from Bare-Earth LiDAR in the Yazoo Basin

**Authors**: C. P. Lipo, D. S. Davis, and R. J. DiNapoli

**Target Journal**: *Archaeological Prospection*

**Status**: Working manuscript. The paper's argument is that a **label-free, zero-shot** approach is a workable and scalable direction for earthwork survey. Such an approach needs no labeled examples from the target landscape. It fits settings where the finite archaeological record cannot supply the training data that supervised detectors require. We evaluate it as a sensitivity (recall) and false-positive screening study, not as a discovery report. No candidates are field-verified here. The fine-tuned vision-language interpretation layer is tested against a field-verified set (Section 3.6). It reads mound-like shapes but does not separate pre-European from modern earthworks, so it contributes description rather than discrimination. Claims are scoped accordingly.

---

## Abstract

Archaeological survey in the Yazoo Basin of the Lower Mississippi Valley is hard for four reasons. Mound relief is low, often under half a meter. The modern landscape is full of human-made features that resemble it. The area to be screened spans thousands of square kilometers. And the supervised detectors used for LiDAR survey need many labeled examples per region, which a finite archaeological record cannot supply. We describe a **label-free** workflow that uses no labeled examples from the target landscape, a zero-shot approach that scales where supervised methods do not. A geomorphon classifier reduces the DEM to landform types, a deterministic detector extracts compact candidates, and a *False Positive Shield* screens each candidate on footprint linearity, land-cover class, and proximity to mapped canals and levees, returning a KEEP, FLAG, or REJECT verdict. A fine-tuned vision-language model (Qwen3-VL-30B-A3B-Thinking, trained zero-shot on New York State LiDAR) serves as an interpretation layer. With no labeled mounds in the training set, the detector recovers 31 of 35 cataloged mounds within a 30 m tolerance, in true (UTM) meters. Tested on a field-verified set, the model reads mound-like shapes but does not separate pre-European from modern earthworks; that task belongs to the context shield. Precision on new candidates is not measured, and closed canopy can hide even a 17 m mound from the public bare-earth product. We present the workflow as a sensitivity and screening study, and as a demonstration that a label-free, zero-shot approach is a scalable path for surveying a finite archaeological record.


## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The problem of the Delta

The Yazoo Basin of northwestern Mississippi, the Delta, holds one of the densest concentrations of pre-European earthen architecture in North America. It was a center of Woodland and Mississippian settlement. It formed part of a Lower Mississippi Valley mound-building tradition whose roots reach back to the Late Archaic (Grooms et al. 2023). Its inhabitants built thousands of mounds that served as platforms, burial places, and ceremonial centers (Lipo and Dunnell 2007). The largest centers, such as Winterville and Lake George, remain prominent monuments on the ground. Even so, as Section 3.4 shows, canopy can hide a 17 m mound from an airborne bare-earth survey. Most mounds are less conspicuous. Many are conical features under a meter in height, eroded and plowed, hidden in secondary forest or standing alone in cropland.

Since the early twentieth century, the Delta has been transformed. Mechanized agriculture has leveled low relief across most of the basin. Large flood-control works by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers have reshaped the rest. Dredging for drainage and navigation produced thousands of kilometers of canals. Each canal is flanked by linear spoil banks. Once eroded and vegetated, these banks take on a shape close to that of an ancient embankment. Levees, raised roadbeds, and industrial pads contribute to the noise. This is the topographic signature of modern human activity, and any high-resolution terrain method must contend with it (Tarolli and Sofia 2016).

This combination is what makes the Delta hard. A human analyst scanning hillshade tiles for mounds faces survey fatigue and a tendency to fixate on large, obvious structures while missing low-profile features. The landscape also yields many false positives. An automated approach sensitive enough to catch a 0.3 m rise will also flag every spoil bank in its path. The cost of that sensitivity is paid later, in wasted ground-truthing.

### 1.2 Contribution

Automated detection of archaeological features from airborne LiDAR is an established and active field (Chase et al. 2010; Vinci et al. 2024). Object-based image analysis has located earthen mounds and shell rings in the Southeastern United States (Davis et al. 2018, 2019) and large earthworks elsewhere (Freeland et al. 2016). Convolutional and region-based neural networks now detect barrows, qanats, and similar structures in LiDAR-derived terrain (Verschoof-van der Vaart and Lambers 2019; Guyot et al. 2021; Soroush et al. 2020). Some of this work uses multi-source remote sensing at Mississippian centers in the Lower Mississippi Valley (Kadhim et al. 2023). These supervised methods work. They also share a dependence that is a structural obstacle for archaeology. They need tens to hundreds of labeled examples of a feature class for training, and their accuracy drops when a detector trained in one region or on one sensor is moved to another region or sensor (Guyot et al. 2021; Lambers et al. 2019). The difficulty is that the archaeological record is finite and unevenly documented. For many feature types and many regions, the labeled examples a supervised detector needs do not exist in sufficient number. Producing them is costly, sometimes destructive, and in some cases not possible (Bickler 2021). A method whose need for labeled data grows with every new landscape does not scale to a discipline in which the training signal is fixed and small. This is the situation in the Yazoo Basin. The known-mound sample is small, of uneven positional quality, and cannot be enlarged at will.

Machine learning offers a response to this problem. **Foundation models** are trained by self-supervision on large, general data sets and then applied to new tasks and domains with little or no task-specific labeling. Applying a trained model to a task or place it never saw in training is called *zero-shot* (Figure 2). Radford et al. (2021) introduced this **zero-shot transfer**, and Bommasani et al. (2021) survey the broader foundation-model program. The approach now reaches remote sensing (Kuckreja et al. 2024; Mai et al. 2024) and the detection of buried archaeological structures (Sech et al. 2023). Its value for archaeology is that it separates where a model learns from where it is applied. The training signal comes from terrain the discipline need not have excavated. The finite archaeological record is then devoted to evaluation and interpretation rather than to training.

**This paper shows that the label-free approach is workable for earthwork survey, and it reports the result in plain terms.** The workflow uses no labeled examples from the target landscape. The geomorphon detector is deterministic and trained on nothing. The vision-language interpretation layer is fine-tuned on New York State terrain and then applied to the Delta without local labels. A pipeline built this way recovers most of a catalog of known Delta mounds (Section 3.1), having seen no Delta mound in training. This is the paper's central claim. We do not claim that Delta survey is solved. We claim that a zero-shot, foundation-model approach works well enough to be worth developing, and that it is one of the few approaches that scales to a finite record. Within that approach, the workflow makes three design choices, each grounded in the literature. First, it reasons about shape rather than height. It works on geomorphons rather than raw elevation, which suits a near-level plain. Second, it automates the context screening recommended by Davis et al. (2018). It combines land-cover class, mapped modern features, and footprint linearity into a KEEP, FLAG, or REJECT shield. This follows from the finding that no single extractor is best and that shape alone cannot separate mounds from modern earthworks (Davis et al. 2018, 2019). Third, it adds a fine-tuned vision-language model as an interpretation layer for the candidates that survive screening.

We are careful about how far the demonstration reaches. We measure the label-free detector's sensitivity and the shield's screening behavior. We also measure the contribution of the interpretation layer (Section 3.6). We do not measure end-to-end precision on new candidates. That measurement requires field verification, which we identify as the next step. We describe the workflow as it is built. We distinguish between the components that run without an analyst and those that require one. We say so wherever a claim rests on a single illustrative run rather than a controlled experiment. We claim the value of a demonstrated approach with stated limits, not a finished instrument.

### 1.3 Study area

The Yazoo Basin is an elliptical alluvial plain of about 11,000 km² between the Mississippi River on the west and the bluffs on the east (Figure 1). It is flat. Slopes are under one percent in most places. Its surface records the migration of the Mississippi and Yazoo rivers as a set of abandoned channels, relict natural levees, and backswamp depressions. The archaeological record ranges from multi-mound platform centers such as Winterville and Lake George (Holly Bluff) to small, isolated conical mounds. The survey problem here is not finding height. It is separating deliberate human construction from modern disturbance on a near-level plain.

## 2. Methodology

The pipeline has four stages: data ingestion, geomorphon classification, the False Positive Shield, and AI reasoning (Figure 3). Each stage narrows the candidate set. The most expensive operation is a call to the vision-language model. It runs only on candidates that have survived the cheaper shape and context filters.

### 2.1 Data ingestion

Bare-earth DEMs are fetched from the USGS 3D Elevation Program (3DEP) ImageServer at 1 m resolution. The regional-scan pipeline processes them in Web Mercator (EPSG:3857); at the basin's latitude (~33° N) that projection inflates ground distances by about 19% and areas by about 42%, so the distances and areas quoted in the regional case studies (Sections 3.3–3.5) are in these projected units. The one exception is the recall evaluation of Section 3.1, which we recompute in the project's analytical CRS, UTM Zone 15N, to report in true ground meters; recomputing the case studies in UTM is a straightforward step that does not change their qualitative conclusions, and we flag it as a revision to make throughout. For regional work, the area of interest is tiled into 500 m squares with 50 m of overlap, and each tile is processed in turn. LiDAR coverage of the Delta derives largely from the 2009 to 2010 USACE Delta Phase 1 acquisition, which spans the major sites. Working from 3DEP delivers a consistent, gridded, bare-earth product without the per-tile point-cloud handling that raw LAZ would require.

### 2.2 Geomorphon classification

LiDAR transformed archaeology by giving bare-earth surfaces under vegetation. A single hillshade is a subjective instrument. What it shows depends on the sun angle the analyst chooses. Instead, we reduce the DEM to **geomorphons** (Jasiewicz and Stepinski 2013), a pattern-recognition classification of local landforms.

For each cell, the method casts lines of sight in eight directions. For each direction, it asks whether the terrain along that line rises, falls, or stays within a flatness tolerance. Each direction yields a value of +1, -1, or 0. The eight values form a code. The counts of ups and downs map the cell to one of ten landform classes: Flat, Peak, Ridge, Shoulder, Spur, Slope, Hollow, Footslope, Valley, and Pit (Figure 4). A cell that looks down in every direction is a Peak. One that looks up in every direction is a Pit. A linear high is a Ridge. A linear low is a Valley. In the original formulation, the flatness tolerance is an *angle* (Jasiewicz and Stepinski use 1.0°). Classification then depends on local form rather than absolute height.

We report our implementation in full, because it differs from that canonical form in two respects that a reader must know to interpret the results. The classifier that produced every detection in Section 3 is a **single-scale** line-of-sight classifier with a search radius of five cells (5 m on the 1 m DEM), and its flatness tolerance is an **elevation-difference threshold of 0.3 m** across that window rather than an angle. The angular, multi-scale form is implemented in the same geomorphon engine (below) but is not the classifier that generated the reported detections. The 0.3 m tolerance sets a practical floor for the relief the detector registers; its interaction with the sub-0.5 m mounds, as emphasized in the introduction, is a limitation we return to in Section 5.

![**Figure 4. Geomorphons.** (A) For each cell, eight lines of sight are classified as rising (+), falling (−), or level (0) using a 1.0 degree flatness threshold. (B) The eight ternary values form a code; a cell that looks down in all directions resolves to PEAK. (C) Six diagnostic landforms and their archaeological analogs. Classification depends on local morphology, not absolute elevation, which is why the method finds subtle mounds on a flat plain.](figures/fig_geomorphon_concepts.png)

This morphological framing is what gives the archaeological mapping its power. A conical burial mound resolves as a compact Peak, a platform mound as a flat-topped Peak or a Flat bounded by Shoulders, a levee or embankment as a linear Ridge, and a drainage ditch or canal as a linear Valley. The classifier is generic; the archaeological interpretation is imposed afterward.

**Multi-scale persistence (implemented, not yet in the detection path).** Mounds exist at different sizes. The geomorphon engine therefore computes classifications at four search radii, 2, 5, 10, and 25 m, with a per-cell persistence score. The score measures how well a cell keeps the same landform class across scales. A feature that is a Peak at 2 m and remains a Peak at 5 m and 10 m is more credible than one that appears at a single scale and vanishes at the next. In the present study, this multi-scale representation is used to generate training data for the vision-language model (Section 2.5.3). It is **not** yet wired into the detection-and-validation path, which uses the single-scale classifier described above. Integrating multi-scale persistence into detection and re-running the Section 3 validation against it are tasks for future work (Section 5). We expect it to improve recovery of the smallest and largest mounds that a single 5 m scale serves least well.

### 2.3 Candidate extraction

The deterministic detector converts a geomorphon raster into a short list of candidate footprints. It selects the landform classes appropriate to the query (for a mound query, Peak, Ridge, Shoulder, and Spur), labels connected components of the resulting mask, and keeps components whose area falls in a class-specific window (for example, 20 to 5,000 m² for a generic mound, 100 to 10,000 m² for a platform mound). For each surviving component, it records the centroid, the footprint area, the local height relative to surrounding terrain, and the bounding-box aspect ratio. The aspect ratio is carried forward as the linearity input to the shield. The detector returns the ten strongest candidates per tile.

### 2.4 The False Positive Shield V2

The shield is the part of the system that turns sensitivity into usable specificity. It is the part of this manuscript that is treated with the most care. Its premise is not new. Davis et al. (2018) already identified land-use context and historical map comparison as means to suppress levee- and construction-derived false positives that defeat morphology-only detection in modified landscapes. Our contribution is an *automated* form of that guidance. The layer queries the context sources by program and combines them into an auditable verdict. It adds two things. The first is a KEEP, FLAG, or REJECT logic that surfaces borderline and untestable cases rather than passing them without record. The second is a rule that protects compact "agricultural island" mounds within cultivated fields. The shield applies three independent layers and returns one of three verdicts for each candidate: KEEP, FLAG, or REJECT (Figure 5). A single decisive layer is enough to reject a candidate. Rejected candidates are dropped before any model call.

1. **Morphological linearity.** Prehistoric mounds are compact; levees, road beds, and canal spoil banks are long and thin. A footprint with a bounding-box aspect ratio of at least **4.5** is rejected as a linear feature. One with an aspect ratio between **3.0 and 4.5** is flagged, and its score is reduced, but it is retained for review. The linearity test is disabled when the active query targets geometric enclosures, which are themselves elongated.

2. **NLCD land cover.** The National Land Cover Database class at the candidate point is queried from the NLCD ImageServer. Developed land (classes 21 to 24) and open water (class 11) are modern, not prehistoric, and candidates that fall on them are rejected. V2 adds explicit protection of **agricultural islands**: compact features (aspect < 2.5) located in `Cultivated Crops` (82) or `Pasture/Hay` (81) are kept, because mounds often survive within mechanized fields. However, moderate elongation in these fields (aspect 2.5 to 4.5) is flagged as a probable agricultural artifact (plow line or ditch).

3. **Modern feature proximity.** Canals, ditches, levees, and roads are read from USGS topographic quadrangles, both the modern US Topo series and the Historical Topographic Map Collection (HTMC), using optical character recognition and color-based line extraction. V2 expands this "noise map" to include **irrigation features, borrow pits, and abandoned railroad grades**. A candidate within 25 m of such a feature is rejected as a probable spoil bank or road shoulder; one within 50 m is flagged. The HTMC layer matters because some early-twentieth-century modifications, such as logging roads, are now under tree cover and are absent from modern maps.

![**Figure 5. The False Positive Shield.** Three context layers screen every candidate and combine into a single verdict. A linear footprint, developed land or open water, or a mapped modern feature within the rejection radius is each sufficient to reject. Borderline cases are flagged and retained with a reduced score. When a layer cannot be evaluated, the candidate is recorded as having incomplete context rather than being passed without record.](figures/fig_fp_shield.png)

Two implementation details are important for honest reporting. First, the modern-feature layer depends on a noise map that must be generated for the area of interest; generating it requires downloading the relevant quadrangles and running optical character recognition, which has a per-image cost, and the regional scanner logs a warning when it runs without one. Second, when the NLCD service is unreachable, which happened during the Jaketown run reported below, the shield does not treat the candidate as clean. It records the layer as unavailable and marks the candidate's context as incomplete, so that downstream reporting can distinguish a candidate that passed the land-cover test from one that was never tested.

### 2.5 AI reasoning

The final stage passes shield survivors to a fine-tuned vision-language model for a natural-language archaeological reading. We describe this stage in full because it is a substantial part of the system as engineered. We also state at the outset what Section 3 bears out. **No reported detection number depends on the model.** The regional scans stop at the shield. We do put the model to a direct test in Section 3.6, on a field-verified set of mounds and modern earthworks. It reads mound-like shapes but does not separate ancient earthworks from modern ones. That separation is the shield's context task, not the interpreter's. What follows describes the designed and benchmarked capability whose measured contribution is reported there.

#### 2.5.1 The multi-view ensemble

Each surviving candidate is rendered as a six-panel composite image (Appendix A, Figure A1) that gives the model the evidence a human analyst would assemble by hand. Four of the panels are hillshades lit from azimuths of 45, 135, 225, and 315 degrees at a 45-degree altitude, so that no feature is lost in the shadow of a single sun angle. This use of several sun angles offsets the strong directionality of any single shaded-relief image (Bennett et al. 2012). Related relief-visualization methods such as topographic openness (Doneus 2013) address the same directionality by other means and could be added to the ensemble. The fifth panel is the color-coded geomorphon raster. The sixth overlays topographic contours on a hillshade. The composite and the detector's candidate list are sent to the model.


Alongside the image, the prompt carries textual context about nearby modern noise. We call this **Context-Aware Visual Correlation**. For every candidate, the detector's shield output is summarized into the prompt (for example, `Nearby Noise: irrigation ditch (15 m away)`). The model is then asked to judge, from the image, whether the feature matches the modern context or instead shows the erosion and symmetry of an ancient earthwork. This directs the model to explain away false positives rather than flag high-scoring shapes.

#### 2.5.2 Model and serving

The reasoning model is **Qwen3-VL-30B-A3B-Thinking** (Yang et al. 2025), a mixture-of-experts (MoE) vision-language model with about 30.5 billion total parameters but only about 3.3 billion active per token (128 experts, eight routed per token), fine-tuned with QLoRA. The MoE design is deliberate. It gives the reasoning capacity of a 30-billion-parameter model at the inference cost of a 3-billion-parameter one, which is what allows the model to run on a single workstation in the field. We describe how it was built in Section 2.5.3. The deployed checkpoint is **V9.1** (`checkpoint-1250`). On our internal benchmark of thirteen terrain-reasoning tasks, it scores 85.3% overall and passes every category threshold (Section 2.5.3 gives the per-category breakdown and the checkpoint history behind it). The model is served with vLLM behind an OpenAI-compatible API (the adapter targets the attention projections and the mixture-of-experts router, so it is served as a LoRA module without merging); on the deployment hardware, a full interpretation takes on the order of twenty to thirty seconds per candidate, against about two seconds for the deterministic detector alone, which is why the model is reserved for shield survivors.

For a subset of candidates, we also performed a **manual cross-check with Gemini 3 Pro**, supplying the same composite image and prompt through that model's interface and comparing its reading to the fine-tuned model's. This cross-check is a manual quality control step performed by the analyst. It is not part of the automated pipeline.

#### 2.5.3 How the model was built: training data, fine-tuning, and zero-shot transfer

The interpretation layer adapts a general vision-language model to terrain reasoning rather than training a detector from scratch. Fine-tuning supplies the vocabulary and priors of a terrain analyst, not new perception. Appendix A gives the full build detail: the training corpus, hyperparameters, checkpoint history, and per-category benchmark scores. Here we state only what bears on the paper's argument.

**Training data and fine-tuning.** The model is trained only on high-resolution New York State LiDAR. It uses no Yazoo Basin or other Delta data. The training corpus is a set of geomorphon-and-hillshade images paired with questions, generated by a program (geomorphon-classification drills, multi-scale feature-identification, and anti-forgetting replay). It is a balanced mixture of about 40,000 examples. Adaptation uses QLoRA (Dettmers et al. 2023; LoRA, Hu et al. 2021). The base is 4-bit-quantized and frozen, with small trained low-rank adapters (rank 64). The run fits on a single workstation-class accelerator. This is the point of the design. The model is meant to be retrainable and deployable in the field, not only in a data center.

**Deployed checkpoint.** We selected V9.1 checkpoint-1250 from a checkpoint history that trades geomorphon-classification accuracy against zero-shot transfer. It is the best-balanced point. It scores **85.3%** overall on an internal thirteen-task terrain-reasoning benchmark and passes every category threshold. We state two cautions here and give the detail in Appendix A. This benchmark originates from a broader military terrain-analysis project, is scored using keyword recall across 13 tasks, and does not test archaeological detection in the Delta. Its scores indicate general terrain competence, not detection performance. The detection test is in Section 3.6.

**Zero-shot transfer.** The model is trained only on New York terrain and applied to the Yazoo Basin with no Delta training data. This **zero-shot transfer** design has a practical motivation. Labeled training data for prehistoric mounds in the Delta is scarce and costly to produce, so a method that needs it for every new region does not scale. The model learns the shape vocabulary of earthworks rather than region-specific templates. It can then be pointed at a new river valley and produce candidates for review without a local labeling effort. Zero-shot transfer is the most demanding task we ask of the model, and on the internal benchmark, it has the most room for improvement. Transfer quality is an open area of work, not a solved problem. The recall and shield results in Sections 3.1–3.5 do **not** pass through the fine-tuned model. They are produced by the deterministic geomorphon detector, which carries no learned weights. We test the model in Section 3.6, on Eskew's field-verified mounds and modern earthworks. The finding is that the model reads as a mound-like shape but cannot distinguish pre-European from modern earthworks by shape alone. This is expected. That separation is the context shield's task, not the interpreter's.


## 3. Results and validation

### 3.1 Recall against the reference mound set

We assembled a reference set ("gold list") of 35 mounds in the Yazoo Basin. They are identified by their **Lower Mississippi Survey grid-quadrat designations** (for example, 18-L-7 and 21-M-11, the survey-grid system of Phillips, Ford, and Griffin 1951 and Phillips 1970). These are not Smithsonian trinomials, which for Mississippi begin with 22 (as in the paper's own 22-Yz-557). The coordinates are legacy positions compiled from prior surveys. Their positional accuracy is not documented uniformly, and it is large in places. Section 3.5 shows one catalog point lying about 200 m from the nearest relief. This bounds how the agreement statistics below should be read. For each mound, the search procedure downloads a DEM, computes geomorphons, runs the single-scale detector of Section 2.2, and records whether any candidate falls within 30 m of the catalog coordinate.

We report the evaluation in the project's analytical CRS, UTM Zone 15N, so that all distances are in true ground meters. Recall against a legacy catalog depends on the match tolerance, because the catalog coordinates carry their own positional error, so we report it as a function of tolerance rather than as a single number (Table 1). At a 30 m tolerance, the detector recovers **31 of 35 mounds (89%)**, at a median centroid-to-catalog offset of 5.1 m. Recall falls at tighter tolerances, from 80% at 25 m to 51% at 10 m, a decline that measures catalog positional error as much as detector centering. Appendix B gives the full tolerance curve, the four mounds missed at 30 m, and the matching protocol.

These distances are **in agreement with a legacy catalog, not positional accuracy.** Both the detected centroid and the catalog point carry error. Section 3.5 shows one catalog coordinate lying about 200 m from the nearest relief. The offset statistics confirm that detections land on the mapped feature. They do not establish sub-meter accuracy against ground truth.

One point bounds the interpretation of these numbers. The recall measures the **geomorphon detector**, the detection stage of the workflow, against a catalog of known mounds. It is a measure of sensitivity, not of end-to-end precision. The fraction of *reported* candidates that are real is a separate and harder question. We take it up in Section 3.2.

### 3.2 The role of the shield, and what is not yet measured

The reference-set evaluation stresses **recall**. Every entry is a real mound, so it cannot measure how many false positives the shield removes. To keep recall honest, we also run each candidate through the shield during validation and record whether screening would have dropped a true mound. This lets us watch for the failure mode in which the shield is too aggressive and discards real features.

What the reference set cannot provide on its own is a **precision** number, the fraction of reported candidates that are real, across a full regional scan. A complete precision evaluation requires a labeled regional sample with both mounds and confirmed modern features, and the production of this sample (including a complete noise map for the scan area) is in progress. We can, however, report the shield's **rejection rate** on a controlled regional scan, which measures how much of the raw candidate stream the context layers remove (Section 3.3).

It is worth being explicit about what precision measures and what it does not, because the two questions are easy to conflate. The validated capability of the system, demonstrated in Section 3.1, is **recall**: pointed at a landscape that contains a known mound, the detector re-finds it, consistently and to within a few meters. Precision asks the harder, complementary question of what fraction of *newly proposed* candidates, in a region not pre-known to contain mounds, are real. The two are different experiments, and low precision on a candidate stream does not undermine high recall on known mounds.

As a first step toward measuring precision, we built a lightweight review instrument. It presents each surviving candidate as a multi-view relief panel: a hillshade plus narrow- and wide-scale local relief. It records a per-candidate verdict, so survivors can be screened systematically rather than on impression. We ran a desk review of the eighty survivors from the Jaketown regional scan (Section 3.3). This was a visual screen, not a field determination. The tile is an adversarial case. It spans the modern town and its engineered floodplain, so most of what the detector finds there is, by construction, modern. On that tile, the survivors are dominated by the compact but modern features of mechanized agriculture and flood control: berms at field corners, drainage furrows, the paired spoil ridges of dredged ditches, a borrow pit, and points along relict natural levees. Few read as convincing mounds. These features survive because they are compact enough to clear the linearity test and sit in Cultivated Crops or Woody Wetlands rather than on Developed land, so neither active shield layer fires. The lesson is not that the method cannot find mounds. Section 3.1 shows it re-finds them. The lesson is that the shield's 71% rejection rate reflects the removal of modern fabric, not archaeological purity. The most useful next filter is a **positive-relief and compactness gate on the survivors themselves**, which the current shield does not yet apply.

We can put a first number on that missing filter. Applying a simple relief-and-compactness threshold to the eighty Jaketown survivors (the detector's local relief at least 0.5 m and a compact 100–5,000 m² footprint) leaves **four** and removes 76. This is not a precision measurement and not an independent validation, for reasons set out in Appendix B; what it establishes is narrow and useful. A two-parameter relief threshold, which the geomorphon-plus-land-cover-plus-linearity shield does *not* contain, performs most of the mound-versus-modern separation on this tile. That is a finding about the shield: it lacks a simple, cheap filter, which we recommend adding as a standard layer tuned to the regime being screened. A genuine precision figure still requires a ground-truthed, non-adversarial evaluation (Section 5); this exercise motivates it and does not substitute for it.

### 3.3 Case study: a controlled Jaketown rescan

A regional scan of the Jaketown complex (Ford, Phillips, and Haag 1955) illustrates both the pipeline and the reporting discipline the shield is meant to support. An earlier draft of this paper described a curated result of four confirmed candidates filtered from 42 peaks. That result was not reproducible from the run records, and we correct it here.

The original scan produced 200 raw candidates, and inspection of its per-candidate records exposed the core problem: for almost every candidate, the NLCD column read "Lookup Failed" or "Circuit Breaker Tripped." The land-cover layer had contributed nothing. We traced this to the land-cover service itself. The Esri Living Atlas NLCD ImageServer the pipeline queried now requires an authentication token and returns HTTP 499 ("Token Required") for anonymous requests, so every NLCD lookup had been failing without notice. We repointed the pipeline at the token-free MRLC public WMS, which returns NLCD class codes, and we hardened the shield so that an unavailable layer is recorded as "incomplete context" rather than treated as a pass.

We then reran the scan over the same area with the functional land-cover layer (Figure 7). The detector produced 277 raw candidates. The shield **rejected 197 of them (71%)**: 189 because they fell on developed land and 2 on open water (both via NLCD), and 6 because their footprints were linear (aspect ratio at or above 4.5, the signature of a levee or road). Eighty candidates survived as KEEP or FLAG. The result is consistent with the geography: the Jaketown tile spans the modern town and its road and building fabric, so the majority of geomorphon "peaks" the detector finds are structures and pads, not mounds, and these are exactly what the land-cover layer is meant to remove.

![**Figure 7. The shield on a controlled Jaketown rescan.** With the repaired NLCD layer active, the shield rejected 197 of 277 raw candidates (71%), almost all because they sit on developed land, plus a few linear levee or road footprints. The modern-feature (canal and levee) layer was inactive for this run because no noise map was supplied, so all candidates are recorded as having incomplete context.](figures/fig_jaketown_shield_funnel.png)

Two cautions apply. The 71% figure is a **rejection rate**, not a precision figure: the 80 survivors have not been ground-truthed, so we cannot yet say how many are real mounds. And the modern-feature layer was inactive for this run because no noise map was supplied, which is why every candidate is marked as having incomplete context. A scan with a complete noise map and field follow-up on the survivors is the immediate next step. What this rescan does establish is that the shield now acts on its context rather than only recording it.

### 3.4 Case study: Lake George / Holly Bluff

Lake George (the Holly Bluff site, 22-Yz-557; Williams and Brain 1983) is one of the largest Mississippian centers in the basin, anchored by a platform mound published at about 17 m in height. It is also where the limits of public bare-earth LiDAR are sharpest, and reporting it required correcting our own earlier run.

An earlier draft described a scan that "resolved the principal Lake George monument" as a 3,519 m² feature with 2.94 m of relief. On audit, that scan was centered on the wrong place: its area of interest (about −90.725 to −90.700 longitude, 32.812 to 32.832 latitude) covers the modern town of Holly Bluff, about 8 km northeast of the Lake George mound group, whose principal mound sits near 32.785, −90.785. The feature reported as "the principal monument" was therefore not Lake George Mound A but an unrelated rise near the town. We correct this here.

We re-ran the scan over the actual mound site, a 2.3 × 2.2 km area of interest centered on the published Mound A coordinate, with the same geomorphon detector and Shield V2. The result is a clean negative. **The mound is not present in the 3DEP bare-earth surface.** Across the whole area of interest, the surface relief is about 2 m. This is the flat backswamp of the Yazoo floodplain. The tallest feature the detector found anywhere in it rises **0.42 m**. The candidates nearest the mapped Mound A location, 120 to 165 m away, are sub-decimeter features of *negative* relief (small depressions, 38–278 m²) within bottomland-hardwood forest classed as Woody Wetlands. There is no 17 m platform, and nothing that resembles one (Figure 8).

The cause is canopy. Lake George Mound A stands under mature bottomland forest, and the seamless USGS 3DEP product over this stand returns a near-flat bare-earth surface: either too few laser pulses reach the ground through the canopy to reconstruct the mound, or the ground-classification that produced the public DTM removed the structure along with the vegetation. Either way, the bare-earth raster the pipeline consumes does not contain the mound. This is a property of the input, not a failure of the detector: a geomorphon classifier cannot recover relief that is absent from the DEM it is given.

Two points follow, and they bound the method. First, recovery of canopy-covered mounds depends on the product and its processing. A purpose-built ground filter applied to the raw point cloud has recovered partial relief at this same mound elsewhere in the project, on the order of 6 m, short of the published 17 m. The failure reported here is specific to the seamless 3DEP product, not to LiDAR in general. A leaf-off or custom-filtered acquisition is the next step for canopy-covered sites. Second, Lake George is the counterpart to an open-terrain positive control. A large mound that survives in open ground is the kind of feature the pipeline is built for. Winterville Mound A (22-Ws-500; Brain 1989) is one example. It stands about 12 m high and is resolved in the same 3DEP product (Figure 9). A large mound under closed canopy is where a public-bare-earth approach reaches its limit. A survey system for the Delta has to be read with that boundary in mind. It screens the open, agriculturally exposed majority of the basin well. It is blind to the forested remainder until a canopy-penetrating surface is supplied.

### 3.5 Case study: agricultural-island protection in cropland

Where Lake George shows the method's limit, a third scan shows the shield doing the work it was designed for. We ran the pipeline over a ~1.5 × 1.5 km tract of mechanized cropland in the central basin that contains a reference mound (21-M-11). This is the setting in which the agricultural-island rule matters most. Here, a compact prehistoric rise and the linear furrows, turn-rows, raised-field roads, and canal spoil banks of modern farming occupy the *same* National Land Cover Database class (Cultivated Crops). A land-cover filter alone cannot separate them. Only shape can.

The scan produced 163 candidates. The shield kept 54, flagged 39, and rejected 70. The split falls along shape. Compact features in cropland are kept as islands. Fifty candidates in Cultivated Crops were kept. One is an 813 m² rise of 1.65 m, with relief 1.47 and aspect 1.47. This is the low, round footprint of a small platform or conical mound surviving in a plowed field. The linear features of the field are removed. Candidates with bounding-box aspect ratios from about 13 to 60 were rejected as linear features. These are plow lines, turn-rows, raised field roads, and the spoil bank flanking the bayou that crosses the tract. Rises with aspect near 3 were flagged as probable plowing artifacts rather than kept or dropped without record (Figure 10). This is the behavior Section 2.4 specifies. Within one Cultivated Crops class the shield returns KEEP for a compact mound-shape and REJECT for a levee-shape.

One caution bounds the reading. The cataloged coordinate for 21-M-11 and the nearest geomorphon candidate differ by about 200 m. This gap is consistent with positional uncertainty in legacy site coordinates rather than with a clean detection at the mapped point. We therefore read this scan as a demonstration of the shield's population-level behavior in cropland, not as a point recovery of that mound. As at Jaketown, the keep/flag/reject split measures how the context layers act on a candidate stream, not how many of the kept islands are prehistoric. That distinction also awaits ground truth.

### 3.6 The interpretation layer as a discriminator (summary; full ablation in Appendix B)

We tested the fine-tuned model against the one ground-truthed set the region affords: Eskew's (2008) field-verified mounds and modern earthworks (Appendix B). The model keeps most real mounds but accepts nineteen of twenty-two modern earthworks as mounds, so as a filter for pre-European features it is ineffective. This is expected. Every modern earthwork is a compact, mound-like earthen feature, so shape cannot separate a Mississippian mound from a 1970s canal-spoil pile. That separation is contextual, not morphological, and it is the shield's task, not the interpreter's. The model contributes description, not discrimination, and no reported detection depends on it.

### 3.7 Generalization to a different feature and region: coastal shell rings

The universal-morphology claim makes a testable prediction. If the workflow recognizes earthworks by structural combinations of geomorphon primitives rather than by a memorized landscape, it should transfer to a different feature type and a different region with no retraining. We test that on the shell rings of coastal South Carolina, a feature and setting the model never saw. A shell ring is a raised annular rim enclosing a lower, flat center, which is a distinct structural combination from a compact mound.

We take Davis's (2018, 2019) catalogue of ten shell-ring and mound sites in Beaufort County, South Carolina, and run the same pipeline on the same public product. The USGS 3DEP ImageServer has full coverage there, in UTM Zone 17N, so no restricted or third-party data is needed. The DEM is reduced to the same geomorphons. Because rings vary in rim height (so they appear as partial arcs) and are often oval rather than circular, a rigid circular template fails; a per-angular-sector detector instead finds the raised radius in each of twenty sectors, keeps a candidate only if the rim spans opposite sectors and encloses a depressed center, and searches a window around the catalogue coordinate. This is a new structural combination, not a retrained model.

The ten sites lie on the tidal coast of Beaufort County, South Carolina (Figure 11a), and the ring morphology is recoverable there in the same public product and representation (Figure 11b). A shell ring is 20 to 120 m across, so the catalogue point marks a spot on or beside the feature, not its center; a detection is a hit when the point falls within the detected ring footprint. On that measure, the catalogue point falls within a detected ring for **seven of ten sites** (Figure 11c). The offset of the point from the ring center scatters in direction with no coordinate-system bias, which confirms genuine marking variation rather than a projection error, the same positional uncertainty seen in the Yazoo catalogue. Two honest limits bound the result and parallel the mound work. About half the coordinates have no recoverable ring in public 3DEP, being tidal-marsh-obscured or destroyed, which is the coastal analog of the canopy that hides Lake George; and a first hand-tuned ring detector still mis-localizes some clear rings, so a production ring detector remains future work.

The discrimination step does not transfer for free, which is itself informative. The shield's land-cover layer rejects none of the ring candidates, because both real shell rings and the natural coastal rises they must be told apart from sit on Wetlands and Forest, not on Water or Developed land. Detection, the universal-morphology part, generalizes across feature type and region; discrimination, the contextual part, needs a signal specific to the setting. This is the same lesson as Section 3.6: morphology finds earthworks, and the shield, not the morphology, does the harder filtering. Read against a six-panel image of each ring, the New-York-trained model calls all five clear rings earthworks despite never seeing a shell ring or coastal terrain in training, further evidence that it responds to the ring morphology; but it over-accepts as in Section 3.6, so it supports universality, not discrimination.

(b) The shell-ring structural signature at site 38BU0300 in public USGS 3DEP bare earth (UTM 17N): a raised annular rim of Ridge/Shoulder/Spur geomorphons enclosing a lower, flat center, captured by the same universal representation used for the Yazoo mounds. ## 4. Discussion

The result we most want to foreground is not the recall number itself but what it is achieved *without*. Every quantified detection in this study is produced without any labeled training examples from the Delta. The geomorphon detector is deterministic, and the interpretation layer never saw a Yazoo mound. That a label-free pipeline recovers most of a known-mound catalog is the proof of concept for the approach this paper argues for. In a discipline whose training signal is finite and cannot be manufactured, separating where a model learns from where it is applied is not just convenient. Where the labeled examples do not exist, it may be the only approach that scales. A supervised detector is bounded by the number of known examples available for a region. A zero-shot, foundation-model approach is bounded instead by the supply of general terrain the world affords, which is very large. The evidence here does not prove that the fine-tuned interpretation model transfers well. Section 3.6 measures it, and the answer is that it reads shape but does not separate ancient from modern earthworks. The evidence establishes that the label-free architecture meets the first bar. It recovers real mounds it was never trained to see. For a field that has pursued automated detection through region-specific supervised training, we think that finding matters most.

The workflow also automates the first pass of large-scale survey. A regional scan that would take an analyst weeks of hillshade inspection runs in hours. Because the detector reasons about shape rather than a single illumination, it surfaces low-profile features that fixed-angle review tends to miss. Its validated core is the **sensitivity of the detector**. At a 30 m tolerance, it recovers about nine in ten cataloged mounds, in true meters, and it resolves the individual mounds within the tight 22-N-13 and 22-N-9 clusters (Section 3.1, Figure 6). We are careful not to overstate this. Recall falls at tighter tolerances (Table 1). The metric is agreement with an imperfect legacy catalog rather than positional truth. The detector run here is the single-scale form of Section 2.2, not the multi-scale form the engine also implements. Sensitivity of this kind is the property a survey tool must earn before its candidates are worth an analyst's time and, later, a field team's time. It is necessary but not sufficient, as Section 3.2 stresses, because it says nothing about precision.

The case studies then show how the shield handles that sensitivity. They are demonstrations of behavior, not yet precision measurements, and they bound the method from three sides. Jaketown shows the shield stripping the modern fabric of a town-spanning tile, and the survivor screen shows where a positive-relief gate is still needed. The cropland scan shows it doing the harder job the method is meant to do. It keeps compact mounds as islands in active fields while rejecting the plow lines and dredge spoil that share that land cover. Lake George marks the outer limit, where closed canopy removes even a 17 m mound from the public bare-earth surface.

The workflow is not a replacement for the archaeologist. It is a prioritization engine. Its output is a ranked, screened list of candidates. Where the interpretation layer is run, each survivor can also carry a reading an analyst may accept, downgrade, or reject. This division of labor suits a domain where ground-truthing is the scarce resource and the cost of a false positive is measured in field days. It also aligns with what the ablation in Section 3.6 shows. The interpretation model reads mound-like shape but does not, on its own, separate pre-European earthworks from modern ones. Its role is interpretation of shield survivors, not discrimination. The age filter stays with the context shield.

The honest accounting in Section 3 is itself a methodological point. An automated survey system accumulates components faster than it accumulates controlled evaluations of them, and the failure modes are easy to miss. A pipeline can report context it never checked. A case study can be run over the wrong coordinates. A benchmark number can be quoted from a superseded checkpoint. We redesigned the shield to surface incomplete context. We corrected the Jaketown candidate counts. We corrected a Lake George case study that had been scanned about 8 km off the actual mound site. These are the kind of discipline a deployable system needs. For a method whose output is a coordinate, getting the coordinate right is not a detail. It is the whole point.


## 5. Limitations and future work

**The interpretation layer adds description, not discrimination (now measured).** No reported detection depends on the fine-tuned model; every detection comes from the geomorphon detector and the shield. We tested the model against Eskew's field-verified set (Section 3.6): it retains most real mounds but accepts 19 of 22 modern earthworks as mounds, so it does not separate pre-European from recently constructed earthworks. This is expected, because that separation is contextual, not morphological, and is the shield's job. But it means the model must not be treated as a discriminator. Its role in the workflow is limited to natural-language interpretation of shield survivors. Improving it is a matter of stricter structural discrimination and a broader range of universal non-mound shapes during training, rather than region-specific data.

**The detector evaluated here is a single-scale approximation.** Section 3's results use a single-scale, elevation-referenced geomorphon classifier (Section 2.2), not the multi-scale, angle-thresholded, persistence-scored method that the engine also implements and that the introduction motivates. Integrating multi-scale persistence into the detection path and rerunning the Section 3 evaluation with it are priorities. The single-scale 0.3 m flatness floor may suppress the sub-0.5 m mounds we emphasize. We expect the multi-scale form to change the recovery of the faintest features and most likely improve it.

**No simpler baseline is reported.** We do not benchmark the geomorphon detector against a lighter alternative (a local-relief-model blob detector, or an object-based rule set) on the same reference set, so we cannot yet say how much of the recall is attributable to the geomorphon representation. A baseline comparison is a straightforward and worthwhile addition.

**Reproducibility bookkeeping is incomplete.** Different reported numbers were produced by different scripts and, at present, two coordinate systems (Section 2.1). A single table mapping each number to its script, detector variant, parameters, CRS, and data artifact should accompany submission. One detector parameter needs documenting. It is an internal aspect-ratio down-weighting (aspect > 4.0), distinct from the shield's 4.5/3.0 linearity thresholds. Stating it keeps the two linearity tests from being conflated.

**Shield precision is unmeasured by field truth, and a desk screen suggests it is low on modern-dominated tiles.** We report detector recall and the shield's rejection rate (71% at Jaketown), but not field-verified precision. A preliminary visual review of the Jaketown survivors (Section 3.2) found none that present as convincing mounds. The survivors are compact modern and natural features (field-edge berms, drainage and dredge-spoil ridges, borrow pits, natural levees) that clear the linearity and land-cover layers. This is a desk screen, not ground truth. It exposes a concrete gap. The shield lacks a simple **positive-relief-and-compactness filter**. On the Jaketown tile, that filter does most of the mound-versus-modern separation the geomorphon, land-cover, and linearity layers do not, cutting the 80 survivors to 4 (Section 3.2). We recommend adding such a layer as standard, tuned as an operating point rather than a fixed cutoff. A 0.5 m floor is suitable for open cropland but would discard the sub-0.5 m mounds we target. Converting any of this into a precision figure requires the labeled, non-adversarial, ground-truthed evaluation that remains required and is in preparation.

**NLCD dependence on an external service.** The Esri NLCD ImageServer used by earlier runs began requiring an authentication token and was failing without notice. We repointed the layer at the token-free MRLC public WMS, which restored it. The layer still depends on a remote service. A cached or local NLCD raster would make it dependable offline.

**Noise map coverage.** The modern-feature layer is only as good as the quadrangle coverage and the optical character recognition behind it, and it must be generated per area of interest. Its recall against actual canals and levees has not been quantified.

**Coordinate error is catalog agreement, not accuracy.** The offsets (median 5.1 m, mean 9.4 m in UTM 15N; Table 1) measure agreement between detected centroids and a legacy catalog whose own positions carry uncertainty, in one case about 200 m (Section 3.5). They are not an independent benchmark for accuracy. The dependence of recall on match tolerance (Table 1) follows from that catalog uncertainty. Characterizing the catalog's positional error relative to an independently surveyed subset would allow recall and accuracy to be reported against a known target.

**Zero-shot transfer.** Transfer from New York terrain to the Delta is the design's central bet. On the internal benchmark, the deployed V9.1 (checkpoint-1250) model scores 76.2% on the zero-shot category. This is the capability we trust least, and it is measured on a proxy benchmark rather than on Delta ground truth. Independent Delta ground truth beyond the reference set is needed to validate the reference set.

**Canopy is a hard limit of the public bare-earth product.** The corrected Lake George rescan (Section 3.4) shows that the seamless 3DEP DTM does not contain the 17 m Mound A under closed bottomland-hardwood canopy. The method is blind to forested mounds until a leaf-off or custom canopy-penetrating surface is supplied. Recovery is product-dependent. Quantifying it across the basin's forested fraction is future work.

**Case studies remain partly preliminary.** The Jaketown rejection rate, the corrected Lake George negative, and the cropland agricultural-island scan are reported here as demonstrations of shield *behavior*. None is yet a precision measurement. Ground-truthing of the Jaketown and cropland survivors, and a canopy-penetrating reacquisition at Lake George, are the next steps.

## 6. Conclusion

We have described and evaluated a reproducible workflow for screening bare-earth LiDAR for earthen mounds in a heavily modified alluvial landscape, and we have tried to report it as we ran it. It has two measured strengths. The first is a geomorphon detector that recovers about 9 in 10 cataloged mounds within a 30 m tolerance in true meters. The second is an auditable context shield that separates compact prehistoric shapes from the linear and developed fabric of the modern Delta, shown from the agricultural islands it preserves to the levees it rejects. We tested the fine-tuned vision-language interpretation layer in Section 3.6. It reads mound-like shapes but does not separate pre-European from modern earthworks. This confirms that the age filter belongs to the context shield, not to shape. The model's role is interpretation, not discrimination. The boundaries are stated in plain terms. Precision on new candidates is not measured here, and no candidate is field-verified. The evaluated detector is a single-scale variant of the multi-scale method envisioned in the workflow. Closed canopy can remove even a large mound from the public bare-earth product. Turning archaeological screening into a reproducible pipeline is feasible, and the pace of landscape change in the Delta makes it more needed each year. The remaining work is an agenda we have tried to make explicit. It includes a precision evaluation on a representative labeled tile, integration of the multi-scale detector, stronger structural discrimination in the interpretation layer, and field verification.

## Data and Code Availability

The complete workflow is released as an open-source Python package, **EarthworkLLM**, at <https://github.com/clipo/earthwork-llm> (Apache 2.0). It contains the geomorphon detector, the False Positive Shield, the regional scanner, and the validation scripts. The detector, the shield, and the regional case-study scans (Sections 3.3–3.5) are retrieved from the public USGS 3DEP ImageServer without an API key and no local data store. The recall evaluation (Section 3.1) and the interpretation-layer ablation (Section 3.6) need restricted data described below. The repository's reproduction guide maps each result to the script that generates it and records the coordinate system used for each (Section 2.1).

In keeping with archaeological data-sensitivity norms, the repository contains coordinates only for already-published public monuments (Winterville, Lake George / Holly Bluff, and Jaketown, each with a citation). The 35-mound reference set used for the recall evaluation (Section 3.1) draws on restricted Lower Mississippi Survey grid-quadrat coordinates and is **not** distributed. Qualified researchers may obtain it from the authors and supply it to the validation scripts through a documented environment variable. We ask that users of the code honor the same policy and refrain from contributing unpublished site locations.

The fine-tuned vision-language interpretation layer is a QLoRA adapter over Qwen3-VL-30B-A3B-Thinking, trained zero-shot on New York State LiDAR. It is released separately as model weights via Hugging Face, linked from the repository. It is required only for the interpretation-layer ablation (Section 3.6; Appendix B, Table B1). No other reported result depends on it.

## Appendix A. Interpretation layer: training corpus, fine-tuning, and benchmark detail

This appendix gives the full build details summarised in Section 2.5.3. None of it measures the interpretation layer's contribution to mound detection (Section 3.6). It is provided for reproducibility.


![**Figure A1. Multi-view ensemble.** The six-panel composite supplied to the model for each candidate: four hillshades at different sun azimuths, the geomorphon classification, and a contour overlay. Multiple illumination angles reveal subtle relief and erosion texture that a single hillshade hides.](figures/fig2_multiview_ensemble.png)

**Training data.** The fine-tuning corpus is built from high-resolution New York State LiDAR (the public NYSGPO collections). New York offers varied, noisy terrain that forces the model to learn the geometric difference between natural and cultural relief rather than memorizing a single landscape. That terrain includes glacial drumlins and eskers, historic cellar holes, quarry pits, and military earthworks. No Yazoo Basin or other Delta data is used in training. The corpus is assembled by program. A DEM tile is reduced to geomorphons and hillshades, a deterministic routine reads off the ground-truth landform, and the pair is written as an image-plus-question training example. Three kinds of example dominate. *Geomorphon drills* (tens of thousands of items) present a hillshade-and-geomorphon composite and ask the model to name the dominant landform, balanced across all ten geomorphon classes. These were added to improve geomorphon classification accuracy, the competence on which everything downstream depends. *Multi-scale feature-identification* examples ask the model to map a morphological primitive at a given scale to a feature interpretation (for example, a linear Valley to a drainage channel, a flat-topped Peak to a platform). *Anti-forgetting replay* examples preserve the base model's general visual and reasoning ability so that domain adaptation does not erode it. The production checkpoint is fine-tuned on a balanced mixture of about 40,000 such examples; an earlier, dense model generation was trained on a far larger corpus (on the order of a million examples), and a central finding of our checkpoint history (below) is that the smaller balanced mixture, targeted at the weakest competence, was the more effective recipe.

**Fine-tuning method.** Adaptation uses QLoRA (Dettmers et al. 2023), which combines 4-bit quantization with low-rank adaptation (LoRA; Hu et al. 2021): the base model is loaded in 4-bit precision (NF4 with double quantization, bf16 compute) and frozen, and small low-rank adapter matrices are trained on top of it. We use a LoRA rank of 64 and an alpha of 128 with 0.05 dropout, and we freeze the vision tower so that only the language-and-reasoning pathway is adapted. Training runs for a single epoch at a learning rate of 1×10⁻⁵ (cosine schedule, brief warmup) with an effective batch size of 16 (a per-device batch of one with sixteen-step gradient accumulation) and a 1,024-token context. The entire run fits on a single workstation-class accelerator with unified memory, which is the point: the system is meant to be retrainable and deployable in the field, not only in a data center. QLoRA makes this feasible by training a few hundred million adapter parameters rather than all 30 billion of the base model.

**Checkpoint history and what it measures.** We track progress on an internal benchmark comprising 13 terrain-reasoning tasks grouped into 5 categories: geomorphon classification, tactical/landscape assessment, recovery-and-detection search, zero-shot transfer to unseen terrain, and domain terminology. The tasks are scored by expected-keyword recall and averaged by category. The trajectory shows that building this model was an exercise in trading capabilities against one another rather than climbing a single number. A first aligned checkpoint (V1) scored about 42% overall. A dense 32-billion-parameter "Thinking" model (V8), trained on the full ~1-million-example corpus, reached 84.1% overall, with geomorphon classification near 71%. Moving to the mixture-of-experts base and retraining on the 40,000-example balanced mixture produced an intermediate checkpoint. It pushed geomorphon classification up to about 85% but cut zero-shot transfer to about 74%. That is the wrong trade for a system whose premise is transfer to an unseen basin. We therefore rebalanced the mixture by adding zero-shot and recovery examples and trained the V9.1 run. From its checkpoints, we selected **`checkpoint-1250`** as the deployed model because it is the best-balanced checkpoint in the run. It scores **85.3%** overall and passes every category threshold at once. The per-category scores are geomorphon classification **72.7%** (the only V9.1 checkpoint above the 70% bar that also keeps transfer high), zero-shot transfer **76.2%**, recovery search **96.4%**, tactical assessment **94.4%**, and domain terminology **90%**. On a mission-weighted metric that prioritizes the capabilities this application depends on (zero-shot transfer: 0.30, recovery: 0.25, tactical: 0.20, geomorphon: 0.15, terminology: 0.10), it scores **85.8%**. We chose it over later checkpoints that edged higher on the transfer-weighted metric but let geomorphon classification fall below the 70% threshold. For a pipeline whose first stage is a deterministic geomorphon detector, a model that has lost its grip on geomorphon vocabulary is the wrong trade.

Three cautions about this benchmark are owed to the reader. It originates in a broader **military terrain-analysis** project, and its five categories reflect that origin. Several test general or tactical terrain competence rather than archaeological mound detection, and the terminology category is a single task quoted at 90%. The thirteen tasks are scored based on expected keyword recall. This is a coarse proxy. It credits the presence of the right vocabulary without checking the reasoning behind it, and it can penalize a correct synonym. With only thirteen tasks, it cannot support fine distinctions between checkpoints, and small differences should not be over-read. The scores measure the model against this internal rubric, not against Delta earthworks. They indicate general terrain-reasoning competence, not performance in archaeological detection. The benchmark does not test detection. The ablation in Section 3.6 provides that test. The per-checkpoint scoring artifacts are released with the project so the numbers can be audited against the code.

## Appendix B. Supplemental methods, statistics, and the interpretation-layer ablation

This appendix holds the detailed statistics summarized in Sections 3.1, 3.2, and 3.6. None of it is required to follow the main argument; it is provided for completeness and reproducibility.

### B.1 Recall: tolerance curve, matching protocol, and missed mounds (Section 3.1)

DEMs were retrieved for all 35 mounds. Recall as a function of match tolerance is: 51% at 10 m (18/35), 60% at 15 m (21/35), 71% at 20 m (25/35), 80% at 25 m (28/35), and 89% at 30 m (31/35). Among the 31 recovered mounds the centroid-to-catalog offset has a median of 5.1 m and a mean of 9.4 m, with a maximum of 29.4 m. The detector is deterministic, so a repeat run on re-fetched DEMs returns the same result; this establishes pipeline determinism, not independent robustness. Four mounds are missed at the 30 m tolerance: 22-M-1 (nearest candidate 74 m), 22-N-13_m5 (51 m), and 22-N-11_m2 and 22-N-11_m3 (both about 43 m). The last two belong to a single site whose two catalog points fall in a tight cluster, where a neighboring feature dominates the candidate ranking; 22-M-1 and 22-N-13_m5 are low-profile features. The matching protocol is lenient: for each mound a 300 m tile is centered on the catalog point, the detector returns up to ten candidates, and a hit is scored if any of them falls within the tolerance. This is a best-case, centered-window sensitivity test rather than detection within an uncurated regional scan; a stricter single-best-candidate rule would make it harder.

### B.2 The positive-relief gate on Jaketown survivors (Section 3.2)

Applying the relief-and-compactness threshold (local relief at least 0.5 m and a 100–5,000 m² footprint) to the eighty Jaketown survivors leaves four and removes 76. Two readings of that number must both be resisted. It is not a precision measurement: none of the four is ground-truthed, and the 95% reduction reflects how flat and modern the survivor pool on this adversarial tile already was, which the desk screen had shown. It is also not an independent validation when checked against that same desk screen, because the threshold and the human review are two forms of one construct, positive relief plus compactness, so their agreement is close to circular; and the agreement is in any case only partial, since of the eight survivors the review rated plausible the threshold keeps three and discards five. The gate should be tuned as an operating point rather than a fixed cutoff: a 0.5 m relief floor suits open cropland but would discard the sub-0.5 m mounds the introduction emphasizes, so the threshold must be set to the regime being screened and its interaction with the detector's own 0.3 m floor characterized. A single adversarial tile cannot settle precision either way; disentangling the explanations requires precision measured on a randomly sited, non-adversarial tile with the complete noise map and the compactness gate enabled (Section 5), anchored by field verification.

### B.3 Interpretation-layer ablation (Section 3.6)

Eskew's (2008) contour-circularity algorithm flagged a set of candidate earthworks in the northern basin; she, Lipo, and Hunt then visited them on the ground in June 2008 and sorted them into genuine pre-European mounds and modern earthworks that resemble them (levee spoil, channel clearance, field berms). That field-verified split is the discrimination a downstream interpreter must make. We assemble it as a labeled set of six field-confirmed mounds (Glover Place, Gates, I.C. Village, Lula, and the open-terrain monuments Winterville and Lake George) and twenty-two field-confirmed modern earthworks. For each site we fetch the same bare-earth DEM, build the six-panel image the pipeline serves, and ask the deployed model (terrallm-v91) for a MOUND / NOT-MOUND verdict, taking a majority vote over three runs because generation at temperature zero is not fully deterministic under vLLM's batching.

The model labels four of the six confirmed mounds as mounds (Gates, I.C. Village, Lula, Winterville); it misses Glover and returns NOT-MOUND for Lake George, whose mound is absent from the canopy-covered bare-earth surface, so recall on the mounds present in the data is four of five. Of the twenty-two modern earthworks it accepts nineteen as mounds and rejects three (Table B1). As a filter for pre-European features it is ineffective. This clarifies the architecture rather than undermining it: every feature in the negative set is a compact, mound-like earthwork, so shape cannot separate a Mississippian mound from a 1970s canal-spoil pile. The separation is contextual, not morphological, and it is the shield's task. The route to improving the model is not region-specific data, because a mound's shape is the same in any valley, but stricter use of the structural combinations and a wider range of universal non-mound shapes in training.
