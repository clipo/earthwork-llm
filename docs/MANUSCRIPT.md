# Toward Label-Free Archaeological Prospection: A Zero-Shot Workflow for Detecting Earthen Mounds from Bare-Earth LiDAR in the Yazoo Basin

**Authors**: C. P. Lipo, D. S. Davis, and R. J. DiNapoli
**Target Journal**: *Archaeological Prospection*
**Status**: Working manuscript. The paper's argument is that a **label-free, zero-shot** approach — one that needs no labelled examples from the target landscape — is a viable and scalable direction for earthwork prospection where the finite archaeological record cannot supply the training data supervised detectors require. It is evaluated as a sensitivity (recall) and false-positive-screening study, not a discovery report: no candidates are field-verified here, and the fine-tuned vision-language interpretation layer is a proposed component whose end-to-end contribution is not yet measured. Claims are scoped accordingly.

---

## Abstract

Archaeological survey in the Yazoo Basin of the Lower Mississippi Valley is constrained by three problems at once: the relief of ancient earthen mounds is subtle, often less than half a meter; the modern landscape is saturated with anthropogenic features (levees, canal spoil banks, road fills) that mimic that relief; and the area to be screened runs to thousands of square kilometers. A fourth constraint is methodological: the supervised detectors that dominate automated LiDAR prospection require tens to hundreds of labelled examples per feature class and transfer poorly between regions, yet the finite, unevenly known archaeological record often cannot supply them. We therefore describe and evaluate a **label-free** workflow — one that uses no labelled examples from the target landscape — and argue that this zero-shot direction is a scalable answer to the training-data problem. It screens public bare-earth LiDAR for compact mound-like features and produces a ranked, human-reviewable candidate list. A geomorphon classifier reduces the digital elevation model (DEM) to morphological primitives; a deterministic detector extracts compact candidates; and a *False Positive Shield* screens every candidate against footprint linearity, National Land Cover Database (NLCD) class, and proximity to canals and levees mapped from USGS topographic quadrangles, returning a KEEP / FLAG / REJECT verdict with explicit handling of missing context. A fine-tuned vision-language model (Qwen3-VL-30B-A3B-Thinking, adapted on New York State LiDAR and applied zero-shot) is provided as an interpretation layer for shield survivors; we describe it in full but treat its end-to-end contribution as an open question, because it is not yet placed in the measurement loop. We evaluate the workflow as a **sensitivity and false-positive-screening study, not a discovery report.** Against a reference set of 35 catalogued mounds, and reporting recall as a function of match tolerance in true (UTM) metres, the geomorphon detector — which is trained on no labelled mounds whatsoever — recovers 31 of 35 at a 30 m tolerance and 18 of 35 at 10 m, an agreement with the legacy catalogue whose strong tolerance dependence reflects the catalogue's own positional uncertainty, at a median centroid-to-catalogue offset near 5 m; that this is achieved with zero training labels from the Delta is the core evidence for the label-free direction. We deliberately foreground the limits: precision on novel candidates is not measured here and requires a labelled regional sample and field verification that we identify as the essential next step; a preliminary desk screen of one regional tile's survivors found few convincing mounds; and closed canopy can remove even a 17 m mound from the public bare-earth product entirely. We argue the workflow's value is twofold: as a prioritisation engine that concentrates scarce field effort, and — more consequentially for the discipline — as a demonstration that a label-free, zero-shot direction is a viable and scalable path for prospecting a finite archaeological record. We report its components — validated, proposed, and unmeasured — as such.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The problem of the Delta

The Yazoo Basin of northwestern Mississippi, the Delta, is one of the densest concentrations of pre-European earthen architecture in North America. It was a center of Woodland and Mississippian settlement, part of a Lower Mississippi Valley mound-building tradition whose roots reach back to the Late Archaic (Grooms et al. 2023), and its inhabitants built thousands of mounds that served as platforms, burial places, and ceremonial foci (Lipo and Dunnell 2007). The largest centers, such as Winterville and Lake George, remain prominent monuments on the ground (though, as Section 3.4 shows, canopy can still hide even a 17 m mound from an airborne bare-earth survey). Most mounds are far less conspicuous. Many are conical features under a meter in height, eroded and plowed, hidden in secondary forest or standing isolated in cropland.

Since the early twentieth century the Delta has been transformed. Mechanized agriculture has leveled subtle relief across most of the basin, and large flood-control works by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers have reshaped the rest. Dredging for drainage and navigation produced thousands of kilometers of canals, each flanked by linear spoil banks that, once eroded and vegetated, present a morphological signature close to that of an ancient embankment. Levees, raised road beds, and industrial pads add to the noise — the distinctive, pervasive topographic signature of modern human activity that any high-resolution terrain method must contend with (Tarolli and Sofia 2016).

This combination is what makes the Delta hard. A human analyst scanning hillshade tiles for mounds faces survey fatigue and a strong tendency to fixate on large, obvious structures while missing low-profile features. At the same time the landscape offers an overwhelming number of false positives. Any automated approach that is sensitive enough to catch a 0.3 m rise will also flag every spoil bank in its path, and the cost of that sensitivity is paid later, in wasted ground-truthing.

### 1.2 Contribution

Automated detection of archaeological features from airborne LiDAR is an established and active field (Chase et al. 2010; Vinci et al. 2024). Object-based image analysis has localised earthen mounds and shell rings in the Southeastern United States (Davis et al. 2018, 2019) and monumental earthworks elsewhere (Freeland et al. 2016), and convolutional and region-based neural networks now detect barrows, qanats, and comparable structures in LiDAR-derived terrain (Verschoof-van der Vaart and Lambers 2019; Guyot et al. 2021; Soroush et al. 2020), including with multi-source remote sensing at Mississippian centres in the Lower Mississippi Valley (Kadhim et al. 2023). These supervised methods work, but they share a dependence that is a structural obstacle for archaeology rather than a passing limitation: they require tens to hundreds of labelled examples of a feature class to train, and their accuracy degrades when a detector trained in one region or on one sensor is moved to another (Guyot et al. 2021; Lambers et al. 2019). The difficulty is that the archaeological record is finite and unevenly known. For many feature types and many regions the labelled examples a supervised detector needs do not exist in sufficient number, and producing them is expensive, sometimes destructive, and occasionally impossible (Bickler 2021). A method whose appetite for labelled data grows with every new landscape does not scale to a discipline whose training signal is fixed and scarce — precisely the situation in the Yazoo Basin, where the known-mound sample is small, of uneven positional quality, and cannot be enlarged at will.

A response to exactly this problem has emerged in machine learning. **Foundation models** are trained by self-supervision on abundant, generic data and then applied to new tasks and domains with little or no task-specific labelling — the **zero-shot transfer** introduced by Radford et al. (2021) and the broader foundation-model programme surveyed by Bommasani et al. (2021), now reaching remote sensing (Kuckreja et al. 2024; Mai et al. 2024) and, tentatively, the detection of buried archaeological structures (Sech et al. 2023). Its attraction for archaeology is that it decouples *where a model learns* from *where it is applied*: the abundant training signal is drawn from terrain the discipline need not have excavated, and the scarce, finite archaeological record is spent on evaluation and interpretation rather than consumed by training.

**The contribution of this paper is to demonstrate that this label-free direction is viable for earthwork prospection — and to do so honestly.** The workflow we describe uses no labelled examples from the target landscape at all: the geomorphon detector is deterministic and trained on nothing, and the vision-language interpretation layer is fine-tuned on New York State terrain and applied to the Delta with no local labels. That a pipeline built this way recovers the great majority of a catalogue of known Delta mounds (Section 3.1) — having seen no Delta mound in training — is the paper's central claim: not that Delta prospection is solved, but that a zero-shot, foundation-model approach to it works well enough to be worth developing, and is one of the few directions that scales to a finite record. Within that direction the workflow makes three concrete design choices, each with a lesson from the literature behind it. It reasons about shape rather than height, working on geomorphons rather than raw elevation, which suits a near-level plain. It automates the ancillary-context screening that Davis et al. (2018) recommend — combining land-cover class, mapped modern features, and footprint linearity into an auditable KEEP / FLAG / REJECT shield — because no single extractor is reliably best and morphology alone cannot separate mounds from modern earthworks (Davis et al. 2018, 2019). And it adds a fine-tuned vision-language model as a natural-language interpretation layer for the candidates that survive screening.

We are deliberate about how far the demonstration reaches. What is measured is the label-free detector's sensitivity and the shield's screening behaviour; what is not yet measured is end-to-end precision and the interpretation layer's specific contribution — the two experiments that would move the direction from promising to proven. We describe the workflow as it is built, distinguish components that run automatically from those that require an analyst, and say so wherever a claim rests on a single illustrative run rather than a controlled experiment. The value we claim is that of a demonstrated direction, honestly bounded, not a finished instrument.

### 1.3 Study area

The Yazoo Basin is an elliptical alluvial plain of roughly 11,000 km² between the Mississippi River on the west and the bluffs on the east (Figure 1). It is flat, with slopes commonly under one percent, and its surface records the migration of the Mississippi and Yazoo rivers as a tapestry of abandoned channels, relict natural levees, and backswamp depressions. The archaeological record ranges from multi-mound platform centers like Winterville and Lake George (Holly Bluff) to small isolated conical mounds. The prospection problem here is not finding height. It is separating cultural intentionality from mechanical noise on a near-level plane.

## 2. Methodology

The pipeline has four stages: data ingestion, multi-scale geomorphon morphology, the False Positive Shield, and AI reasoning (Figure 2). Each stage narrows the candidate set, so that the most expensive operation, a call to the vision-language model, runs only on candidates that have survived the cheaper morphological and contextual filters.

![**Figure 2. The screening workflow.** Four stages move from raw elevation to a ranked candidate list. (The figure image carries the project's legacy "discovery pipeline" title, to be relabelled at typesetting.) Parameter values shown are those of the geomorphon engine (1 m DEM; scales 2, 5, 10, 25 m; 1.0° flatness); note that the detector actually evaluated in Section 3 uses a single 5 m scale with a 0.3 m elevation-difference tolerance (Section 2.2). The shield returns KEEP, FLAG, or REJECT, and the fine-tuned Qwen3-VL-30B-A3B-Thinking model is the (not-yet-validated) interpretation layer.](figures/fig4_workflow.png)

### 2.1 Data ingestion

Bare-earth DEMs are fetched from the USGS 3D Elevation Program (3DEP) ImageServer at 1 m resolution. The regional-scan pipeline processes them in Web Mercator (EPSG:3857); at the basin's latitude (~33° N) that projection inflates ground distances by about 19% and areas by about 42%, so the distances and areas quoted in the regional case studies (Sections 3.3–3.5) are in these projected units. The one exception is the recall evaluation of Section 3.1, which we recompute in the project's analytical CRS, UTM Zone 15N, to report in true ground metres; recomputing the case studies in UTM is a straightforward step that does not change their qualitative conclusions, and we flag it as a revision to make throughout. For regional work the area of interest is tiled into 500 m squares with 50 m of overlap, and each tile is processed in turn. LiDAR coverage of the Delta derives largely from the 2009 to 2010 USACE Delta Phase 1 acquisition, which spans the major sites. Working from 3DEP delivers a consistent, gridded, bare-earth product without the per-tile point-cloud handling that raw LAZ would require.

### 2.2 Geomorphon classification

LiDAR transformed archaeology by giving bare-earth surfaces under vegetation, but a single hillshade is a subjective instrument; what it shows depends on the sun angle the analyst happens to choose. We instead reduce the DEM to **geomorphons** (Jasiewicz and Stepinski 2013), a pattern-recognition classification of local landform.

For each cell, the method casts lines of sight in eight directions and asks, for each, whether the terrain along that line rises, falls, or stays within a flatness tolerance; each direction yields a ternary value of plus one, minus one, or zero, the eight values form a code, and the counts of ups and downs map the cell to one of ten landform classes: Flat, Peak, Ridge, Shoulder, Spur, Slope, Hollow, Footslope, Valley, and Pit (Figure 3). A cell that looks down in every direction is a Peak; one that looks up in every direction is a Pit; a linear high is a Ridge; a linear low is a Valley. In the canonical formulation the flatness tolerance is an *angle* (Jasiewicz and Stepinski use 1.0°), so that classification depends on local form rather than absolute height.

We report our implementation exactly, because it differs from that canonical form in two respects that a reader must know to interpret the results. The classifier that produced every detection in Section 3 is a **single-scale** line-of-sight classifier with a search radius of five cells (5 m on the 1 m DEM), and its flatness tolerance is an **elevation-difference threshold of 0.3 m** across that window rather than an angle. The angular, multi-scale form is implemented in the same geomorphon engine (below) but is not the classifier that generated the reported detections. The 0.3 m tolerance sets a practical floor on the relief the detector registers; its interaction with the sub-0.5 m mounds emphasised in the introduction is a limitation we return to in Section 5.

![**Figure 3. Geomorphons.** (A) For each cell, eight lines of sight are classified as rising (+), falling (−), or level (0) using a 1.0 degree flatness threshold. (B) The eight ternary values form a code; a cell that looks down in all directions resolves to PEAK. (C) Six diagnostic landforms and their archaeological analogues. Classification depends on local morphology, not absolute elevation, which is why the method finds subtle mounds on a flat plain.](figures/fig_geomorphon_concepts.png)

This morphological framing is what gives the archaeological mapping its power. A conical burial mound resolves as a compact Peak, a platform mound as a flat-topped Peak or a Flat bounded by Shoulders, a levee or embankment as a linear Ridge, and a drainage ditch or canal as a linear Valley. The classifier is generic; the archaeological interpretation is imposed afterward.

**Multi-scale persistence (implemented, not yet in the detection path).** Mounds exist at different sizes, and the geomorphon engine therefore computes classifications at four search radii — 2, 5, 10, and 25 m — with a per-cell persistence score that measures how consistently a cell keeps the same landform class across scales: a feature that is a Peak at 2 m and remains a Peak at 5 m and 10 m is more credible than one that appears at a single scale and vanishes at the next. In the present study this multi-scale representation is used to generate the vision-language model's training data (Section 2.5.3); it is **not** yet wired into the detection-and-validation path, which uses the single-scale classifier described above. Integrating multi-scale persistence into detection — and re-running the Section 3 validation against it — is a priority for future work (Section 5); we expect it to improve recovery of the smallest and largest mounds that a single 5 m scale serves least well.

### 2.3 Candidate extraction

The deterministic detector converts a geomorphon raster into a short list of candidate footprints. It selects the landform classes appropriate to the query (for a mound query, Peak, Ridge, Shoulder, and Spur), labels connected components of the resulting mask, and keeps components whose area falls in a class-specific window (for example 20 to 5,000 m² for a generic mound, 100 to 10,000 m² for a platform mound). For each surviving component it records the centroid, the footprint area, the local height relative to surrounding terrain, and the bounding-box aspect ratio. The aspect ratio is carried forward as the linearity input to the shield. The detector returns the ten strongest candidates per tile.

### 2.4 The False Positive Shield V2

The shield is the part of the system that turns sensitivity into usable specificity, and it is the part this manuscript treats most carefully. Its premise is not new: Davis et al. (2018) already identified land-use context and historic-map comparison as the means to suppress the levee- and construction-derived false positives that defeat morphology-only detection in modified landscapes. What we contribute is an *automated* realisation of that guidance — a reproducible layer that queries the context sources programmatically and combines them into an auditable verdict — together with two specific additions: an explicit KEEP / FLAG / REJECT logic that surfaces borderline and unevaluable cases rather than silently passing them, and a rule protecting compact "agricultural island" mounds within cultivated fields. The shield applies three independent layers and returns one of three verdicts for each candidate: KEEP, FLAG, or REJECT (Figure 4). A single decisive layer is enough to reject a candidate, and rejected candidates are dropped before any model call.

1. **Morphological linearity.** Prehistoric mounds are compact; levees, road beds, and canal spoil banks are long and thin. A footprint with a bounding-box aspect ratio at or above **4.5** is rejected as a linear feature. One with an aspect ratio between **3.0 and 4.5** is flagged and its score is reduced, but it is retained for review. The linearity test is disabled when the active query targets geometric enclosures, which are themselves elongated.

2. **NLCD land cover.** The National Land Cover Database class at the candidate point is queried from the NLCD ImageServer. Developed land (classes 21 to 24) and open water (class 11) are modern, not prehistoric, and candidates that fall on them are rejected. A significant enhancement in V2 is the explicit protection of **agricultural islands**: compact features (aspect < 2.5) located in `Cultivated Crops` (82) or `Pasture/Hay` (81) are kept, acknowledging that mounds frequently survive within mechanized fields. However, moderate elongation in these fields (aspect 2.5 to 4.5) is flagged as a probable agricultural artifact (plow line or ditch).

3. **Modern feature proximity.** Canals, ditches, levees, and roads are read from USGS topographic quadrangles, both the modern US Topo series and the Historical Topographic Map Collection (HTMC), using optical character recognition and color-based line extraction. V2 expands this "noise map" to include **irrigation features, borrow pits, and abandoned railroad grades**. A candidate within 25 m of such a feature is rejected as a probable spoil bank or road shoulder; one within 50 m is flagged. The HTMC layer matters because some early twentieth-century modifications, such as logging roads, are now under tree cover and absent from modern maps.

![**Figure 4. The False Positive Shield.** Three context layers screen every candidate and combine into a single verdict. A clearly linear footprint, developed land or open water, or a mapped modern feature within the rejection radius is each sufficient to reject. Borderline cases are flagged and retained with a reduced score. When a layer cannot be evaluated, the candidate is recorded as having incomplete context rather than being silently passed.](figures/fig_fp_shield.png)

Two implementation details are important for honest reporting. First, the modern-feature layer depends on a noise map that must be generated for the area of interest; generating it requires downloading the relevant quadrangles and running optical character recognition, which has a per-image cost, and the regional scanner logs a warning when it runs without one. Second, when the NLCD service is unreachable, which happened during the Jaketown run reported below, the shield does not treat the candidate as clean. It records the layer as unavailable and marks the candidate's context as incomplete, so that downstream reporting can distinguish a candidate that passed the land-cover test from one that was never tested.

### 2.5 AI reasoning

The final stage passes shield survivors to a fine-tuned vision-language model for a natural-language archaeological reading. We describe this stage in full because it is a substantial part of the system as engineered, but we state plainly at the outset what Section 3 bears out: **the interpretation layer is a proposed component, and its contribution to detection outcomes is not measured in this study.** The regional scans reported below stop at the shield; no reported detection number depends on the model. What follows is therefore the description of a designed and benchmarked capability whose end-to-end value awaits the controlled ablation we identify as the essential next step (Section 5).

#### 2.5.1 The multi-view ensemble

Each surviving candidate is rendered as a six-panel composite image (Figure 5) that gives the model the evidence a human analyst would assemble by hand. Four of the panels are hillshades lit from azimuths of 45, 135, 225, and 315 degrees at a 45 degree altitude, so that no feature is lost in the shadow of a single sun angle — a multi-illumination strategy that compensates for the strong directionality of any single shaded-relief image (Bennett et al. 2012); complementary relief-visualization techniques such as topographic openness (Doneus 2013) address the same directionality by other means, and could be added to the ensemble. The fifth panel is the color-coded geomorphon raster, and the sixth overlays topographic contours on a hillshade. The composite, together with the detector's candidate list, is sent to the model.

![**Figure 5. Multi-view ensemble.** The six-panel composite supplied to the model for each candidate: four hillshades at different sun azimuths, the geomorphon classification, and a contour overlay. Multiple illumination angles reveal subtle relief and erosion texture that a single hillshade hides.](figures/fig2_multiview_ensemble.png)

Alongside the image, the prompt carries explicit textual context about nearby modern noise — a feature we call **Context-Aware Visual Correlation**. For every candidate, the detector's shield output is summarized into the prompt (e.g., `Nearby Noise: irrigation ditch (15 m away)`), and the model is instructed to assess visually whether the detected feature aligns with that modern context or instead shows the subtle erosion and symmetry of an ancient earthwork. This pushes the model to "explain away" false positives rather than merely flag high-probability shapes.

#### 2.5.2 Model and serving

The reasoning model is **Qwen3-VL-30B-A3B-Thinking** (Yang et al. 2025), a mixture-of-experts (MoE) vision-language model with roughly 30.5 billion total parameters but only about 3.3 billion active per token (128 experts, eight routed per token), fine-tuned with QLoRA. The MoE design is deliberate: it gives the reasoning capacity of a 30-billion-parameter model at the inference cost of a 3-billion-parameter one, which is what allows the model to run on a single workstation in the field. We describe how it was built in Section 2.5.3. The deployed checkpoint is **V9.1** (`checkpoint-1250`). On our internal benchmark of thirteen terrain-reasoning tasks it scores 85.3% overall and passes every category threshold (Section 2.5.3 gives the per-category breakdown and the checkpoint history behind it). The model is served with vLLM behind an OpenAI-compatible API (the adapter targets the attention projections and the mixture-of-experts router, so it is served as a LoRA module without merging); on the deployment hardware a full interpretation takes on the order of twenty to thirty seconds per candidate, against roughly two seconds for the deterministic detector alone, which is why the model is reserved for shield survivors.

For a subset of candidates we also performed a **manual cross-check with Gemini 3 Pro**, supplying the same composite image and prompt through that model's interface and comparing its reading to the fine-tuned model's. This cross-check is a manual quality control step performed by the analyst. It is not part of the automated pipeline.

#### 2.5.3 How the model was built: training data, fine-tuning, and zero-shot transfer

The interpretation layer adapts a general vision-language model to terrain reasoning rather than training a detector from scratch: fine-tuning supplies the vocabulary and priors of a terrain analyst, not new perception. Its full build detail — training corpus, hyperparameters, checkpoint history, and per-category benchmark scores — is given in Appendix A; here we state only what bears on the paper's argument.

**Training data and fine-tuning.** Crucially for the zero-shot design, the model is trained only on high-resolution New York State LiDAR — no Yazoo Basin or other Delta data — from a programmatically generated corpus of geomorphon-and-hillshade images paired with questions (geomorphon-classification drills, multi-scale feature-identification, and anti-forgetting replay), a balanced mixture of roughly 40,000 examples. Adaptation uses QLoRA (Dettmers et al. 2023; LoRA, Hu et al. 2021) — a 4-bit-quantised, frozen base with small trained low-rank adapters (rank 64) — in a run that fits on a single workstation-class accelerator, which is the point: the model is meant to be retrainable and deployable in the field, not only in a data centre.

**Deployed checkpoint.** From a checkpoint history that trades geomorphon-classification accuracy against zero-shot transfer, we selected V9.1 checkpoint-1250 as the best-balanced point: it scores **85.3%** overall on an internal thirteen-task terrain-reasoning benchmark and passes every category threshold. We flag plainly — and detail in Appendix A — that this benchmark originates in a broader military terrain-analysis project, is scored by keyword recall over only thirteen tasks, and does not test archaeological detection in the Delta; its scores indicate general terrain competence, not detection performance, which the Section 5 ablation is required to establish.

**Zero-shot transfer.** Having been trained only on New York terrain, the model is applied to the Yazoo Basin with no Delta training data — a deliberate **zero-shot transfer** design whose motivation is practical. Labeled training data for prehistoric mounds in the Delta is scarce and expensive to produce, so a method that needs it for every new region does not scale. By learning the morphological primitives of earthworks rather than region-specific templates, the model can be pointed at a new river valley and produce candidates for expert review without a local labeling campaign. We are explicit that zero-shot transfer is the most demanding capability we ask of the model and, on the internal benchmark, among the ones with the most headroom; transfer quality is an active area of work, not a solved problem. We are equally explicit that the Delta validation in Section 3 does **not** test this transfer: those results are produced by the deterministic geomorphon detector, which carries no learned weights, so nothing in Section 3 flows through the fine-tuned model. A genuine Delta test — running the model on the recovered gold-list mounds and on shield survivors and scoring its readings against them — is part of the ablation we defer to future work (Section 5).

## 3. Results and validation

### 3.1 Recall against the reference mound set

We assembled a reference set ("gold list") of 35 mounds in the Yazoo Basin, identified by their **Lower Mississippi Survey grid-quadrat designations** (for example 18-L-7, 21-M-11 — the survey-grid system of Phillips, Ford, and Griffin 1951 and Phillips 1970). These are not Smithsonian trinomials, which for Mississippi begin with 22 (as in the paper's own 22-Yz-557). The coordinates are legacy positions compiled from prior survey; their individual positional accuracy is not uniformly documented and is in places large — Section 3.5 shows one catalogue point lying ~200 m from the nearest relief — which bounds how the agreement statistics below should be read. For each mound, the search procedure downloads a DEM, computes geomorphons, runs the single-scale detector of Section 2.2, and records whether any candidate falls within 30 m of the catalogue coordinate.

We report the evaluation in the project's analytical CRS, UTM Zone 15N, so that every distance is in true ground metres; DEMs were retrieved for all 35 mounds. Because recall against a legacy catalogue depends strongly on the match tolerance — the catalogue coordinates carry their own positional error — we report it as a function of tolerance rather than as a single number (Table 1). At a 30 m tolerance the detector recovers **31 of 35 mounds (89%)**; recall falls to 80% at 25 m, 71% at 20 m, and 51% at 10 m. That decline is as much a measure of catalogue positional uncertainty as of the detector's centring, and it is why a single headline recall number would be misleading. Among the 31 recovered mounds the centroid-to-catalogue offset has a median of **5.1 m** and a mean of 9.4 m (maximum 29.4 m), in true metres. The detector is deterministic, so a repeat run on re-fetched DEMs returns the same result: this establishes pipeline determinism, not independent robustness, and we do not present it as the latter. Four mounds are missed at the 30 m tolerance — 22-M-1 (nearest candidate 74 m), 22-N-13_m5 (51 m), and 22-N-11_m2 and 22-N-11_m3 (both ~43 m); the last two belong to a single site whose two catalogue points fall in a tight cluster where a neighbouring feature dominates the candidate ranking, and 22-M-1 and 22-N-13_m5 are low-profile features. The matching protocol is itself lenient and we state it plainly: for each mound a 300 m tile is centred on the catalogue point, the detector returns up to ten candidates, and a hit is scored if any of them falls within the tolerance — a best-case, centred-window sensitivity test rather than detection within an uncurated regional scan, and one a stricter single-best-candidate rule would make harder.

We stress that these distances are **agreement with a legacy catalogue, not positional accuracy.** Both the detected centroid and the catalogue point carry error, and Section 3.5 shows one catalogue coordinate lying ~200 m from the nearest relief; the offset statistics confirm that detections land on the mapped feature, they do not establish sub-metre accuracy against ground truth.

One point bounds the interpretation of these numbers: the recall measures the **geomorphon detector**, the detection stage of the workflow, against a catalogue of known mounds. It is a measure of sensitivity, not of end-to-end precision — the fraction of *reported* candidates that are real is a separate and harder question, which we take up in Section 3.2.

### 3.2 The role of the shield, and what is not yet measured

The reference-set evaluation deliberately stresses **recall**: every entry is a real mound, so it cannot measure how many false positives the shield removes. To keep recall honest we also run each candidate through the shield during validation and record whether screening would have dropped a true mound; this lets us watch for the failure mode in which the shield is too aggressive and discards genuine features.

What the reference set cannot provide on its own is a **precision** number, the fraction of reported candidates that are real, across a full regional scan. A complete precision evaluation requires a labeled regional sample with both mounds and confirmed modern features, and producing it (including a complete noise map for the scan area) is in progress. We can, however, report the shield's **rejection rate** on a controlled regional scan, which measures how much of the raw candidate stream the context layers remove (Section 3.3).

It is worth being explicit about what precision is and is not measuring, because the two questions are easy to conflate. The validated capability of the system, demonstrated in Section 3.1, is **recall**: pointed at a landscape that contains a known mound, the detector re-finds it, consistently and to within a few meters. Precision asks the harder, complementary question of what fraction of *newly proposed* candidates, in a region not pre-known to contain mounds, are real. The two are different experiments, and a low precision on a candidate stream does not undercut a high recall on known mounds.

As a first step toward measuring precision we built a lightweight review instrument that presents each surviving candidate as a multi-view relief panel — hillshade plus narrow- and wide-scale local relief — and records a per-candidate verdict, so survivors can be screened systematically rather than impressionistically. A preliminary desk review of the eighty survivors from the Jaketown regional scan (Section 3.3; a visual screen, not a field determination) is instructive precisely because that tile is an adversarial case: it spans the modern town and its engineered floodplain, so most of what the detector finds there is modern by construction. On that tile the survivors are dominated by the compact-but-modern furniture of mechanized agriculture and flood control — berms at field corners, drainage furrows, the paired spoil ridges of dredged ditches, a borrow pit, and points along relict natural levees — and few read as convincing mounds. These features survive because they are compact enough to clear the linearity test and sit in Cultivated Crops or Woody Wetlands rather than on Developed land, so neither active shield layer fires. The lesson is not that the method cannot find mounds — Section 3.1 shows it re-finds them reliably — but that the shield's 71% rejection rate measures removed modern fabric, not archaeological purity, and that the most valuable next filter is a **positive-relief and compactness gate on the survivors themselves**, which the current shield does not yet apply. It is worth being blunt about what such a gate does and does not show. Applying a simple relief-and-compactness threshold — the detector's local relief at least 0.5 m together with a compact 100–5,000 m² footprint — to the eighty Jaketown survivors leaves **four** and removes 76. Two tempting readings of that number must both be resisted. It is not a precision measurement: none of the four is ground-truthed, and the 95% reduction mainly reflects how flat and modern the survivor pool on this adversarial tile already was — which the desk screen had shown qualitatively. Nor is it an independent validation when checked against that same desk screen, because the threshold and the human review are two operationalisations of one construct (positive relief plus compactness), so their agreement is close to circular; and the agreement is in any case only partial — of the eight survivors the review rated plausible, the 0.5 m relief threshold keeps three and discards five. What the exercise does establish is narrower and more useful: a two-parameter relief threshold, which the geomorphon-plus-land-cover-plus-linearity shield does *not* contain, performs most of the mound-versus-modern discrimination that matters on this tile, cutting eighty survivors to four. That is really a finding about the shield — it is missing an elementary, cheap filter — and we recommend adding a positive-relief-and-compactness layer as standard, but tuned as an *operating point* rather than a fixed cutoff: a 0.5 m relief floor suits open cropland yet would discard the sub-0.5 m mounds the introduction emphasises, so the threshold must be set to the regime being screened, and its interaction with the detector's own 0.3 m floor characterised. A genuine precision figure still requires the ground-truthed, non-adversarial evaluation described below; this exercise motivates that experiment, it does not substitute for it. These preliminary verdicts are recorded in the review log for analyst confirmation and field follow-up; they sharpen the precision question rather than answer it. We are aware, too, that a single deliberately adversarial tile cannot settle precision either way: poor survivor quality there is consistent both with the method working (the tile is mostly modern) and with the shield needing the positive-relief gate it lacks, and pre-declaring the tile "adversarial" must not be allowed to excuse the pipeline from measurement. Disentangling those explanations requires precision measured on a randomly sited, non-adversarial tile with a complete noise map and the compactness gate enabled — the experiment we identify as the essential next step (Section 5), and one that field verification, out of scope for this study, must ultimately anchor.

### 3.3 Case study: a controlled Jaketown rescan

A regional scan of the Jaketown complex (Ford, Phillips, and Haag 1955) illustrates both the pipeline and the reporting discipline the shield is meant to support. An earlier draft of this paper described a curated result of four confirmed candidates filtered from 42 peaks. That result was not reproducible from the run records, and we correct it here.

The original scan produced 200 raw candidates, and inspection of its per-candidate records exposed the core problem: for almost every candidate the NLCD column read "Lookup Failed" or "Circuit Breaker Tripped." The land-cover layer had contributed nothing. We traced this to the land-cover service itself. The Esri Living Atlas NLCD ImageServer the pipeline queried now requires an authentication token and returns HTTP 499 ("Token Required") for anonymous requests, so every NLCD lookup had been failing silently. We repointed the pipeline at the token-free MRLC public WMS, which returns NLCD class codes directly, and we hardened the shield so that an unavailable layer is recorded as "incomplete context" rather than treated as a pass.

We then reran the scan over the same area with the functional land-cover layer (Figure 7). The detector produced 277 raw candidates. The shield **rejected 197 of them (71%)**: 189 because they fell on developed land and 2 on open water (both via NLCD), and 6 because their footprints were strongly linear (aspect ratio at or above 4.5, the signature of a levee or road). Eighty candidates survived as KEEP or FLAG. The result is consistent with the geography: the Jaketown tile spans the modern town and its road and building fabric, so the majority of geomorphon "peaks" the detector finds are structures and pads, not mounds, and these are exactly what the land-cover layer is meant to remove.

![**Figure 7. The shield on a controlled Jaketown rescan.** With the repaired NLCD layer active, the shield rejected 197 of 277 raw candidates (71%), almost all because they sit on developed land, plus a few strongly linear levee or road footprints. The modern-feature (canal and levee) layer was inactive for this run because no noise map was supplied, so all candidates are recorded as having incomplete context.](figures/fig_jaketown_shield_funnel.png)

Two cautions apply. The 71% figure is a **rejection rate**, not a precision figure: the 80 survivors have not been ground-truthed, so we cannot yet say how many are real mounds. And the modern-feature layer was inactive for this run because no noise map was supplied, which is why every candidate is marked as having incomplete context. A scan with a complete noise map and field follow-up on the survivors is the immediate next step. What this rescan does establish is that the shield now acts on its context rather than merely recording it.

### 3.4 Case study: Lake George / Holly Bluff

Lake George (the Holly Bluff site, 22-Yz-557; Williams and Brain 1983) is one of the largest Mississippian centres in the basin, anchored by a platform mound published at roughly 17 m in height. It is also where the limits of public bare-earth LiDAR are sharpest, and reporting it honestly required correcting our own earlier run.

An earlier draft described a scan that "resolved the principal Lake George monument" as a 3,519 m² feature with 2.94 m of relief. On audit, that scan was centered on the wrong place: its area of interest (approximately −90.725 to −90.700 longitude, 32.812 to 32.832 latitude) covers the modern town of Holly Bluff, about 8 km northeast of the Lake George mound group, whose principal mound sits near 32.785, −90.785. The feature reported as "the principal monument" was therefore not Lake George Mound A but an unrelated rise near the town. We correct this here.

We re-ran the scan over the actual mound site (a 2.3 × 2.2 km area of interest centered on the published Mound A coordinate), with the same geomorphon detector and Shield V2. The result is a clean negative: **the mound is not present in the 3DEP bare-earth surface at all.** Across the entire area of interest the surface relief is about 2 m — the flat backswamp of the Yazoo floodplain — and the tallest feature the detector found anywhere in it rises only **0.42 m**. The candidates nearest the mapped Mound A location, 120 to 165 m away, are all sub-decimeter features of *negative* relief (small depressions, 38–278 m²) within bottomland-hardwood forest classed as Woody Wetlands. There is no 17 m platform, and nothing that resembles one (Figure 8).

The cause is canopy. Lake George Mound A stands under mature bottomland forest, and the seamless USGS 3DEP product over this stand returns a near-flat bare-earth surface: either too few laser pulses reach the ground through the canopy to reconstruct the mound, or the ground-classification that produced the public DTM removed the structure along with the vegetation. Either way, the bare-earth raster the pipeline consumes simply does not contain the mound. This is a property of the input, not a failure of the detector: a geomorphon classifier cannot recover relief that is absent from the DEM it is given.

Two points follow, and they bound the method honestly. First, recovery of canopy-covered mounds is product- and processing-dependent: a more aggressive, purpose-built ground filter applied directly to the raw point cloud has recovered partial relief at this same mound elsewhere in the project (on the order of 6 m, still short of the published 17 m), so the failure reported here is specific to the seamless 3DEP product, not to LiDAR in general. A leaf-off or custom-filtered acquisition is the natural next step for canopy-covered sites. Second, Lake George is the instructive counterpart to an open-terrain positive control: a large mound that survives in open ground (for example Winterville Mound A, 22-Ws-500; Brain 1989 — which stands roughly 12 m high and is plainly resolved in the same 3DEP product, Figure 9) is exactly the kind of feature the pipeline is built for, while a large mound under closed canopy is exactly where a public-bare-earth approach reaches its limit. A prospection system for the Delta has to be read with that boundary in mind: it screens the open, agriculturally exposed majority of the basin well, and is blind to the forested remainder until a canopy-penetrating surface is supplied.

### 3.5 Case study: agricultural-island protection in cropland

Where Lake George shows the method's limit, a third scan shows the shield doing the discriminating work it was designed for. We ran the pipeline over a ~1.5 × 1.5 km tract of mechanized cropland in the central basin that contains a reference mound (21-M-11) — the setting in which the agricultural-island rule matters most. Here a compact prehistoric rise and the linear furrows, turn-rows, raised field roads, and canal spoil banks of modern farming occupy the *same* National Land Cover Database class (Cultivated Crops), so a land-cover filter alone cannot separate them; only morphology can.

The scan produced 163 candidates, of which the shield kept 54, flagged 39, and rejected 70, and the discrimination falls cleanly along shape. Compact features in cropland are preserved as islands: 50 candidates in Cultivated Crops were kept, including an 813 m² rise of 1.65 m relief and aspect 1.47 — the squat, near-isotropic signature of a small platform or conical mound surviving in a plowed field. The linear furniture of the field is removed: candidates with bounding-box aspect ratios from roughly 13 to 60 — plow lines, turn-rows, raised field roads, and the spoil bank flanking the bayou that crosses the tract — were rejected as linear features, while moderately elongated rises (aspect near 3) were flagged as probable plowing artifacts rather than silently kept or dropped (Figure 10). This is exactly the behavior Section 2.4 specifies: within one Cultivated Crops class the shield returns KEEP for a compact mound-shape and REJECT for a levee-shape.

One caution bounds the reading. The cataloged coordinate for 21-M-11 and the nearest geomorphon candidate differ by about 200 m — a discrepancy consistent with the positional uncertainty of legacy site coordinates rather than a clean detection at the mapped point — so we read this scan as a demonstration of the shield's population-level behavior in cropland, not as a point recovery of that specific mound. And as at Jaketown, the keep/flag/reject split measures how the context layers act on a candidate stream, not how many of the kept islands are genuinely prehistoric; that distinction, too, awaits ground truth.

## 4. Discussion

The result we most want to foreground is not the recall number itself but what it is achieved *without*. Every quantified detection in this study is produced with no labelled training example from the Delta: the geomorphon detector is deterministic, and the interpretation layer never saw a Yazoo mound. That a label-free pipeline recovers the great majority of a known-mound catalogue is the proof of concept for the direction this paper argues for. In a discipline whose training signal is finite and cannot be manufactured, decoupling *where a model learns* from *where it is applied* is not merely convenient; where the labelled examples do not exist, it may be the only approach that scales. A supervised detector is bounded by the number of known examples a region affords; a zero-shot, foundation-model approach is bounded instead by the abundance of generic terrain the world affords, which is effectively unlimited. The evidence here does not prove that the fine-tuned interpretation model transfers well — that remains to be measured (Section 5) — but it does establish that the label-free architecture as a whole clears the first bar, recovering real mounds it was never trained to see. For a field that has largely pursued automated detection through region-specific supervised training, that is the finding we think matters most.

The workflow also automates the first pass of large-scale survey. A regional scan that would take an analyst weeks of hillshade inspection runs in hours, and because the detector reasons about morphology rather than a single illumination it surfaces subtle, low-profile features that fixed-angle review tends to miss. Its validated core is the **sensitivity of the detector**: at a 30 m tolerance it recovers about nine in ten catalogued mounds, in true metres, resolving even the individual mounds within the tight 22-N-13 and 22-N-9 clusters (Section 3.1, Figure 6). We are careful not to overstate this: recall falls steeply at tighter tolerances (Table 1), a reminder that the metric is agreement with an imperfect legacy catalogue rather than positional truth, and that the detector actually run is the single-scale approximation of Section 2.2, not the multi-scale form the engine also implements. Sensitivity of this kind is the property a prospection tool must earn before its candidates are worth an analyst's — and eventually a field team's — time; it is necessary but, as Section 3.2 stresses, not sufficient, because it says nothing about precision.

The case studies then show what the shield does with that sensitivity. They are demonstrations of behavior, not yet precision measurements, and they bound the method from three sides: Jaketown shows the shield stripping the modern fabric of a town-spanning tile (and the survivor screen shows where a positive-relief gate is still needed); the cropland scan shows it doing the harder discrimination the method exists for, keeping compact mounds as islands in active fields while rejecting the plow lines and dredge spoil that share that land cover; and Lake George marks the outer limit, where closed canopy removes even a 17 m mound from the public bare-earth surface entirely.

The workflow is not a replacement for the archaeologist. It is a prioritisation engine. Its output is a ranked, screened candidate list; where the interpretation layer is run, each survivor can additionally carry an explicit reading an analyst may accept, downgrade, or reject. This division of labour is the appropriate one for a domain where ground-truthing is the scarce resource and the cost of a false positive is measured in field days. It is also the appropriate posture given that the most demanding component we propose — zero-shot transfer of the interpretation model to a region it never trained on — is the one we trust least and, as Section 2.5 makes plain, have not yet measured in the Delta at all.

The honest accounting in Section 3 is itself a methodological point. An automated prospection system accumulates components faster than it accumulates controlled evaluations of them, and the failure modes are insidious: a pipeline that reports context it never actually checked, a case study run over the wrong coordinates, a benchmark number quoted from a superseded checkpoint. The redesign of the shield to surface incomplete context, the correction of the Jaketown candidate counts, and the correction of a Lake George case study that had been scanned ~8 km off the actual mound site are all examples of the discipline a deployable system needs — and a reminder that for a method whose output is a coordinate, getting the coordinate right is not a detail but the whole point.

## 5. Limitations and future work

- **The interpretation layer's contribution is unmeasured.** The fine-tuned vision-language model is described in full (Section 2.5) but never enters the measurement loop: every reported detection comes from the geomorphon detector and the shield, and the reported scans do not invoke the model. Whether it improves precision, recall, or ranking over the deterministic detector-plus-shield is unknown. The essential experiment is a controlled ablation — detector + shield versus detector + shield + model — on a labelled sample, reporting the change in precision and recall the model produces; until it is run, the model remains a proposed component, not a validated one.
- **The detector evaluated here is a single-scale approximation.** Section 3's results use a single-scale, elevation-referenced geomorphon classifier (Section 2.2), not the multi-scale, angle-thresholded, persistence-scored method the engine also implements and the introduction motivates. Integrating multi-scale persistence into the detection path and re-running the Section 3 evaluation against it is a priority; the single-scale 0.3 m flatness floor may currently suppress the sub-0.5 m mounds we emphasise, so we expect the multi-scale form to change — most plausibly improve — recovery of the faintest features.
- **No simpler baseline is reported.** We do not benchmark the geomorphon detector against a lighter alternative (a local-relief-model blob detector, or an object-based rule set) on the same reference set, so we cannot yet say how much of the recall is attributable to the geomorphon representation specifically. A baseline comparison is a straightforward and worthwhile addition.
- **Reproducibility bookkeeping is incomplete.** Different reported numbers were produced by different scripts and, currently, two coordinate systems (Section 2.1); a single table mapping each number to its script, detector variant, parameters, CRS, and data artefact should accompany submission. One detector parameter in particular — an internal aspect-ratio down-weight (aspect > 4.0), distinct from the shield's 4.5 / 3.0 linearity thresholds — is undocumented and should be stated so the two linearity tests are not conflated.
- **Shield precision is unmeasured by field truth, and a desk screen suggests it is low on modern-dominated tiles.** We report detector recall and the shield's rejection rate (71% at Jaketown), but not field-verified precision. A preliminary visual review of the Jaketown survivors (Section 3.2) found none that present as convincing mounds — the survivors are compact modern and natural features (field-edge berms, drainage and dredge-spoil ridges, borrow pits, natural levees) that clear the linearity and land-cover layers. This is a desk screen, not ground truth, but it exposes a concrete gap: the shield lacks an elementary **positive-relief-and-compactness filter** that, on the Jaketown tile, does most of the mound-versus-modern discrimination the geomorphon, land-cover, and linearity layers do not — a relief-and-compactness threshold cuts the 80 survivors to 4 (Section 3.2). We recommend adding such a layer as standard, tuned as an operating point rather than a fixed cutoff (a 0.5 m floor suits open cropland but would discard the sub-0.5 m mounds we target). Converting any of this into a precision figure requires the labelled, non-adversarial, ground-truthed evaluation that remains required and is in preparation.
- **NLCD dependence on an external service.** The Esri NLCD ImageServer used by earlier runs began requiring an authentication token and was failing silently; we repointed the layer at the token-free MRLC public WMS, which restored it. The layer still depends on a remote service, so a cached or local NLCD raster would make it fully dependable offline.
- **Noise map coverage.** The modern-feature layer is only as good as the quadrangle coverage and the optical character recognition behind it, and it must be generated per area of interest. Its recall against actual canals and levees has not been quantified.
- **Coordinate error is catalogue agreement, not accuracy.** The offsets (median 5.1 m, mean 9.4 m in UTM 15N; Table 1) measure agreement between detected centroids and a legacy catalogue whose own positions carry uncertainty — in one case ~200 m (Section 3.5). They are not an independent accuracy benchmark, and the strong dependence of recall on match tolerance (Table 1) follows directly from that catalogue uncertainty. Characterising the catalogue's positional error against an independently surveyed subset would let recall and accuracy be reported against a known target.
- **Zero-shot transfer.** Transfer from New York terrain to the Delta is the design's central bet. On the internal benchmark the deployed V9.1 (checkpoint-1250) model scores 76.2% on the zero-shot category — the capability we trust least, and one measured on a proxy benchmark rather than on Delta ground truth. Independent Delta ground truth beyond the reference set is needed to validate it.
- **Canopy is a hard limit of the public bare-earth product.** The corrected Lake George rescan (Section 3.4) shows that the seamless 3DEP DTM does not contain the 17 m Mound A under closed bottomland-hardwood canopy; the method is blind to forested mounds until a leaf-off or custom canopy-penetrating surface is supplied. Recovery is product-dependent, and quantifying it across the basin's forested fraction is future work.
- **Case studies remain partly preliminary.** The Jaketown rejection rate, the corrected Lake George negative, and the cropland agricultural-island scan are reported here as demonstrations of shield *behavior*; none is yet a precision measurement. Ground-truthing of the Jaketown and cropland survivors, and a canopy-penetrating reacquisition at Lake George, are the next steps.

## 6. Conclusion

We have described and evaluated a reproducible workflow for screening bare-earth LiDAR for earthen mounds in a heavily modified alluvial landscape, and we have tried to report it as honestly as we ran it. Its measured strengths are two: a geomorphon detector that recovers about nine in ten catalogued mounds at a 30 m tolerance in true metres, and an auditable context shield that separates compact prehistoric shapes from the linear and developed fabric of the modern Delta, demonstrated from the agricultural islands it preserves to the levees it rejects. Its proposed but unmeasured component is a fine-tuned vision-language interpretation layer, whose end-to-end contribution we deliberately leave as an open question rather than assert. And its boundaries are stated plainly: precision on novel candidates is not measured here and no candidate is field-verified; the detector evaluated is a single-scale approximation of the multi-scale method the workflow envisions; and closed canopy can remove even a large mound from the public bare-earth product entirely. Formalising archaeological screening into a reproducible pipeline is both feasible and, given the pace of landscape modification in the Delta, increasingly necessary. The work that would turn this validated detector and principled shield into a measured end-to-end instrument — an ablation of the interpretation layer, a precision evaluation on a representative labelled tile, integration of the multi-scale detector, and ultimately field verification — is the agenda we have tried to make explicit.

## Appendix A. Interpretation layer — training corpus, fine-tuning, and benchmark detail

This appendix gives the full build detail summarised in Section 2.5.3. None of it measures the interpretation layer's contribution to mound detection (Section 5); it is provided for reproducibility.

**Training data.** The fine-tuning corpus is built from high-resolution New York State LiDAR (the public NYSGPO collections), chosen because New York offers varied, noisy terrain — glacial drumlins and eskers, historic cellar holes, quarry pits, and military earthworks — that forces the model to learn the geometric difference between natural and cultural relief rather than memorizing a single landscape. Crucially, no Yazoo Basin or other Delta data is used in training. The corpus is assembled programmatically: a DEM tile is reduced to geomorphons and hillshades, a deterministic routine reads off the ground-truth landform, and the pair is written as an image-plus-question training example. Three kinds of example dominate. *Geomorphon drills* (tens of thousands of items) present a hillshade-and-geomorphon composite and ask the model to name the dominant landform, balanced across all ten geomorphon classes; these were added specifically to lift geomorphon-classification accuracy, the competence on which everything downstream depends. *Multi-scale feature-identification* examples ask the model to map a morphological primitive at a given scale to a feature interpretation (for example, a linear Valley to a drainage channel, a flat-topped Peak to a platform). *Anti-forgetting replay* examples preserve the base model's general visual and reasoning ability so that domain adaptation does not erode it. The production checkpoint is fine-tuned on a balanced mixture of roughly 40,000 such examples; an earlier, dense model generation was trained on a far larger corpus (on the order of a million examples), and a central finding of our checkpoint history (below) is that the smaller balanced mixture, targeted at the weakest competence, was the more effective recipe.

**Fine-tuning method.** Adaptation uses QLoRA (Dettmers et al. 2023), which combines 4-bit quantization with low-rank adaptation (LoRA; Hu et al. 2021): the base model is loaded in 4-bit precision (NF4 with double quantization, bf16 compute) and frozen, and small low-rank adapter matrices are trained on top of it. We use a LoRA rank of 64 and an alpha of 128 with 0.05 dropout, and we freeze the vision tower so that only the language-and-reasoning pathway is adapted. Training runs for a single epoch at a learning rate of 1×10⁻⁵ (cosine schedule, brief warmup) with an effective batch size of 16 (a per-device batch of one with sixteen-step gradient accumulation) and a 1,024-token context. The entire run fits on a single workstation-class accelerator with unified memory, which is the point: the system is meant to be retrainable and deployable in the field, not only in a data center. QLoRA makes this feasible by training a few hundred million adapter parameters rather than all thirty billion of the base.

**Checkpoint history and what it measures.** We track progress on an internal benchmark of thirteen terrain-reasoning tasks grouped into five categories — geomorphon classification, tactical/landscape assessment, recovery-and-detection search, zero-shot transfer to unseen terrain, and domain terminology — scored by expected-keyword recall and averaged by category. The trajectory shows that building this model was an exercise in trading capabilities against one another rather than climbing a single number. A first aligned checkpoint (V1) scored about 42% overall. A dense 32-billion-parameter "Thinking" model (V8), trained on the full ~1-million-example corpus, reached 84.1% overall, with geomorphon classification near 71%. Moving to the mixture-of-experts base and retraining on the 40,000-example balanced mixture produced an intermediate checkpoint that pushed geomorphon classification up to roughly 85% but cut zero-shot transfer to about 74% — the wrong trade for a system whose entire premise is transfer to an unseen basin. We therefore rebalanced the mixture, adding zero-shot and recovery examples, and trained the V9.1 run. From its checkpoints we selected **`checkpoint-1250`** as the deployed model, because it is the best-balanced point on the run: it scores **85.3%** overall and is the checkpoint that passes every category threshold at once — geomorphon classification **72.7%** (the only V9.1 checkpoint above the 70% bar that also keeps transfer high), zero-shot transfer **76.2%**, recovery search **96.4%**, tactical assessment **94.4%**, and domain terminology **90%**. On a mission-weighted metric that prioritizes the capabilities this application depends on — zero-shot transfer (0.30), recovery (0.25), tactical (0.20), geomorphon (0.15), terminology (0.10) — it scores **85.8%**. We chose it over later checkpoints that edged slightly higher on the transfer-weighted metric but let geomorphon classification fall below the 70% threshold: for a pipeline whose first stage is a deterministic geomorphon detector, a model that has lost its grip on geomorphon vocabulary is the wrong trade.

Three cautions about this benchmark are owed to the reader. It originates in a broader **military terrain-analysis** project, and its five categories reflect that origin; several test general or tactical terrain competence rather than archaeological mound detection, and the terminology category is a single task quoted at 90%. The thirteen tasks are scored by expected-keyword recall — a coarse proxy that credits the presence of the right vocabulary without verifying the reasoning behind it, and that can penalise a correct synonym; with only thirteen tasks it cannot support fine distinctions between checkpoints, and small differences should not be over-read. And the scores measure the model against this internal rubric, not against Delta earthworks: they are indicative of general terrain-reasoning competence, not evidence of archaeological detection performance, which the benchmark does not test and which the ablation in Section 5 is required to establish. The per-checkpoint scoring artefacts are released with the project so the numbers can be audited against the code.
